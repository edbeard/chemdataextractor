UV–vis absorption and fluorescence spectra, solvatochromism, and application to pH sensors of novel xanthene dyes having thienyl and thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl rings as auxochrome

Abstract

Novel xanthene dyes having thienyl and thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl rings as auxochrome have been synthesized. The UV–vis absorption maximum in dichloromethane was in the order of the auxochrome: NEt 2 (Rhodamine B, 558 nm) < 2-thienyl (569 nm) < 5-thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl (608 nm). These new xanthene dyes colored only in non-hydrogen-bond accepting solvents such as chloroform and dichloromethane. The lactone precursors of the thienyl and thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl derivatives colored by adding strong acids whose p K a value is lower than −2.2 and 0.23, respectively, whereas that of rhodamine B colored by adding weak acids whose p K a value is lower than 4.7. These results originate from less stability of colored cationic dyes by the heteroaromatics than by the diethylamino auxochrome.

1. Introduction

Rhodamines and Fluorescein are well-known xanthene dyes which have unique spiroring-opening structures controlled by acids or bases. Rhodamines having (di)alkylamino auxochrome color and emit by adding acids and Fluorescein having hydroxy groups by bases. Xanthene dyes can also cover the absorption and emission wavelength in the range of visible to near-infrared region. Therefore, they have been used as bioimaging probes [1] , chemosensors [2] ; [3] , and sensitizers for singlet oxygen formation [4] ; [5] ; [6] and dye-sensitized solar cells [7] . To obtain a variety of absorption band and hue, π-excess heteroaromatics are used in dye chromophore. However, they are preferentially involved in the chromophoric system [8] ; [9] ; [10] ; [11] ; [12] ; [13] ; [14] . There are less organic dyes in which π-excess heteroaromatic rings are used as auxochrome. We consider that not only electron-donating amino, alkylamino, dialkylamino, and hydroxy groups but also π-excess heteroaromatic auxochrome can change the properties of dyes. We report here the properties of xanthene dyes having π-excess heteroaromatic rings as auxochrome.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Instruments

Melting points were measured with a Yanaco MP-S2 micro-melting-point apparatus. NMR spectra were taken with a JEOL JNM-ECX 400P spectrometer. Mass spectra were taken on a JEOL MStation 700 spectrometer. IR spectra were taken on a Shimadzu Affinity-1 spectrophotometer. Elemental analysis was performed with a Yanaco MT-6 CHN corder. UV–vis absorption and fluorescence spectra were taken on Hitachi U-4100 and JASCO FP-8600 spectrophotometers, respectively. Fluorescence quantum yields and fluorescence lifetime were measured by Hamamatsu Photonics Quantaurus-QY and Quantaurus-Tau instruments, respectively.

2.2. Materials

Compound 1 was prepared as described in the paper [15] . Compounds 2 and 3 were purchased from Tokyo Chemical Industry Co., Ltd. RB-lactone and bis(perfluorobutylsulfonyl)amide were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich Co. and Wako Chemical Ltd., respectively. All the solvents used in the solvent effects were dried using molecular sieve 4A before use.

To THF (10 mL) were added 1 (0.45 g, 1.0 mmol) and tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)parlladium(0) (0.14 g, 0.15 mmol). The mixture was stirred at room temperature for 10 min. After the reaction was completed, to the mixture was added brine (100 mL). 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

Novel xanthene dyes, 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − and 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , were synthesized as shown in Scheme 1 . Compound 1 , obtained by the reaction of 3-bromophenol with phthalic anhydride, was allowed to react with boronic acids 2 and 3 to afford 4-lactone and 5-lactone , which were treated with bis(perfluorobutylsulfonyl)imide to produce 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − and 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , respectively. Since Rhodamine B ( RB ) was used as a reference compound, RB-lactone and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − were also prepared.

3.2. UV–vis absorption and fluorescence spectra

The UV–vis absorption and fluorescence spectra of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − in dichloromethane are shown in Fig. 1 . The data are listed in Table 1 . The fluorescence decay curve for 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − are shown in Fig. S1 . As the absorbance of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − and 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − at the first absorption maximum ( λ max ) significantly reduced in the presence of a small amount of water, the spectra were measured in the presence of 1 M amount of (C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 NH in dry dichloromethane. The λ max of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − were observed at 569, 608, and 558 nm, respectively. Thus, 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − was most bathochromic followed by 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − . The molar absorption coefficients ( ε ) of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − were observed to be 740,000, 50,000, and 116,000 dm 3 mol −1 cm −1 respectively. The fluorescence maxima ( F max ) of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − were observed at 619, 732, and 583 nm, respectively. 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − exhibited intense fluorescence, being the fluorescence quantum yields ( Φ f ) 0.80 and 0.84, respectively. 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − was a weakly fluorescent compound. The radiative rate constants ( k f ) of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − were observed in the range of 0.09–0.22 × 10 9 s −1 , respectively, being no remarkable differences among them. However, the non-radiative rate constants ( k nr ) of 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − (0.98 × 10 9 s −1 ) was significantly large compared with those of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − (0.06 × 10 9 s −1 ) and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − (0.03 × 10 9 s −1 ). Thus, the weak fluorescence of 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − is attributed to its fast non-radiative processes in the excited state.

3.3. DFT calculations

The optimized structures of 4 + , 5 + , and RB + were obtained by the B3LYP/3-21G level. The results are shown in Fig. 2 . They show that the 2-carboxyphenyl ring is perpendicular for the xanthene ring. The dihedral angles between the xanthene ring and thienyl, thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl, and diethylamino auxochrome were calculated to be 0.4, 0.2, and 1.9°, respectively. Thus, the diethylamino-nitrogen is a sp 2 form and the heteroaromatic rings are located on the same plane to the xanthene ring. Therefore, not only the unshared electron pair on the diethylamino-nitrogen atoms but also the π-electrons on the heteroaromatic rings conjugate with the xanthene ring.

The single-point energy calculations performed by the B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) level are shown in Fig. 3 . Dyes 4 + , 5 + , and RB + consist of alternative conjugated chromophoric system. The conjugation of 5 + is longest followed by 4 + and RB + . As the diethylamino group is much more electron-donating than the thienyl and thienothenyl rings, both the HOMO and LUMO levels of RB + are higher than those of 4 + and 5 + . Those of 5 + are higher than those of 4 + . Consequently, the energy gap between the HOMO and LUMO levels was in the order: 5 + (2.10 eV) < 4 + (2.47 eV) < RB + (2.85 eV).

3.4. Effects of solvents on the UV–vis absorption spectra

The effects of solvents on the UV–vis absorption spectra of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , and RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − are indicated in Fig. 4 . Fig. 4 a shows the UV–vis absorption spectra in dichloromethane and ethanol. They colored in dichloromethane. However, interestingly, 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − and 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − were completely colorless in ethanol, whereas RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − colored with slight negative solvatochromism in ethanol. Fig. 4 b indicates that the addition of ethanol to the dichloromethane solution of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − reduces the first absorption band and the absorption at around 300 nm increases with an isosbestic point at 330 nm, indicating that the decoloration process quantitatively proceeds. Fig. 4 c shows the UV–vis absorption spectra of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − in various solvents. 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − colored only in dichloromethane and chloroform.

To understand this solvatochromic behavior, the role of solvents was examined. Fig. 5 shows the relationship between the absorbance of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − at λ max and three solvatochromic parameters: (a) polarity parameter ( E T (30)) [16] , (b) hydrogen-bond- donating parameter ( α ) [17] ; [18] , and (c) hydrogen-bond-accepting parameter ( β ) [17] ; [19] . It is clear that no correlation is observed for E T (30) and α as shown in Fig. 5 a and b. Fig. 5 c indicates that 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − colored only in non-hydrogen-bond accepting solvents, dichloromethane and chloroform, whose β value is 0.0. In other words, 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − becomes colorless in hydrogen-bond accepting solvents which have unshared electron pairs on an oxygen or a nitrogen atom in the molecule such as alcohols, ethers, acetone, ethyl acetate, DMSO, and acetonitrile. Surprisingly, 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − is colorless even in weak hydrogen-bond-accepting toluene, which can form OH-π interactions.

A plausible solvatochromic behavior of xanthene dyes is shown in Scheme 2 . Colored cations exist as tautomeric resonance forms A , B , and C in solution. The acidic hydrogen atom at the carboxyl group can easily react with surrounded large excess amounts of hydrogen-bond-accepting solvents to form a carboxylate D . In the case of RB + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , as the unshared electrons on the diethylamino-nitrogen atoms can readily stabilize the positive charge of D , no colorless lactone form is produced. In the case of 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − , 4-lactone was isolated as the decoloration product in ethanol. In this case, the positive charge of D can be stabilized by π-excess thienyl rings in some extent. However, as the thienyl rings become unstable non-aromatic cations to completely stabilize D , D is easily rearranged to form colorless lactone E .

3.5. Application as pH sensors

Application of novel lactone precursors, 4-lactone and 5-lactone , to pH sensor was examined. The results are depicted in Fig. 7 . RB-lactone was sensitive for weak acids whose p K a value is lower than 4.7 to show coloration and emission. Interestingly, 4-lactone colored and emitted with acids whose p K a value is lower than −2.2. 5-lactone colored with acids whose p K a value is lower than 0.23. No emission was observed for 5-lactone by naked eyes. The colored 4 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − and 5 + -(C 4 F 9 SO 2 ) 2 N − with conc. HCl were pale pink and pale blue, respectively, owing to the presence of water, in which the colored cationic form is in equilibrium with the colorless lactone form. The carbonyl-oxygen at the lactone moiety reacts with proton to produce the colored cationic form. The lone-electron pairs on the diethylamino-nitrogens in RB-lactone can most readily stabilize the cations A , B , and C in Scheme 2 followed by electron-rich thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl and thienyl ring. Thus, new xanthene dyes exhibited unique performance for Brønsted acids.

4. Conclusion

Novel xanthene dyes having thienyl and thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl rings as auxochrome have been synthesized. The λ max was in the order of auxochrom: NEt 2 (Rhodamine B, 558 nm) < 2-thienyl (569 nm) < 5-thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl (608 nm) in dichloromethane. These new dyes easily discolored in hydrogen-bond-accepting solvents to produce the lactone forms, whereas Rhodamine B colored in the solvents. The lactone forms of thienyl, thieno[3,2- b ]thienyl, and Rhodamine B colored with an acid whose p K a value is lower than −2.1, 0.23, and 4.7, respectively. The difference between the heteroaromatic rings and diethylamino auxochrome is stabilization of colored cations. The stabilization ability of the cations by the heteroaromatic rings is weak compared with that by the diethylamino auxochrome. These lactone derivatives exhibited the potential applications as pH sensors.
