A new family of dinuclear Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes containing dibenzo-14-crown-4

  • Feixiang Cheng,
  • Ning Tang ,
  • College of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, State Key Laboratory of Applied Organic Chemistry, Lanzhou University, Tianshui Road, Lanzhou 730000, China
Received 15 April 2008, Accepted 5 May 2008, Available online 8 May 2008

Abstract

Three bis(bidentate) ligands cis -di(4,5-diazafluoren-9-ylimino)dibenzo-14-crown-4 (BL 1 ), trans -di(4,5-diazafluoren-9-ylimino)dibenzo-14-crown-4 (BL 2 ), and 1,4,8,11-tetraoxacyclotetradecyl[2,3:9,10]bis-dipyrido[3,2- a :2′3′- c ]phenazine (BL 3 ), and corresponding bimetallic Ru(II) complexes [(bpy) 2 RuBL 1−3 Ru(bpy) 2 ](PF 6 ) 4 (Ru–BL 1−3 ) have been synthesized. Cyclic voltammetry of these complexes are consistent with one Ru(II)-centered reversible oxidation and three ligand-centered reductions. These complexes show metal-to-ligand charge transfer absorption at 445–454 nm and emission at 575–592 nm.

Graphical abstract

Three novel bis(bidentate) ligands derived from dibenzo-14-crown-4, and corresponding bimetallic Ru(II) complexes have been synthesized. These complexes exhibit one Ru(II)-centered reversible oxidation and three ligand-centered reductions, show characteristic MLCT absorption band at 445–454 nm and emission at 575–592 nm.

Ligands with an ion recognition unit attached to a metal polypyridyl core are potentially useful in the preparation of chemosensors, in which the properties of metal polypyridyl complexes are modified by molecular level interactions between the recognition center and the substrate [1] . Chemosensors have drawn increasing attention for their use in fields as diverse as biology, medical analysis, environmental monitoring and so on [2] . Among the kinds of metal polypyridyl complexes, Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes have proved to be particularly versatile in sensor applications, because their emitting state energies and excited state redox properties are quite sensitive to variations of external stimuli [3] . Lithium plays an important therapeutic role in the treatment of manic depressive behaviour. Manic psychosis is commonly treated by administering daily dose of lithium carbonate. However, an excessive dose of lithium can cause adverse physiological effects such as nausea and vomiting. Clearly, the monitoring of lithium levels in blood is desirable, and a variety of lithium sensitive electrodes have been developed. Although many crown ether derived from 14-crown-4, benzo-14-crown-4 and dibenzo-14-crown-4 as Li + ion selective carriers [4] , and a lot of Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes for Na + , K + , alkali-earth metal ions and transition metal ions probing have been reported [5] , only few mononuclear Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes [6] and none oligonuclear Ru(II) polypyridyl complex for lithium probing can be found. Herein, we report first the synthesis and characterization of three ligands derived from dibenzo-14-crown-4, their corresponding Ru(II) complexes, the spectroscopic and electrochemical properties of these complexes.

The outline of the synthesis of the three ligands and corresponding complexes is presented in Scheme 1 . BL 1 and BL 2 were prepared by the reaction of 4,5-diazafluoren-9-one [7] with cis -diaminodibenzo-14-crown-4 and trans -diaminodibenzo-14-crown-4 [8] in ethanol respectively. BL 3 formed by the condensation of 1,10-phenanthroline-5,6-dione [9] with tetraaminodibenzo-14-crown-4 [10] in methanol under nitrogen atmosphere 1 . Reaction of Ru(bpy) 2 Cl 2 · 2H 2 O [11] with ligands BL 1−3 , respectively under nitrogen atmosphere afforded three bimetallic complexes [(bpy) 2 RuBL 1−3 Ru(bpy) 2 ](PF 6 ) 4 2 . The resulting complexes were characterized by 1 H NMR, ESI-HRMS, and elemental analysis.

Absorption spectra of the three complexes are shown in Fig. 1 , and the data are summarized in Table 1 . Absorption spectra of complexes Ru–BL 1 and Ru–BL 2 comprise three distinct regions, bands at 285 and 237 nm are attributed to the intraligand π → π ∗ transitions centered on the bipyridine. The set at 445 nm can be assigned as MLCT, dπ → π ∗ transitions, which is blue-shifted by 6 nm compared with that of Ru ( bpy ) 3 2 + [12] .

As observed for [(bpy) 2 Ru(dppz)](PF 6 ) 2 [13] and [Ru(bpy) 2 tpphzRu(bpy) 2 ](PF 6 ) 4 [14] , Ru–BL 3 displays a strong MLCT band at 454 nm attributed to the overlapping of dπ(Ru) → π ∗ (bpy) and dπ(Ru) → π ∗ (BL 3 ). At higher energy region around 397 nm, the spectra display one characteristic π(BL 3 ) → π ∗ (BL 3 ) band of BL 3 . The strong bands below 300 nm can be attributed to π(bpy) → π ∗ (bpy).

Emission band maxima and emission quantum yields of the Ru(II) complexes are summarized in Table 2 . Upon excitation into the MLCT band of the complexes, Ru–BL 1−2 show no emission at all in CH 3 CN at room temperature, but they display vibrational components similar to that of Ru ( bpy ) 3 2 + at 77 K in 4:1 EtOH:MeOH glassy matrix [15] . Cheery et al. [16] have reported crisply that the ligand field of 4,5-diazafluorene was lower than that of bipyridine, and that mixed Ru(II) complexes were poor emitters at room temperature. Complexes Ru–BL 1−2 display their characteristic emission at 575 nm ( Fig. 2 ). Contrary to complexes Ru–BL 1−2 , Ru–BL 3 shows an intense emission both in CH 3 CN solution at room temperature at 592 nm, and in 4:1 ethanol:methanol glassy matrix at 77 K at 580 nm ( Fig. 3 ).

Cyclic voltammetry of the three complexes are measured in CH 3 CN, and the data are summarized in Table 3 . Cyclic voltammetry of complex Ru–BL 1 exhibits one Ru(II)-centered oxidation at 1.32 V. This potential is slightly more positive (by about 40 mV) than that of Ru ( bpy ) 3 2 + . But compared with parent complex [(bpy) 2 Ru(dafo)](PF 6 ) 2 [17] , this potential is slightly more negative (by about 70 mV), which indicates that ligand BL 1 is a weaker π ∗ acceptor than dafo [18] . In the current study, bimetallic complex Ru–BL 1 shows a single and unperturbed wave in cyclic voltammetry, and a single peak without broadening in differential pulse voltammetry. On the basis of the above results, the oxidation can be ascribed to a two-electron reversible process.

The reduction at around −0.86 V is a two-electron reversible process associated with the BL 1 . This is consistent with the addition of electrons to the low energy LUMO mainly localized on BL 1 to give complex [(bpy) 2 Ru II BL 2− Ru II (bpy) 2 ] 2+ . The second reduction at −1.45 V is located on one of the two bpy ligands on each metallic end, adding two electrons to the bpy type LUMO + 1 giving the species [(bpy)(bpy ·− )Ru II BL 2− Ru II (bpy ·− )(bpy)]. Just as the oxidation, the two one-electron reductions of the remote bpy appear at the same potential, indicating the two sites are not interacting. The third reduction appearing at −1.75 V is irreversible and yields the species [(bpy − )(bpy − )Ru II BL 2− Ru II (bpy − )(bpy − )] 2− . Electrochemistry behavior of Ru–BL 2 is similar to Ru–BL 1 .

Redox properties of complex Ru–BL 3 are similar to that of (bpy) 2 Ru(dppz) 2+ . The oxidation potential is very close to that of Ru ( bpy ) 3 2 + , appearing little influence of the strong π-accepting character of BL 3 . This is consistent with the fact that the HOMO has little contribution from the phenazine part. In contrast with the oxidation property, the first two-electron reduction process shows that Ru–BL 3 is better electron acceptor than Ru(bpy) 3 2+ by about 0.2 V. The remaining two waves located at −1.39 and −1.75 V consist of one two-electron process for each wave related to reduction of the bipyridine ligands on each ruthenium center. The reductions of the bipyridine ligands occur sequential at each metal center prior to reduction of the second bipyridine ligand. The reduction processes are presented in Scheme 2 .

In conclusion, three dinuclear Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes containing dibenzo-14-crown-4 have been synthesized. Electrochemical and spectroscopic properties of these complexes have been measured. Taking advantage of the character that the cavity of the 14-membered crown ether ring matches most closely the dimension of lithium ion. So, the three complexes are potential chemical sensor for Li + .
