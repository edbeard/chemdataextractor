A novel, environmentally friendly dispersive liquid–liquid microextraction procedure for the determination of copper

Abstract

A novel, simple and environmentally friendly procedure for copper determination has been developed. The method is based on the formation of an ion associate of Cu(I) with 1,3,3-trimethyl-2-[5-(1,3,3-trimethyl-1,3-dihydroindol-2-ylidene)-penta-1,3-dienyl]-3 H -indolium (DIDC) in the presence of chloride ions as ligand, followed by dispersive liquid–liquid microextraction (DLLME) of the formed ion associate into organic phase and UV–Vis spectrophotometric detection. The following experimental conditions were used: pH 3, 0.24 mol L − 1 chloride ions, 0.06 mmol L − 1 DIDC. The effect of the nature of the extraction solvent, auxiliary solvent and disperser solvent used was studied. A mixture of amyl acetate, tetrachloromethane, and methanol in a 1:1:3 v/v/v ratio was selected for the DLLME procedure. The absorbance of the coloured extracts at 640 nm wavelength obeys Beer's law in the range 0.020–0.090 mg L − 1 of Cu. The limit of detection calculated from a blank test ( n = 10) based on 3 s is 0.005 mg L − 1 of Cu. The developed procedure was applied to the analysis of water samples. The suggested DLLME is compared with two procedures previously reported from our laboratory based on (1) conventional liquid–liquid extraction, and (2) sequential injection extraction performed in a dual-valve sequential injection system. The advantages and disadvantages of each method are discussed.

1. Introduction

Copper has been well-known since ancient times, and at present copper and its compounds are widely used in a variety of industries; it occurs widely in nature and is found naturally in ores. Copper chemistry was discussed in Ref. [1] . It is an important bioelement that plays an important role in biological systems [2] ; [3] . According to Council Directive 98/83/EC on the quality of water intended for human consumption [4] and the Guidelines for Drinking-water quality of the World Health Organisation [5] , the limit for copper content in drinking water is 2.0 mg L − 1 .

A variety of techniques requiring expensive instrumentation have been applied for the determination of copper [6] . In contrast, spectrophotometry is distinguished by its low cost and the simplicity of its apparatus. Vidotti et al. [7] quipped that “the old spectrophotometry is not old fashioned”. The advantages of spectrophotometry and trends in organic complex-forming reagents have been previously discussed [8] . Spectrophotometric methods for copper determination probably began with the work of Mehlig, who in 1935 suggested a colourimetric determination of copper with ammonia [9] ; [10] . Later, the reaction of copper with a variety of organic complex-forming reagents such as rubeanic acid [11] , diethyldithiocarbamate [12] , neo-cuproine [13] , biscyclohexanoneoxalyldihydrazone [14] , salicylaldoxime [15] , 1,5-diphenylcarbohydrazide [16] , tiron [17] and piperidinium piperidyl dithioformate [18] was reported for spectrophotometric and extractive spectrophotometric determination of copper. The more recently published methods are summarised in Table 1 . Among the primary disadvantages of existing methods are, for example, the large amount of organic solvents used [20] ; [21] ; [22] ; [23] , the need for multiple repeated extraction [20] ; [21] ; [22] , the use of hazardous solvents [20] ; [22] ; [23] ; [24] , the low value of molar absorptivity [25] and the complicated procedure [26] . Accurate determination of copper in different kinds of samples has been a difficult task until now.

In addition, complex-forming and precipitation reactions of copper have been used for indirect determination of amino acids [27] ; [28] , primary amines [29] , some drugs of aliphatic amine nature [30] , quinine and its related drugs [31] , soil fumigant [32] , haemoglobin [33] , insulin [34] , sulphide [35] and sodium ceftriaxone [36] .

The aim of our work was to develop a novel, environmentally friendly procedure for the determination of copper. The method is based on the formation of an ion associate of Cu(I) with 1,3,3-trimethyl-2-[5-(1,3,3-trimethyl-1,3-dihydroindol-2-ylidene)-penta-1,3-dienyl]-3 H -indolium (DIDC) in the presence of chloride ions as ligand, followed by dispersive liquid–liquid microextraction (DLLME) of the formed ion associate into organic phase and UV–Vis detection.

2. Experimental

2.1. Reagents

All chemicals used were of analytical reagent grade and solvents used for the extraction were of UV spectroscopy grade. Ultra pure water from Millipore Milli-Q RG (Millipore, USA) was used throughout the work. A stock solution containing 1000 mg L − 1 of Cu(II) was prepared by dissolving CuSO 4 × 5H 2 O in water and standardising by EDTA titration [37] . Working solution containing 1 mg L − 1 copper was prepared by appropriate step-by-step dilution of the stock solution. A 2% solution of ascorbic acid was used as the reducing reagent for preparing the copper(I) solution. A 3 mol L − 1 aqueous solution of NaCl was used to set the appropriate concentration of ligand. A 1 mmol L − 1 aqueous solution of 1,3,3-trimethyl-2-[5-(1,3,3-trimethyl-1,3-dihydroindol-2-ylidene)-penta-1,3-dienyl]-3 H -indolium (DIDC) was prepared by dissolving a precise amount of its chloride salt in 1 mL of methanol and subsequent dilution with water up to a volume of 100 mL. The required pH of the aqueous phase was adjusted using 0.01 mol L − 1 of HCl.

2.2. Apparatus

A Specord S 300 diode-array spectrophotometer (Analytik Jena AG, Germany) and a Spekol-11 spectrophotometer (Carl Zeiss, Germany) equipped with a matched cell of 1 mm path length were used for UV–Vis spectrophotometric measurements. The pH values of the solutions were measured using an ORION 720A + pH meter with a glass electrode. Centrifugation was performed using an MPW-310 centrifuge (Mechanika Precyzyjna, Poland).

The LDI-MS measurements were performed with a Bruker BIFLEX III mass spectrometer equipped with a time-of-flight (TOF) analyser. In all cases an acceleration voltage of 19 kV was used, and the ions were detected in the reflectron mode (20 kV). A nitrogen laser (337 nm, 3 ns pulse width, 10 6 –10 7 W/cm 2 ) operating at 4 Hz was used to produce laser desorption.

2.3. Calibration of the method

Samples of a 5 mL aqueous solution containing from 0.020 to 0.090 mg L − 1 of copper, with the pH adjusted to 3, 0.5 mL 2% ascorbic acid as reducing reagent, and the required concentrations of chloride ions (0.24 mol L − 1 ) and DIDC (0.06 mmol L − 1 ), were placed into 10 mL conical centrifugal tubes and mixed thoroughly. A 0.5 mL of a mixture of solvents containing the extraction solvent (amyl acetate), the auxiliary solvent (tetrachloromethane) and the disperser solvent (methanol) in a 1:1:3 v/v/v ratio was rapidly injected into the solution using a glass microsyringe to produce a cloudy state. The mixture was gently shaken 3 times and then centrifuged for 2 min at 3000 rpm. Finally, about 150 μL of the organic sediment was withdrawn using a microsyringe and transferred into a matched quartz cell with 1 mm path length, and the absorbance of ion associate formed was measured at 640 nm.

2.4. Procedure for real water samples

An aliquot of sample of up to 3.3 mL was introduced into a conical centrifugal tube, and the determination performed as described in the Calibration of the method section. There was no other pre-treatment performed before the DLLME procedure, except the addition of the necessary reagents.

3. Results and discussion

Copper(I) in the presence of chloride ions as ligand is able to form with DIDC an ion associate extractable by a variety of organic solvents [38] . The reaction mechanism for the formation and extraction of ion associate may be expressed in this way:

where R means DIDC reagent.

Fig. 1 shows the negative LDI-TOF MS spectra of the organic phase when applying DIDC. It can be seen that the CuCl 2 − complex ion appeared in the spectra. The measured masses and isotopic distributions are in very good agreement with the calculated values (Software: Xmas5.0), as can be seen in inset 1. Thus, the copper content in the organic phase is confirmed, but it is evident that the solubility of CuCl 2 − complex ion is very low in organic solvents. Ion associate recombination seems to be the easiest way to explain the increase in CuCl 2 − solubility. As can be seen in inset 2, the DIDC cation (R + ) appeared in the positive LDI-TOF MS spectra of the organic phase. Therefore, due to the resulting copper ion associate content, the solubility of copper can increase in variety organic solvents. The suggested mechanism of ion association is described above.

We previously applied this reaction to the spectrophotometric determination of copper based on conventional LLE [38] and an air-assisted LLE using a dual-valve sequential injection manifold [39] . Therefore, the experimental conditions were not investigated herein, and the conditions previously reported [38] were chosen for this work: pH 3, 0.24 mol L − 1 chloride ions, 0.06 mmol L − 1 DIDC, amyl acetate as extraction solvent.

3.1. Investigation of the dispersive liquid–liquid microextraction procedure

3.1.1. Effect of the nature of the solvents

The main requirement for the extraction solvent is high efficiency for extraction of the target analyte. In general, the extraction solvents used in dispersive liquid–liquid microextraction should have a higher density than water in order to ensure the simple separation of phases by centrifugation. Since amyl acetate (density 0.875 g L − 1 ), which was selected as the extraction solvent, has a density lower than that of water, we used an auxiliary solvent to adjust the density. This approach was recently developed in our laboratory [40] and was applied to the determination of gold [40] and manganese [41] . The main principles for the selection of auxiliary solvents have been previously discussed in detail [40] . We selected tetrachloromethane (density 1.595 g L − 1 ) as the auxiliary solvent for further studies.

Whereas the extraction solvent and the auxiliary solvent have to have solubility as low as possible in water, the disperser solvent has to be highly miscible in both the aqueous and organic phase. Therefore, the nature of the disperser solvent can markedly affect the equilibrium of the water–extraction solvent–auxiliary solvent–disperser solvent system. Several organic solvents such as methanol, ethanol, acetone, acetononitrile, dimethylsulphoxide (DMSO), ethylene glycol, pyridine and N′,N′-dimethylforamide (DMFA), were tested as disperser solvents. A series of experiments was performed in which Cu(I) was extracted in the form of ion associate with DIDC under optimum experimental conditions using 0.5 mL of the mixture of solvents containing amyl acetate as the extraction solvent, tetrachloromethane as the auxiliary solvent and various disperser solvents in a 1:1:3 (v/v/v) ratio. Three repetitions of all measurements were performed. In order to choose the disperser solvent, the value of the analytical signal as well as its repeatability was considered. Based on the results obtained ( Fig. 2 ), methanol was selected as the most suitable disperser solvent.

3.1.2. Effect of solvents ratio

The effect of the ratio of extraction solvent, auxiliary solvent, and disperser solvent on the analytical signal and its repeatability in the amyl acetate–tetrachloromethane–methanol system was also studied. Two series of the mixtures of solvents were prepared. In the first, the ratio of extraction solvent and auxiliary solvent was kept constant at 1:1 (v/v), and the proportion of disperser solvent was altered. In the second, the ratio of auxiliary solvent and disperser solvent was fixed at 1:3 (v/v), and the proportion of extraction solvent was changed. The 0.5 mL samples of the mixture of solvents prepared in these ways were then used in the DLLME procedure for Cu(I):Cl:DIDC ion associate under optimum experimental conditions.

With an increase in the methanol proportion of the mixture of solvents from 1:1:1 to 1:1:2.5 (v/v/v), the analytical signal increased gradually ( Fig. 3 A). However, increasing the methanol proportion caused a sharp decrease in the volume of sedimented phase, consequently decrease in analytical signal. At a 1:1:4.5 (v/v/v) ratio, not enough sedimented phase formed for UV–Vis detection.

With an increase in the amyl acetate proportion in the mixture of solvents, the analytical signal decreased ( Fig. 3 B). This can be explained by the increase in the sedimented phase obtained. At a proportion of amyl acetate less than 0.8:1:3 (v/v/v), the volume of sedimented phase was less than 100 μL, which was not enough for subsequent UV–Vis analysis. Increasing the amyl acetate proportion in the mixture of solvents to more than a 2:1:3 (v/v/v) ratio might cause a decrease in the density of the mixture of solvents, such that it becomes lower than that of the aqueous phase, thus disabling simple phases separation by centrifugation.

The maximum analytical signal that simultaneously provided the best repeatability was obtained using a solvents ratio of 1:1:3 (v/v/v), and therefore this ratio was selected for further experiments.

3.2. Validation

Laboratories should introduce method validation in order to ensure that it is producing reliable analytical data [42] . In our work, the optimised DLLME procedure (0.5 mL of the mixture of solvents containing amyl acetate–tetrachloromethane–methanol in the volume ratio 1:1:3) was evaluated by checking the following factors: linearity, limit of detection, accuracy and precision, and selectivity.

3.2.1. Linearity and limit of detection

A calibration plot was constructed in the range of 0.020–0.090 mg L − 1 , with eight concentration levels. The regression equation was A = 0.001 + 2.103 × C (where A means the absorbance and C the concentration of copper in mg L − 1 ), with a correlation coefficient ( r ) of 0.9993, which indicates good linearity in the mentioned concentration range. The limit of detection (LOD) was calculated from the regression equation as 3 times the standard deviation of the blank samples and was found to be 0.005 mg L − 1 .

3.2.2. Precision and accuracy

Precision, which is the closeness of agreement between independent test results obtained under stipulated conditions [42] , was estimated as the relative standard deviation (RSD, %). Accuracy of measurement, which is the closeness of agreement between the result of a measurement and the true value of the measured [43] , was estimated as the recovery percentage (R, %). Both the precision and accuracy were evaluated for six replicate determinations at four different concentration levels of copper over two days during a single week. The obtained inter-day and intra-day results are summarised in Table 2 and show sufficient repeatability of the measurements.

3.2.3. Interference study

The effect of diverse ions on the determination of 0.060 mg L − 1 of Cu was studied according to the above-described procedure. The tolerable amount of each interferent was taken as a copper:interferent ratio that resulted in an error not exceeding ± 5%. Most of the examined ions (Na + , Mg 2+ , Ca 2+ , SO 4 2− , HCO 3 − , and CH 3 COO − ) did not interfere with the determination of copper at more than a 1000-fold excess; K + , NH 4 + , Al 3+ NO 3 − and H 3 PO 4 − did not interfere at more than a 100-fold excess and the cations (Li + , Co 2+ , Fe 2+ , and Zn 2+ ) did not disturb determination at more than a 10-fold excess.

3.3. Analytical application

The results obtained from the interference study indicate that the developed procedure would be suitable for the analysis of samples with a real matrix. The suggested DLLME procedure was applied to the determination of Cu in three kinds of commercially available samples (ice tea, apple drink, and multivitamin tablet) as well as in water samples such as tap water and mineral waters. A standard addition principle was applied and the samples were spiked with copper at two concentration levels. The results given in Table 3 showed good precision and accuracy of the determination and no matrix interference.

3.4. Figure of merits

The same reaction mechanism based on the formation of an ion associate of Cu(I) with DIDC in the presence of chloride ions as ligand was previously reported from our laboratory for conventional liquid–liquid extraction [38] and dual-valve sequential injection extraction [39] of copper followed by UV–Vis spectrophotometric detection. Each extraction technique has both advantages and disadvantages. A comparison of the main characteristics of these three procedures is given in Table 4 . Both the novel DLLME procedure and the previously reported DV-SIA procedure [39] have a significant advantages in comparison with the conventional LLE procedure: (a) in terms of the lower consumption of extraction solvent and consequent production of a lower amount of organic waste, thus making the procedure environmentally friendly; (b) shorter extraction and analysis times. Moreover, the DV-SIA procedure has, in comparison with conventional LLE, additional advantages such as (a) a closed system for the handling of organic solvent and (b) reduced risk of analyte loss and sample contamination. The suggested DLLME procedure also has a lower detection limit in comparison with both conventional LLE and the DV-SIA procedure. Moreover, no expensive instrumentation is needed for the determination, in contrast with dual-valve sequential injection procedure [39] .

4. Conclusion

A novel, simple and environmentally friendly procedure for copper determination based on the formation of an ion associate of Cu(I) with 1,3,3-trimethyl-2-[5-(1,3,3-trimethyl-1,3-dihydroindol-2-ylidene)-penta-1,3-dienyl]-3 H -indolium in the presence of chloride ions as ligand, followed by dispersive liquid–liquid microextraction of the formed ion associate into organic phase and UV–Vis spectrophotometric detection has been developed. The novel DLLME procedure for copper determination showed several advantages compared with conventional liquid–liquid extraction [38] : (a) lower consumption of extraction solvent and consequently less organic waste; (b) lower detection limit; and (c) shorter extraction and analysis times. Moreover, no expensive instrumentation is needed for determination, in contrast with the dual-valve sequential injection procedure [39] . The developed procedure was applied to the analysis of water samples.
