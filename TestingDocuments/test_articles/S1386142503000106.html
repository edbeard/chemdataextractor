Potentiometric, spectrometric, thermal and conductimetric studies on some 3-phenyl-4-(arylazo)-5-pyrazolones and their complexes with divalent cobalt metal ion

  • H.B. Hassib a ,
  • S.A. Abdel-Latif b , ,
  • a Faculty of Science, Chemistry Department, Cairo University, Giza, Egypt
  • b Faculty of Science, Chemistry Department, Helwan University, Cairo, Egypt
Received 10 July 2002, Revised 13 September 2002, Accepted 20 September 2002, Available online 13 August 2003

Abstract

3-phenyl-4-arylazo-5-pyrazolones (I–IV) have been synthesized and characterized by elemental, infrared (IR), ultraviolet and visible spectra (UV–Vis), proton nuclear magnetic resonance ( 1 H NMR) and Mass spectra. It has been proved that these compounds exhibit a keto-enol tautomerism in solution. The donor character of the substituent increases the enol form. The ionization constants of the investigated ligands have been determined potentiometrically and found to decrease in the order OCH 3 (IV)>CH 3 (III)>H(I)>Cl(II). The Co(II) complexes of the investigated 3-phenyl-4-arylazo-5-pyrazolones (I–IV) have been prepared and characterized by elemental and thermal analyses as well as by IR, UV–Vis, electronic transition, potentiometric, conductimetric and magnetic measurements. The data suggest octahedral geometry for Co(II) (1:1) complexes and tetrahedral for Co(II) (2:3) complexes.

1. Introduction

The coordination chemistry of pyrazolone-5-ones and their azo derivatives have attracted much attention by virtue of their applicability as potential ligands for a large number of metal ions [1] ; [2] . The metal chelates thus produced have wide applications in the dye industry, as analytical reagents for the microdetermination of metals and in biological uses [3] . Different methods were reported for the synthesis of 4-arylazo-5-pyrazolone [4] ; [5] . The electronic and infrared (IR) spectra of a large number of arylazo-pyrazolones were investigated for structure elucidation [6] ; [7] . It was confirmed that these compounds exist mainly as a chelated hydrazo-keto structure but under the influence of high electron accepting substituents and in highly polar solvents, the azopyrazolone compound was liable to exist in hydrazone-azo tautomeric equilibrium [8] . Nuclear magnetic resonance was used as a tool to determine the tautomeric forms of arylazo-3-, -4- and -5-pyrazolones and several related azo heterocycles in chloroform [9] . Mass spectral fragmentation patterns of arylazo-pyrazolones were found to be largely dependent on structure. Fragmentation might occur through rupture of the phenyl N-bond and less readily via rupture of the pyrazolone-oxycyclic N-bond through loss of ketene and secondary rupture of the ring-azo group bonds [10] , or cleavage might occur either side of the azo group [11] . The metal complexes of the arylazo-pyrazolone were prepared and their structure determined by electronic absorption spectra, IR, 1 H NMR spectra and thermogravimetric (TG) analyses [12] ; [13] ; [14] ; [15] ; [16] . The proton-ligand stability constants of different substituted pyrazolones and the stability constants of their complexes with bivalent metal ions were determined potentiometrically [17] ; [18] .

2. Experimental

All chemicals used were of the highest purity available from BDH. They include CoCl 2 ·6H 2 O, sodium hydroxide, ammonium hydroxide, potassium hydrogen phthalate, sodium carbonate, disodium salt of ethylenediamine-tetraacetic acid, sodium chloride and hydrochloric acid. The solvents used are ethanol, cyclohexane, deuterated dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) and dimethylformamide (DMF). These solvents were purified by distillation. The water used was doubly distilled.

The 3-phenyl-4-arylazo-5-pyrazolones were prepared as previously mentioned [19] by coupling the diazonium salt of substituted anilines in ethanolic sodium hydroxide solutions on cold. 3. Apparatus and techniques

The IR spectra of the prepared complexes were recorded as KBr pellets using FTIR-8201 PC (Shimadzu) spectrometer. Thermal analyses were carried out using Shimadzu TGA-50, DTA-50 TG and DTA analyzer. The Mass spectra were done using Shimadzu QP-5000 mass spectrometer coupled with Shimadzu GC-17 A gas chromatography. The NMR spectra were measured by using a Varian–Mercury 300 MHz NMR spectrometer.

The molar conductance of the investigated complexes in DMF (saturated solution) were measured using a Jenway 4310 conductivity meter. Electronic spectra were recorded in DMF solution using Jasco V-530 UV–Vis recording spectrophotometer.

Magnetic measurements of the complexes were made at room temperature using magnetic susceptibility balance (Sherwood Scientific), Cambridge Science Park, Cambridge, England. pH-metric titrations were performed using a Jenway 3310 pH-meter.

4. Results and discussion

The structure of the investigated ligands, (3-phenyl-4-arylazo-5-pyrazolones) were established by the use of IR, UV–Vis, 1 H NMR and mass spectral analyses. In so doing we were guided by previously published work [7] ; [8] ; [9] on the closely related pyrazolone type compounds.

4.1. Infrared spectra of the investigated ligands

The most significant IR bands that affect the structural features are listed in Table 2 . The broad and low frequency band appearing at 3421 cm −1 denotes the presence of intramolecular hydrogen bonding [20] . The ν NH appears as a medium broad one within the range 3124–3197 cm −1 for all compounds studied. The strong band appearing at 1685 cm −1 is ascribed to the stretching frequency of the carbonyl group. The ν NN bands are observed in the range 1488–1498 cm −1 conjugated with the aromatic ring skeletal vibrations ( ν CC) 1546–1604 cm −1 [7] . The strong band observed at 1248 cm −1 is assigned to the CO vibration.

4.2. Nuclear magnetic resonance

The chemical shifts of the different types of protons in DMSO and deuterated DMSO for the investigated ligands (I–IV) are reported [19] . In DMSO 3-phenyl-4-arylazo-5-pyrazolones (I–IV) exhibits a sharp singlet signal observed within the range 2.51–2.52 ppm which does not disappear upon deuteration. This signal is assigned to the aliphatic proton H 4 . The singlet signal observed downfield at 12.06–12.15 ppm is assigned to NH [19] . Further evidence has been gained by investigating the 1 H NMR spectrum of 1,3-diphenyl-4-phenylazo-5-pyrazolone. From the fact that in the latter spectrum the singlet observed at 12.06–12.15 ppm corresponding to NH was not observed which confirm the keto structure of the investigated ligands. On the other hand, the signals in the range 8.1–7.0 ppm corresponding to the aromatic protons (the integration curve shows 9 protons except for ligand (I) which shows 10 protons). For the ligand (III) a singlet observed at 2.33 ppm is assigned to CH 3 protons (the integration curve shows 3 protons). Also for ligand (IV) a singlet observed at 3.81 ppm is assigned to the OCH 3 protons.

4.3. Electronic absorption spectra of the investigated ligands

The electronic absorption spectra of the investigated compounds are scanned in ethanol ( D =24.3) and cyclohexane ( D =2.01) and depicted in Table 4 . In ethanol solutions the spectra show four bands within the region 220–450 nm. These bands are referred to as A (400–430 nm), B (290–292 nm), C (276–278 nm) and D (242 nm). According to previously reported results [21] on similar compounds, the two bands C and D are assigned to π-π* transitions within the pyrazolone and phenyl rings, respectively. Band B is ascribed to n -π* transition of the carbonyl and azo (NN) groups within the system. The broad absorption band observed within the wavelength range (400–430 nm) (A) arises from a transition involving electron migration along the entire conjugate system of the ligands i.e. it comprises charge transfer (CT) from the substituted phenyl ring to the carbonyl group by resonance and from hetero-ring by induction. This latter assumption is substantiated by the fact that λ max of this band is highly influenced by the nature of the substituent (X). The donor substituents cause a red shift relative to the unsubstituted derivative (H) in the order OCH 3 >CH 3 >H, The X-phenyl moiety acts as an electron donor center while the CO group acts as an electron acceptor. Thus these compounds behave as donor-acceptor conjugated system and the CT takes place between these two centers [8] .

In the case of chloro derivative, λ max is more or less similar to that of the unsubstituted compound. This behaviour can be ascribed to antagonistic effect viz. mesomeric and inductive effects. The former effect is due to the strong withdrawing character of the carbonyl group of the pyrazolone ring with respect to that of the chlorine while the later is due to the withdrawing character of the chloro substituent which is highly dependent on the neighbouring groups. Thus, the observed shift is the resultant of both effects. It is worth-mentioning that the keto form of the investigated compounds are enhanced by the acceptor group while donor groups enhance the enol form due to increased polarization of the carbonyl group. In cyclohexane solution, all bands are blue shifted except band B where a counter shift is observed, ( Table 4 ). The later band is red shifted. The difference in absorbance and shape of band B in both solvents indicate a change in the structure of the pyrazolone moiety by changing the polarity of the solvent. This can be explained on the basis of a shift in the keto–enol tautomerism where the enol-form is favoured in cyclohexane. 4.4. Mass spectra

The mass spectra of 3-phenyl-5-pyrazolone and 3-phenyl-4-arylazo-5-pyrazolone derivatives (I–IV) were carried out. In this work our attention is restricted only to the general mechanism of fragmentation thus not all the peaks are going to be interpreted due to the fact that there are usually many possibilities for molecular rearrangements of the produced ionic fragments. For 3-phenyl-5-pyrazolone, it is evident that primary cleavages occur at the hetercarbon atom bond as is the case in five-membered ring heteroaromatics [24] . The fragmentation pattern of the unsubstituted 3-phenyl-4-phenylazo-5-pyrazolone (I) shows a main peak of 264 (calc. M. Wt.=264), for ligand (II), the base peak is observed at 298 (calc. M. Wt.=298), for ligand (III), the base peak is observed at 278 (calc. M. Wt.=278, 100%), for ligand (IV), the base peak is observed at 294 (calc. M. Wt.=294, 100%). From the data obtained we concluded that the molecular weights are in good agreement with those of the calculated molecular weights of the investigated ligands [19] .

4.5. Potentiometric studies

The pH-meter readings were corrected for non-aqueous solvents [25] . It is clear from the data that the proton-ligand ionization constants decreased in the order OCH 3 >CH 3 >H>Cl (p K a (I)=9.80, p K a (II)=9.68, p K a (III)=10.08 and p K a (IV)=10.44). These results reveal that ionization is enhanced by the increase of the electron withdrawing power of the substituent.

The formation curves for the complexes were obtained according to previous recorded method [26] . The values of log β 1 and log β 2 derived from these data are 6.01 and 11.31, 7.00 and 12.38, 7.44 and 13.00, 7.64 and 13.80 for ligands I–IV, respectively. Also the values of free energy of formation calculated are 65.70, 71.90, 75.50 and 80.20 KJ mol −1 for the ligands I–IV, respectively. The large negative magnitudes of these values are indicative of the stability of the complexes formed.

4.6. Conductimetric titrations of Co 2+ metal ions with pyrazolone derivatives

The calculated molar ratio [L]/[M] are plotted against the corrected molar conductance values. The results indicate that the conductance increases with the addition of the metal ion solutions due to the release of the highly conducting hydrogen ions as a result of chelation. Inspection of the titration curves shows the presence of two distinctive breaks at metal to ligand molar ratios of 1:1 and 1:2, respectively, and 2:3, implying the formation of complex of formula M 2 L 3 .

4.7. Stoichiometry and structure of the solid complexes

The prepared solid complexes of Co 2+ with 3-phenyl-4-arylazo-5-pyrazolones (I–IV) were subjected to elemental analysis for their C, H, N, Cl and metal content, IR spectral studies, magnetic susceptibility, TG, DTA and electronic spectral analysis.

The results of elemental analysis given in Table 1 are in good agreement with the proposed formulae where in all cases 1:1 and 2:3 (M:L) solid complexes were separated. The suggested formulae for the 1:1 complexes are [M 2 L 2 OHCl· x H 2 O]· y H 2 O or [M 2 L 2 Cl 2 · x H 2 O]· y H 2 O whereas for the 2:3 complexes the suggested formulae are [M 2 L 3 OH]· y H 2 O or [M 2 L 3 Cl]· y H 2 O where x and y are the numbers of inner and outer sphere water molecules, respectively. These numbers are varied in the range 0–2 each.

4.8. Thermogravimetric and differential thermal analysis

The TG curves obtained by following the decrease in sample weight (water molecules were expelled) with the linear increase in temperature (10 °C min −1 ) to 800–1000 °C are given in Fig. 1 ; Fig. 2 . The found and calculated (based on the suggested stoichiometry) mass losses are collected in Table 5 . Inspection of the TG curves for 1:1 complexes of Co 2+ with ligands I, II and IV reveals an initial weight loss occurring at temperature <160 °C and this is attributed to loss of water of hydration during the drying of the complexes. At higher temperatures <290 °C loss of coordinated water occurs. On further heating, the TG curves show the decomposition of the organic moiety of the chelates and this continues till a constant weight is obtained where the metal oxide (MO) or (MO+MCl 2 ) residues are formed as final product. TG analyses of 2:3 (M:L) complexes of Co 2+ with all of the investigated ligands (I–IV) proved to contain neither water of hydration nor water of coordination. This behaviour is consistent with elemental analysis data, ( Table 1 ).

4.9. Infrared spectra of the complexes

The main IR bands of the solid complexes given in Table 3 are compared with those of the free ligands, ( Table 2 ). The ν CO of the free ligands completely disappeared on complex formation. The absence of the CO band in the spectra of the complexes indicates that the complex formation takes place through the displacement of the hydrogen which may be in the enol form. Also the disappearance of CO indicates that it is a center of chelation as it loses its double bond character (COM).

The OH stretching frequency appears in the spectra of (1:1) complexes studied as a broad band around 3400 cm −1 . This is due to the presence of water of hydration and/or coordinated water. In some complexes, a sharp band appears at around 3550 cm −1 ascribed to chelated OH − ion, which neutralizes the charge on the metal ion. As the strength of OH chelation to the central metal ion increases a shift to lower wavelength is observed. The OH frequency will then be masked under the previously detected broad band. The ν NN and ν CO exhibit a shift to higher wavelength on complex formation indicating that they are centers of chelation. The spectra of metal complexes exhibit bands in the ranges 505–567 and 409–426 cm −1 which may be assigned to (MN) and (MO) stretching frequencies [27] , respectively. In other words, these bands are possibly due to the formation of coordinate bonds between the donor atoms (O and N) and the central metal ion.

4.11. Magnetic susceptibility measurements

The magnetic moments ( μ ) of Co 2+ complexes were calculated using the general equation used for first raw transition metal ions [28] . From this equation we can determine the number of unpaired electrons present in the metal ion and consequently we can indicate, in some cases, the structure of the complex.

The obtained magnetic moments of 1:1 and 2:3 (M:L) complexes with divalent cobalt metal ion are in the range 7.03–8.23 B.M. indicating the presence of three unpaired electrons per metal ion ( Table 1 ). These experimental values are found to be higher than those calculated using the spin only approximation thus denoting that orbital contribution can be neglected in these complexes.

All complexes show paramagnetism indicating that the ligands have little or no effects on the metal ion field.

Based on the above mentioned discussion, the proposed chemical structures for the Co 2+ complexes suggest octahedral geometry with respect to Co 2+ -I, Co 2+ -II and Co 2+ -IV (1:1), tetrahedral for Co 2+ -III (1:1) and all the 2:3 cobalt complexes which proved to contain no coordinated water molecules. 5. Conclusion

According to the results of elemental analyses, IR, electronic spectra, potentiometric, conductimetric, thermal analysis and magnetic moment measurements, the divalent Cobalt complexes with the investigated ligands may be formulated, where 1:1 and 2:3 (M:L) complexes are formed through the formation of Cl and OH bridges. The investigated ligands exhibit a keto–enol tautomerism in solution. The donor character of the substituent increases the enol form. The ionization constants of the investigated ligands have been determined potentiometrically and found to decrease in the order OCH 3 (IV)>CH 3 (III)>H(I)>Cl(II).

The proposed chemical structures for the Co 2+ complexes suggest octahedral geometry with respect to Co 2+ -I, Co 2+ -II and Co 2+ -IV (1:1), tetrahedral for Co 2+ -III (1:1) and tetrahedral for all the 2:3 cobalt complexes that proved to contain no coordinated water molecules.
