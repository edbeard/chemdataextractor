One- and two- photon absorption properties of novel multi-branched molecules

Xin Zhou a , Ai-Min Ren a , Ji-Kang Feng * a , Xiao-Juan Liu a , Junxiang Zhang b and Juzheng Liu b
a State Key Laboratory of Theoretical and Computational Chemistry, Institute of Theoretical Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun, 130023, P. R. China. E-mail: Jikangf@yahoo.com
b Department of Chemistry, Southeast University, Nanjing, 210096, P. R. China

Received 19th April 2002 , Accepted 16th July 2002

First published on the web 20th August 2002

One- photon absorption (OPA) and two- photon absorption (TPA) properties of a class of novel multi-branched molecules have been investigated. These molecules exhibit strong one- and two- photon absorption and have comparatively large TPA cross sections. The one- photon absorption of compounds containing two different stilbene (or a stilbene and an azobenzene ) derivatives offers an unprecedented combination of that of individual parts. On the basis of correct geometries, optimized by using the AM1 method, the TPA cross sections of molecules were examined. The peak TPA cross section values ( δ max ) calculated with the ZINDO-SOS method are in the range 11.35–41.18 × 10 −48 cm 4 ·s photon −1 . The δ max value increases as the donor strength increases and the π-center has an important effect on the magnitude of the δ max value. Compared with individual parts, the TPA cross sections of combinations of two different stilbene (or a stilbene and an azobenzene ) derivatives exhibit a significant enhancement. Thus, another design strategy to maximize the TPA cross section is established.

Introduction

The two- photon absorption of organic molecules has attracted much attention recently as reflected by the many experimental and theoretical results reported for a selection of systems. 1–11 This interest stems from various technical applications that potentially can be realized using organic materials, such as three-dimensional optical storage, 12–14 two- photon fluorescence excitation microscopy , 15–19 two- photon optical power limiting, 20–22 two- photon upconverted lasing, 23,24 and photodynamic therapy. 25 Various design strategies have been employed to synthesize organic molecules with large TPA cross sections. Albota et al. 1 synthesized a class of organic molecules with different donor (D) or acceptor (A) attached to a trans -stilbene type of π center and formed D–π–D, D–A–D or A–D–A combinations. They pointed out that conjugation length, D/A strength, and molecular symmetry are important factors responsible for the increase of TPA cross sections. Meanwhile, a different kind of organic molecule based on chromophores containing fluorene as a π-center coupled with D or A was synthesized by Reinhardt et al. 26 They showed that the TPA cross sections become large if a planar fluorene is used as the π-center. The important role of the π-center for the design of large TPA dyes was demonstrated by Luo et al. , who showed that the TPA cross section of the D–π–D chromophores based on dithienothiophene as the π-center are larger by an order of magnitude than those of the fluorene derivatives. 8 Recent reports from various groups have shown that increasing the dimensionality of the charge-transfer networks is another design strategy for enhancing the TPA cross section. Prasad and co-workers showed that a multi-branched structure significantly increased the TPA cross section in comparison to one-branched counterparts. 27 The effect of the electronic coupling between different branches was studied by Macak et al. by using the ab initio response theory. 28 Recently, the group of Prasad discussed excited-state energy-transfer properties of a new multi-branched molecule. They found that linear absorption spectra and luminescence spectra of the component and the multi-branched chromophores had revealed a hypochromic effect for the multi-branched dye . 6 In ref. 9 and 11 , Cho et al. paid attention to two- photon absorption and nonlinear optical properties of octupolar molecules. They point out that the TPA cross section increases as the strength of the donor or acceptor increases and the TPA cross section of a few series of octupolar molecules is linearly proportional to the first hyperpolarizability.

Marder et al. showed that mixtures of symmetrical bis-donor stilbene derivatives offer an unprecedented combination of broad optical limiting bandwidth and high linear transparency. 29 Nevertheless, there does not appear to have been any systematic theoretical study on the TPA process of compounds containing two different conjugated systems. In this paper, we design and synthesize a class of chromophores , in which two different stilbene (or a stilbene and an azobenzene ) derivatives are connected by an ether chain, and study one- and two- photon absorption in these compounds.

3 Results and discussion

3.1 Molecular structures

The structures of the molecules studied in the present work are shown in Fig. 1 . They have the structures D–π–D, D–π–A, and combinations of two different structures. The molecules a1 and a2 have the same type of conjugated bridge ( bis-(styryl)benzene ) with additional donors—alkoxy side groups. The difference between a1 and a2 is the type of substituents: a1 : R = H, a2 : R = NEt 2 . By comparing a1 and a2 , one can obtain some information about the effect of the donor strength on the TPA cross sections. In order to be sure of the influence of the π center on the TPA cross sections, we have designed the molecules b1 and b2 with the same electron donor and acceptor. In the molecule b1 , trans -stilbene acts as the π center, while for b2 the π center is phenylazobenzene. Subsequently, to probe the effect of combinations of different conjugated systems on TPA cross sections, we have synthesized the molecules A , B , C , D . The geometric structures have been optimized with the semi-empirical AM1 method. For a1 , a2 , b1 , b2 , and the chain 1 and chain 2 parts of A – D , except for the terminal substituents, the π center is almost in the same plane. The planar structures can safely be employed in the spectroscopic studies on stilbene-like systems, which has been supported by a lot of theoretical and experimental work. 34–36 In ref. 34 , the authors employed the CASSCF/CASPT2 method to perform a detailed search on the stilbene-like system and came to the following conclusion: the optimal QCFF/PI distance of the ethylene bond for ground-state planar stilbene is predicted to be the most stable conformer. The correspondence between the electronic spectra in crystal and solution phases, together with the available experimental information in the gas phase, indicates that a consistent interpretation of the electronic spectrum of stilbene can be achieved from calculations on the planar structure. Nonplanarity is expected to have a minor influence on the computed properties.

3.2 One- and two- photon absorption

In order to get one- and two- photon absorption spectra , we used the ZINDO program to calculate configuration interaction ( CI ) on the basis of equilibrium geometry. The CI calculations included 196 singly (SCI) and 11 doubly (DCI) excited configurations, a total of 208 configurations including the ground state.
3.2.1 One- photon absorption. Table 1 lists the calculated and experimental (given in parentheses) wavelength λ (1) , oscillator strength f and the nature of the transition for the maximum one- photon absorption (OPA) of every compound. One can see from Table 1 that the results we calculated are in agreement with experimental values. The λ (1) max values increase with the change of the substituent in the order Et 2 N ( a2 ) > H ( a1 ). The reason for this result is that Et 2 N– has a stronger electron-donating ability than H–. From b1 to b2 , the λ (1) max value exhibit a bathochromic shift by 27.4 nm as the π center ( trans -stilbene ) is replaced by phenylazobenzene. In the molecule b2 , the lone electron pair of the N atom takes part in the delocalization of the π band and makes the extent of the π-electron delocalization over the entire molecule larger than that of b1 . It is interesting to note that there appear to be two outstanding peaks in the one- photon absorption spectra of A – D (shown in Fig. 2 ). From Fig. 2 , we can learn that the first absorption peak of A – D stems from the contribution of chain 2 and the second one comes from the contribution of chain 1. It is notable that there is always another peak, centered at around 300 nm, in each spectrum . However, the origin of this peak has not been identified. In order to reveal these phenomena micromechanically, we will take the molecule A as an example to investigate its frontier orbitals and get insight into the nature of these peaks. For compound A , the first main excited state S 3 comes from the mixing of electron transition from HOMO − 1 to LUMO and HOMO − 1 to LUMO + 1. As seen in Fig. 3 , the electron transitions mostly concentrate on chain 2, which is good agreement with the experimental absorption spectrum. The two electronic transitions may be regarded as the charge transfer between the electron donor D and the electron acceptor A. The second main excited state S 6 is mostly constituted by HOMO to LUMO + 2, which is equal to the internal charge transfer of chain 1; the experiment shows the same result. It is notable that the third absorption is an unknown peak in the experimental one- photon absorption spectrum of A . Our calculations give some reasonable explanations (shown in Fig. 4 ). The third absorption comes from mixing of the electron transitions from HOMO − 3 to LUMO + 7, HOMO − 2 to LUMO + 4, HOMO to LUMO + 10 orbitals. Such a kind of transition can be assigned as electron transfer on chain 1. A possible reason that the third absorption apex appears is the combination of two chains and interaction between them. Molecules B , C and D have been dealt with by the same method and the similar results found.

3.2.2 Two- photon absorption. In this subsection we will present calculation results of TPA properties of molecules. The positions of the two- photon absorption maximum ( λ max (2) ) and nature of the transition are summarized in Table 2 ; the corresponding experimental values are given in parentheses. We can see from Table 2 that the λ max (2) of a2 we calculated is in good agreement with experimental value. From a1 to a2 , there is a slight shift of the peak position of the two- photon absorption to longer wavelength upon increasing the donor strength. With the π center of b1 having been replaced by phenylazobenzene of b2 , the λ max (2) is shifted by 20 nm. From a N to b N ( N = 1,2), the λ max (2) values are blueshifted by 60–80 nm. This is because the length of the conjugated chain affects the magnitude of the peak two- photon absorption. The combined molecules A – D exhibit shorter λ max (2) than a N and b N molecules. A reasonable explanation for this phenomenon is that the nature of the transition of the molecules changed. From Table 1 , one can find that the TPA final states from a1 to D are S 5 , S 5 , S 8 , S 8 , S 12 , S 12 , S 16 , S 12 respectively.

In other words, the combination of two different conjugated systems unites the TPA of two individual parts to form a new two- photon absorption, and results in an increase in the magnitude of the TPA cross section which is larger than the simple sum of TPA cross sections of individual parts.

4. Conclusions

We have investigated the one- and two- photon absorption properties of a series of novel multi-branched molecules. The one- photon absorption of compounds containing two different stilbene (or a stilbene and an azobenzene ) derivatives offers an unprecedented combination over that of the individual parts. These molecules exhibit comparatively large TPA cross sections and the TPA cross sections of combinations are much larger than the simple sum of that of the individual parts.
