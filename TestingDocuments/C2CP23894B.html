Strategic emission color tuning of highly fluorescent imidazole -based excited-state intramolecular proton transfer molecules †

Highly fluorescent molecules harnessing the excited state intramolecular proton transfer (ESIPT) process are promising for a new generation of displays and light sources because they can offer very unique and novel optoelectronic properties which are different from those of conventional fluorescent dyes . To realize innovative ESIPT devices comprising full emission colors over the whole visible region, a molecular design strategy for predictable emission color tuning should be established. Here, we have developed a general strategy for a wide-range spectral tuning of imidazole -based ESIPT materials based on three different strategies – introduction of a nodal plane model, extension of effective conjugation length, and modification of heterocyclic rings. A series of nine ESIPT molecules were designed, synthesized and comprehensively investigated for their characteristic emission properties. All these molecules commonly showed no clear and transparent visible range absorption with no absorption color, but showed different colors of intense photoluminescence over broad visible regions from 450 nm (HPI) to 630 nm (HPNO) depending on their molecular structure. With the aid of density functional theory and time-dependent DFT calculations using M06, wB97XD, and B3LYP parameters with the 6-31G(d,p) basis set, these tuned emission bands of nine emitters were assigned from the stabilized excited state conformations that were derived from modified molecular structures.

Introduction

Recently, organic fluorescent molecules harnessing the excited-state intramolecular proton transfer (ESIPT) process have been drawing great attention due to their unique photophysical properties which facilitate novel optoelectronic applications such as laser dyes , 1 solar energy concentrators, 2 chemosensors, 3,4 and electroluminescent materials. 5 Because the ESIPT molecules are normally more stable in enol (E) form in the ground states but in keto (K)* form in the excited states, photo-excitation of them is immediately followed by the four-level cyclic proton -transfer reactions (E → E* → K* → K → E) mediated by the intramolecular H-bonds (see Scheme 1a ). 6 It is characteristically observed, therefore, that the absorption from E and emission from K* result in an abnormally large Stokes shift with no self-absorption, providing an ideal scheme for the proton -transfer lasers, 7 photoscreeners, 8 and UV-photostabilizers. 9
Scheme 1 (a) Schematic representation of the ESIPT photocycle. (b) Molecular structures of a series of nine ESIPT molecules. The green dashed lines indicate the nodal planes perpendicular to the molecular plane.

Based on such unique properties, novel classes of highly emissive oxazole , 10 oxadiazole , 11 and imidazole -based multifunctional ESIPT molecules were recently investigated, 12–14 paving the way for innovative optoelectronic applications of the intramolecular proton transfer process. Especially, it was demonstrated that the proper molecular structure for ESIPT, particularly the 2-hydroxyphenyl imidazole, could effectively suppress the concentration quenching and even enhance the fluorescence emission efficiencies in the solid state, if it is incorporated with multiphenyl substituents. 12 As a result, highly efficient amplified spontaneous emission as well as blue and white organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) could be demonstrated with these imidazole derivatives. 12,13,15,16

Moreover, based on the characteristic 4-level photophysical properties of the ESIPT process, the “molecular pixel” concept for organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) was recently proposed, 15,17 where the energy transfer between two different imidazole -based ESIPT emitters, either in mixed films 16 or in covalently bonded single molecules, 12 could be completely frustrated. To realize innovative ESIPT devices which comprise full emission colors over the whole visible region suitable for practical device application, however, a molecular design strategy for predictable emission color tuning of ESIPT fluorophores should be established beforehand.

For the spectral tuning of imidazole -based ESIPT molecules, we have designed and synthesized a series of nine molecules based on three different strategies: introduction of a nodal plane model, extension of effective conjugation length, and modification of central heterocyclic rings (see Scheme 1b ). All these ESIPT molecules commonly showed no clear and transparent visible range absorption with no absorption color, but showed different colors of intense photoluminescence over broad visible regions from 450 nm to 630 nm depending on their molecular structures. In this work, the molecular design strategy will be comprehensively discussed by comparing their photophysical properties in terms of the experimental data and theoretical calculation.

Materials and methods

Materials and synthesis

Materials obtained from commercial suppliers were used as received. Reactions were monitored using thin layer chromatography ( TLC ) with commercial TLC plates ( silica gel 60 F 254 , Merck Co.). Chemical structures were fully identified by 1 H and 13 C NMR (Bruker, Avance DPX-300), GC-Mass (JEOL, JMS-AX505WA), and elemental analysis (CE Instrument, EA1110). A series of nine ESIPT materials, HPI and its derivatives (see Scheme 1b ), were synthesized according to the classical method of lophine synthesis in good yields. 18 Detailed synthetic procedures and the corresponding structural characterization are elaborated in ESI. †

Measurements

Absorption spectra were obtained using either of the two UV-visible spectrophotometers (Scinco S-2040 and Shimadzu UV-1650PC). Photoluminescence emission spectra were obtained using a fluorescence spectrophotometer (Varian, Cary Eclipse); emission spectra were corrected for wavelength-dependent sensitivity of the detector. Photoluminescence quantum efficiencies ( Φ PL values) for solutions were obtained using quinine sulphate (10 μM in 1 M H 2 SO 4 ) as in ref. 19 The Φ PL values of thin films on quartz plates were measured using a 6 inch integrating sphere (Labsphere, 3P-GPS-060-SF) equipped with a 325 nm CW He–Cd laser (Omnichrome, Series 56) and a PMT detector (Hamamatsu, PD471) attached to a monochromator (Acton Research, Spectrapro-300i). The detailed procedure for obtaining solid-state Φ PL values has been described elsewhere. 20 Time-resolved fluorescence lifetime experiments were performed through the time-correlated single photon counting ( TCSPC ) methods by using a FluoTime 200 instrument (Picoquant, Germany). A 342 nm diode laser (pulse energy = 35 pJ) with a repetition rate of 10 MHz was used as the excitation source. Fluorescence decay times were analyzed by FluoFit Pro software, using exponential fitting models through deconvolution employing instrumental response functions. Cyclic voltammetric experiments were performed using a model 273A machine (Princeton Applied Research) with a one-compartment electrolysis cell consisting of a platinum working electrode, a platinum wire counter- electrode , and a quasi Ag + /Ag electrode as reference. Measurements were performed in 0.5 mM CH 2 Cl 2 solution, with tetrabutylammonium tetrafluoroborate as a supporting electrolyte, at a scan rate of 50 mV s −1 . Each oxidation potential was calibrated using ferrocene as a reference.

Calculations

All density functional theory (DFT) and time-dependent DFT (TD-DFT) calculations were carried out in the gas phase using the Gaussian 03 21 and 09 22 quantum-chemical package. The geometry optimizations for the ground state of E form of four selected imidazole derivatives (HPI, HPO, HPNI, and HPNO) were performed using M06, wB97XD, and B3LYP functionals with the 6-31G(d,p) basis set. Vibrational frequency calculations at the same level were performed for the obtained structures to confirm the global minimum. We compared the results from different functionals and concluded that DFT calculations employing Becke's three-parameter hybrid exchange functional and the Lee–Yang–Parr gradient-corrected correlation functional (B3LYP) and the 6-31G** basis set 23 appear to be appropriate for a series of our nine imidazole -based ESIPT molecules (see Fig. S7 and S8 in ESI † ). The ground state optimizations of the E form geometry for the rest of the series were performed using B3LYP functional with the 6-31G** basis set. 24

Results and discussion

To be useful in high performance optoelectronic device applications like in OLEDs or lasers, it is important to develop highly emissive systems operating not only in the dilute solution state but also in the condensed solid-state. However, in many cases, the fluorescence efficiency of the large Stokes shifted K* emission is generally very low due to the presence of unfavorable non-radiative deactivation pathways of K* states, which is also a typical drawback in ordinary ESIPT systems reported so far. 25,26

Different from these ordinary ESIPT molecules, it was recently reported that imidazole -based molecules with multiphenyl substituents (2-(1,4,5-triphenyl-1 H -imidazol-2-yl)phenol, HPI) could favorably reduce the intermolecular vibronic interactions which would otherwise induce the non-radiative deactivation process. 27 In the HPI molecule, for example, a deliberately incorporated pair of twisted phenyl groups at the 1- and 5-position (see Scheme 1b ) of the central imidazole ring could impose strong steric limitation so as to prevent close stacking of the active ESIPT chromophores and thus to increase Φ PL in the solid state. 12,13,27 One of the most important applications of these highly fluorescent molecules is to use them as an emitting material in organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs). Among them, materials for white organic light emitting diodes (WOLEDs) are drawing special attention in both industry and academia as a promising source for the next generation solid-state lighting. The most straightforward method for generating white-light in WOLEDs, in principle, is simply blending different chromophores emitting the three primary colors (red, green, and blue) or two complementary colors. 12 However, in reality, simple mixing of conventional fluorescent dyes is complicated and problematic due to inevitable energy transfer between dyes , leading to the dominant emission from lower bandgap components, even when they are present in very small amounts. 28–30 In contrast, energy transfer between two different HPI-based ESIPT emitters, either in mixed films or in covalently bonded single molecules, 12 could be completely blocked due to their characteristic 4-level photophysical emission scheme mediated by proton transfer .

Briefly, energy transfer could be totally frustrated in HPI-based molecular dyads, due to the depleted ground state population of the emitting state (K) of low energy acceptors, and the concept of ‘molecular pixel’ was demonstrated for the first time. 12 To extend this innovative idea to the WOLEDs and full-color displays which comprise various proton transfer emission colors over a wide-range visible region, the molecular design strategy for tailoring their emission colors was investigated in this work.

In the weakly polar and aprotic chloroform solution, the long wavelength emission from a keto (K) tautomer is mainly observed above 450 nm in a series of HPI compounds. It is noteworthy that despite the similarity in absorption spectra , in which the S 0 → S 1 peak wavelengths are all located within 330–360 nm, the corresponding emission peaks reveal remarkable differences (see Fig. 1 and Fig. S1–S4, ESI † ). For spectral tuning of ESIPT emission, three different strategies were thoughtfully introduced in the design of the molecules: (1) introduction of a nodal plane model, (2) extension of effective conjugation length, and (3) modification of central heterocyclic rings.

The first strategy is called as ‘nodal plane model’, which was proposed by Nagaoka et al. as a simple but qualitative model. 31,32 It provides useful guidelines for predicting the stabilization due to ESIPT without performing large-size theoretical calculations. In this model, the stabilization effect of the additional aromatic group at the 2-position of the imidazole ring can be predicted by considering the nodal pattern of the wave function and the delocalization of electrons in the excited states. As in the case of a particle in a two-dimensional rectangular potential box, the wave function of the S 0 state of the enol (E) form has no nodal plane. In the excited state (S 1 ), however, one nodal plane exists along the reconfigured double bonds of the keto (K*) form as a result of proton transfer (see Scheme 1a ). Thus, only the extended aromatic conjugation length along the nodal plane could have a significant impact on the stabilization of the excited state of K*.

According to this approach, we introduced a naphthalene group at the 2-substitution position of a central imidazole ring, to give the stabilization effect through the nodal plane (see Scheme 1b ). Similar to Nagaoka's results, emission spectra of molecules with the extended aromatic rings along the nodal plane showed drastically lowered energy of K*. 33 Consequently, in the newly synthesized HPNI, HPNIC, and HPNO molecules, the naphthalene group significantly stabilized the energy of K* by enhanced conjugation via delocalization of π electrons along the naphthalene backbone (see the green dashed line in the HPNI structure, N means “ naphthalene ”, Scheme 1b ), 32,33 and they showed a huge bathochromic shift (Stokes shift ∼ 12 489 cm −1 ) of photoluminescence (PL) compared to HPI ( λ max = 570 nm for HPNI, 590 nm for HPNIC, and 630 nm for HPNO).

On the other hand, if a fused naphthalene ring lies out of the nodal plane, the stabilization effect was significantly decreased, as expected. To re-confirm this, NHPI and HBPI (see Scheme 1b ) were additionally synthesized and their photophysical properties were examined. It was found that the absorption and emission λ max of NHPI and HBPI are almost similar to those of HPI. In NHPI, the nodal plane lies totally out of the fused aromatic rings of the naphthalene moiety. As a result, the extended aromatic conjugation length of the additional aromatic ring has no effect on the stabilization of the K* states. Slightly different from NHPI, absorption and emission λ max of HBPI were slightly bathochromically shifted up to 20 nm, as shown in Table 1 . This slight stabilization effect was probably due to the position and rotation effect of biphenyl , because the nodal plane could be temporally effective while rotating.

The second strategy for tuning their bandgap is extension of effective conjugation length of a molecule. It is well known that the elongation of the conjugation path will increase the optical nonlinearity of organic molecules , while accompanied by a red-shift of the absorption spectra. 34 In a basic molecule HPI, two phenyl rings at the 4- and 5-position of the central imidazole ring are almost perpendicular to the chromophore plane, and effective conjugation length in this multiphenyl system is normally very short according to the calculation results.

Fortunately, we found an easy way for extending the conjugation length of the HPI molecule. It was the crosslinking of two phenyl rings at the 4- and 5-position to make more planar structures. In the synthesis of the HPI series, 1,2-diketone such as benzil ( 1,2-diphenylethane-1,2-dione ) is required to form a central imidazole unit. 27 After simple alternation of benzil by a crosslinked compound ( phenanthrene-9,10-dione ), HPIC, HBPIC, and HPNIC (C means “crosslinked”) could be easily synthesized (see ESI † for the reaction details).

Because the effective conjugation length is affected by the planarity of the molecule, as shown in Table 1 , all the three crosslinked compounds showed a bathochromic shift in absorption λ max (∼50 nm) as well as in photoluminescence (∼30 nm) compared to the initial molecule, HPI. The extended conjugation length from the crosslinking strategy, however, has only an indirect and limited effect on the stabilization of K*, resulting in rather smaller change in their emission than the nodal plane model.

The last strategy for the emission spectrum tuning was modification of central heterocyclic rings. It is well known that substitution of an electron-donating or an accepting group results in a decrease in electronic transition energy, leading to change of highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels of the molecule. 35,36 According to this scheme, we changed the central imidazole unit of HPI to oxazole , which is a more electron withdrawing (deficient) heterocyclic aromatic compound . Because of the mesomeric effect of more electron-accepting oxazole ring than an initial imidazole group, the LUMO levels of the compounds with oxazole central units (HPO and HPNO, O means “ oxazole ”) were stabilized more than the HOMO level. As a consequence, more significantly Stokes shifted emissions were observed from HPO ( λ max,ems = 475 nm) and HPNO ( λ max,ems = 630 nm), respectively.

Especially, it is noteworthy that among the listed three different methods, two or more strategies can be combined to tune the energy level more effectively. For example, extremely large Stokes shifted red emission (Stokes shift ∼ 12 489 cm −1 ) from the HPNO molecule was due to the nodal plane effect as well as the substitution effect of the central unit. HPNIC is another example, because the orange emission ( λ max,ems = 590 nm) originated from the crosslinking as well as the nodal plane effect. Because these fluorescent molecules can be directly applied to the emission layer in OLEDs, wide-range color-tuned ESIPT materials have great potential for full color displays in future.

Another important characteristic of these HPI-based ESIPT chromophores is their intense emission in the solid state as well as in the solution state. As mentioned above, thanks to the twisted phenyl ring(s), they all showed intense fluorescence in the solid state. 12,13 To identify their photophysical properties, absolute PL efficiencies in the solid state were monitored, together with fluorescence lifetime and rate constants for the decay after ESIPT. As shown in Table 1 and Table S1 (see ESI † ), intriguing photophysical properties are observed, for which the tautomer emission yield reveals a decreasing tendency upon lowering of the emission gap. For the K* → K deactivation process, as an empirical approach ( i.e. energy gap law), 37 the rate constant of a K* → K internal conversion can be estimated by ν exp(− α Δ E ) where α is the proportionality constant and Δ E indicates the emission energy gap. The non-radiative decay rate constant ( k nr ) can be deduced from k nr = k obs − k r , in which k obs and k r indicate the observed and radiative decay rate constants, respectively. k r can be deduced by k r = k obs Φ PL , in which Φ PL specifies the emission quantum yield. In our study, almost all HPI derivatives showed intense fluorescence with rather fast k r , compared to previously reported ESIPT fluorophores. 35,38 Especially, previously reported ESIPT red-emitting materials typically showed very weak fluorescence emission of less than Φ PL = 0.005, 35 but synthesized HPNI, HPNIC, and HPNO molecules showed several times stronger emission efficiencies than the typical ESIPT orange–red fluorophores in the solution state. Actually, as shown in Table 1 , absolute Φ PL values of solid state molecules (thin films on quartz plates) were identified to be 0.223, 0.156, and 0.04, respectively.

To rationalize the trends of the energy gaps of these emission-tuned chromophores , we performed a theoretical calculation of the HOMO and LUMO levels and vertical electronic transition energy of the compounds and compared the calculated results with experimental data . All calculations discussed in this work were carried out using the GAUSSIAN 03 21 and 09 22 package. On the basis of our previous successful calculation for ESIPT molecules, 12,36 the geometry optimizations for S 0 states were performed using the DFT method with the B3LYP/6-31G** basis set.

There exist a number of different theoretical calculation methods for evaluating excited states of molecules, such as CISD, CCSD, and CASSCF. These methods provide more accurate results than TD-DFT, but they require very expensive computational cost and still have some practical limitations. 39,40 Fortunately, recent studies showed that TD-DFT methods can reliably estimate the excited state properties for ESIPT-allowed molecules with adequate accuracy at relatively moderate computational cost, 42,43 compared to classical approaches which are more accurate but a bit more time consuming. 44–46 In particular, it is reported that the Franck–Condon curves obtained from the corresponding GSIPT curves using TD-DFT methods can provide acceptable results in predicting the ESIPT process without computationally expensive optimization procedures of excited state geometries. 47–49

To get insight into the proton transfer process of the series of molecules, potential energy surface calculation of the representative molecule, HPI, was performed based on a distinguished coordinate approach using DFT and TD-DFT methods with the B3LYP/6-31G** basis set. The GSIPT curve of HPI (see Fig. S9, ESI † ) shows that E form ( r O(2)–H(1) = 1.00 Å) is the most stable tautomeric form and no shallow minima of the K form in the ground state. On the other hand, the Franck–Condon ESIPT curve of HPI shows two minima corresponding to E ( r O(2)–H(1) = 1.00 Å) and K ( r O(2)–H(1) = 1.85 Å) forms, respectively (see Fig. S8, ESI † ). The K form is found to be more stable than its E form by 6.1 kcal mol −1 and the energy barrier height of the proton transfer process is 3.0 kcal mol −1 . These results are comparable with our previous ones which are obtained from the geometry optimization of the excited state K form using TD-DFT. 12 Based on this approach, the geometry optimizations for the ground state K form of the rest of the series of molecules were performed and Franck–Condon vertical transition energies were calculated using DFT and TD-DFT, respectively, with the B3LYP/6-31G** basis set.

Fig. 2a shows experimental HOMO and LUMO energy levels in the enol tautomer, which were obtained from cyclic voltammetry and optical spectroscopy . The tendency of the spectral shift by the aforementioned empirical approach based on the relationship between HOMO and LUMO is depicted in Fig. 2 . From the calculation results, the replacement of central heterocyclic rings ( imidazole ) with oxazole units decreased the HOMO level, while the addition of the naphthalene group instead of the 2-hydroxyphenyl site significantly decreased the LUMO level by stabilization of the energy level at the K* state.

After obtaining the computational calculation results, the expected photophysical properties were correlated with the experimental data, to prove our molecular design strategies more clearly. Fig. 3 shows the comparison of experimental optical bandgap and calculated results. It is noteworthy that the linear plotted line gives a sufficiently straight feature ( r = 0.91426, SD = 0.06369). Likewise, the calculated transition energies of K form of the molecules were plotted and compared to the experimental results (emission maximum) for each molecule (see Fig. S10, ESI † ). Obtained linear correlation from them ( r = 0.96378, SD = 0.08512) strongly suggests that our theoretical calculations have a quite good correlation for the series of HPI derivatives. Consequently, with our wide-range spectral tuning strategy, new pioneering materials for full color displays and WOLEDs will be achievable, if it is combined with the emission enhancement property in the solid state and the innovative concept about frustrated energy transfer.

Conclusions

In conclusion, an ideal material for future light sources should be cost-effective, stable, robust, emit over the whole visible light region, not suffer from self-absorption, and easily reproducible. 41 With this goal in mind, we have successfully synthesized and characterized emission tuned imidazole -based ESIPT materials, and we comprehensively demonstrated the strategy by extensive experimental results as well as theoretical calculations. Because these spectrally tuned molecules showed intense, reproducible, and stable photoluminescence in the solid state, the molecules are expected to have a significant impact on the development of a new class of displays and light sources.
