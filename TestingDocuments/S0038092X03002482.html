The dye, bis(tetrabutylammonium) cis–bis(thiocyanato)bis(2,2′-bipyridine-4-carboxylic acid, 4′-carboxylate)ruthenium(II), adsorbed to a nanostructured TiO2 film was studied with UV–VIS and IR spectroscopy after being exposed to visual and ultra-violet radiation, increased temperature, air, electrolyte, and water in the electrolyte. The thiocyanate ion ligand is lost in air, at temperatures equal to and above 135 °C, in electrolyte and possibly upon UV irradiation. The loss of the SCN− ligand in air was accelerated under visual illumination. From working electrodes immersed in the electrolyte or in degraded complete solar cells it was observed that the absorption peak from the thiocyanate ion ligand at around 2100 cm−1 had broadened, blue-shifted and decreased. One failure mechanism is thus that the thiocyanate ion ligand is lost from the dye together with the electrolyte. Together with water in the electrolyte (5 v%) the SCN− ligand is exchanged with H2O and/or OH−. The ligand exchange between SCN− and H2O/OH− was accelerated under visual illumination.1IntroductionDye-sensitized solar cells, developed by O’Regan and Grätzel (1991), have achieved solar light-to-electric-energy conversion yields above 10%. The production cost of dye-sensitized solar cell (DSC) devices is believed to be low, due to low material cost and low energy demanding production methods. Stability of the DSC, corresponding to at least 10 years outdoor equivalent operation, has been demonstrated under accelerated illumination tests at 20 °C (Hinsch et al., 2000). Complications may show up, however, at higher temperatures (Hinsch et al., 2001), under UV-exposure (Hinsch et al., 2000), with too much water in the electrolyte (Rijnberg et al., 1998), or if the chemical balancing of the electrolyte components are not correct (Grünwald and Tributsch, 1997; Kohle et al., 1997; Haque et al., 2000).By deliberately treating the samples harshly enough to cause chemical degradation of the solar cell components we have in the present work used UV–VIS and IR spectroscopy to study the factors and mechanisms of degradation in a dye-sensitized solar cell device based on the dye, bis(tetrabutylammonium) cis–bis(thiocyanato)bis(2,2′-bipyridine-4-carboxylic acid, 4′-carboxylate)ruthenium(II), below referred to as N719, adsorbed on TiO2. All treatments and measurements were made under simulated open circuit condition, which has been observed to be the most severe condition for a dye-sensitized solar cell (Gregg et al., 2001).2Experimental section2.1Preparation of the samplesThe TiO2 colloidal solution was prepared by hydrolysis of titanium tetraisopropoxide (O’Regan and Grätzel, 1991). The TiO2 solution was spread on transparent conducting glass sheets (Libbey Owens Ford, fluorine-doped SnO2 glass with a sheet resistance of 8 Ω/square). By use of Scotch tape as frame and spacer, thin films were formed by raking off the excess colloidal solution with a glass rod. The samples were sintered in air at 450 °C for 20 min to form a nanostructured film electrode. The films consisted of 10–14 nm sized particles in diameter. Film thicknesses were determined with a Dektak 3 profilometer and were typically 5 μm. The electrodes were transferred into an argon atmosphere glove box while they were still hot, in order to minimise the amount of surface hydroxyl groups. Dye adsorption of the nanostructured TiO2 electrodes was carried out by soaking the film for 24 h in a 0.5 mM ethanolic (distilled spectral grade, denoted with “∗” or spectral grade ethanol) solution of the ruthenium complex N719. The dye was purchased from Solaronix S.A. (Lausanne, Switzerland). The excess of dye in the TiO2 film was rinsed off with ethanol (spectral grade).The samples were placed in steel cuvettes with fused silica windows. The active areas of the electrodes placed in the cuvettes were typically 1 cm2. In this set-up the electrodes could be exposed to illumination, heat treatment, air, electrolyte and electrolyte with added water. The samples could be continuously analysed with UV–VIS spectroscopy through the fused silica windows. Moreover the steel cuvettes holding the samples could be transported from and back to the argon containing glove box without exposing the samples to air.In the preparation of complete solar cells also a counter electrode was used. The counter electrode consisted of the same conducting glass as described above, in which two holes were drilled for the filling of electrolyte. The counter electrode was platinized by adding H2PtCl6 to the conducting side of the electrode and fired at 380 °C for 10 min. The small amount of platinum, formed in this way on the counter electrode, improves the short circuit current through a catalytic effect of the reduction of the redox couple. The sealing of the solar cells was made with epoxy (Varian Torr Seal for vacuum products). The active areas of the cells were typically 3.2 cm2 (0.8×4 cm2). All samples were held in argon atmosphere except for the experiments in air and with water in the electrolyte.2.2Impacting the samplesThe light source for the visual radiation was a halogen lamp (55 W). A collimated xenon arc lamp (50 W) was the source for the ultra-violet radiation (∼7 W). Fig. 1 shows the spectra of the halogen and xenon lamps. IR radiation from the lamps was removed with infrared-blocking filters (about 1 dm). Some visual radiation from the xenon lamp was removed with a filter absorbing between 400 and 680 nm. Thus, the radiation reaching the samples from the xenon lamp was mainly in the regions 240–400, 680–950 nm and from the halogen lamp 400–950 nm. The intensity of the halogen lamp (without the water filter) was about 1000 W/m2. The intensity of the UV radiation from the xenon lamp was approximately 700 W/m2.The electrodes were exposed to heat treatment in the dark by an oven (Heraus instrument, Kelvitron®t, Series 6000).In the experiments with water in the electrolyte, ∼3 ml electrolyte with 1–5 volume per cent (v%), Milli-Q water was added to the samples in the cuvettes outside the glove box (and therefore these samples were also exposed to air). The electrolyte consisted of 50 mM iodine (I2), 500 mM lithium iodide (LiI) in the solvent 3-methoxypropionitrile (below referred to as 3MPN).When 4-tert-butylpyridine (500 mM) was added to the electrolyte (hereafter denoted by 4TBP), also a few drops of 4TBP were added on the dye-sensitized working electrodes.2.3Electrochemical and spectroscopic measurementsUltra-violet–visible absorption spectra were registered by using a Hewlett Packard 8453 UV–VIS diode array spectrophotometer.The IR spectra were obtained with a Perkin Elmer 2000 FT-IR system with a photoaccoustic detector, MTEC, Photoaccoustics Model 300. The IR measurements covered the range 550–4000 cm−1, with a resolution of 4 cm−1. Twenty scans were averaged.Both the UV–VIS and IR spectra were registered from the working electrodes, i.e. the dye-sensitized titanium dioxide film on the conducting glass substrate. A UV–VIS spectrum of the specific sample under investigation before any treatment served as reference spectrum to the UV–VIS spectrum of the treated sample (with exception of the air-treated samples and the complete solar cells). The UV–VIS reference spectra were registered from freshly prepared samples. An IR reference spectrum is registered from a freshly prepared sample of the same batch as the samples of the particular treatment. Interference patterns appearing from the TiO2 film on the glass substrate can be observed in the IR spectra but do not hamper the observation of new peaks originating from the degradation experiments since these are much more narrow and distinct.The overall light-to-electric energy conversion efficiency measurements were performed with a Light Drive 1000 lamp (type 1400-E2/1) through an infrared-blocking filter. The light intensity was 100 W/m2.2.4Thermal analysisThermogravimetric (TG) analysis was performed using TGA 2050 Thermogravimetric Analyzer, TA instruments, in an atmosphere of either nitrogen or oxygen. Dye-sensitized titanium dioxide powder scraped from the glass substrate was used in the TG analysis.3Results and discussion3.1Samples investigated and the molecular changes observedCharacteristic UV–VIS absorption maxima of the working electrodes, used as reference in this study, are found at ∼400 and ∼550 nm due to two metal to ligand charge transfer transitions (MLCT), see Fig. 2A. In ethanolic solution these MLCT transitions are located at 395 and 535 nm (Nazeeruddin et al., 1999).With IR spectroscopy the characteristic vibrational bands observed (and used as reference), for the thiocyanate ion (SCN−) ligand, were the CN stretching band at 2100 cm−1, for the carboxylic acid group (COOH) the CO stretching band, centred at ∼1715 cm−1 and for the carboxylate ion (COO−) broad bands, centred at ∼1610 and ∼1380 cm−1, corresponding to an antisymmetric and symmetric C–O stretching band, respectively (Fig. 2A). These assignments are in accordance with Finnie et al. (1998). Finnie et al. suggested a bidentate chelate or bridging coordination to the TiO2 surface via two carboxylate groups per dye molecule and also that the two carboxylic acids are likely to be uncoordinated in the adsorbed dye molecule. The band at 1470 cm−1 is assigned to the tetrabutylammonium cation (Bu4N+) co-adsorbed with the dye onto the TiO2 surface (Nazeeruddin et al., 2000). Nazeeruddin et al. (2000) have shown that one tetrabutylammonium cation was adsorbed on the titanium dioxide surface per dye molecule.The different treatments of the working electrodes and molecular changes, observed by UV–VIS and IR spectroscopy, are found in Table 1. The samples in Table 1, for which molecular changes could be observed, will be discussed in the following sections.3.2Working electrodes exposed to UV and VIS light in argon atmosphereWhen dry working electrodes were illuminated with visual light (380 h) or kept in the dark (380 h and 840 h) in an argon atmosphere no molecular changes were observed with UV–VIS or IR spectroscopy, as compared to references. The UV irradiated electrode (160 h) in argon atmosphere showed a slight decrease of the absorbance of the CN stretching band from the thiocyanate ion ligand at 2100 cm−1 compared to that from the reference sample (Fig. 2). From the UV–VIS measurement of the UV-treated sample, a slight blue-shift of one of the absorbance maxima was observed (535 nm) compared to the same sample before the UV irradiation, being the reference (546 nm) (Fig. 2). Weak degradation of the SCN− ligands from UV irradiation is possible. Conceivable products are cyanide ion ligands and H2O ligands which both would give rise to blue-shifted absorption maxima (λmax=493 and 500 nm for Ru-complexes with CN− and H2O as ligands, respectively) (Nazeeruddin et al., 1993).From literature data typical vibrational wave numbers for CN stretching from cyanide ions are found at 2215–2275 cm−1 and stretching and bending vibrations from water molecules are found at 3750–3200 cm−1 (antisymmetric and symmetric stretching) and 1700–1600 cm−1 (symmetric bending). Two small peaks are observed at 2280 and 2310 cm−1 indicating that CN− ligands possibly are being formed when working electrodes are exposed to UV light.3.3Working electrodes exposed to VIS light in airAir degraded the working electrodes. Degradation of the dye in air is probably due to the oxygen and water contents in air. The electrodes exposed to air during 840 h in darkness and under visual illumination lost thiocyanate ion ligands, initially coordinated to the dye, as observed from the diminishing CN stretching band at 2100 cm−1 with IR spectroscopy (Fig. 3). For the electrode being in the dark and exposed to air a small absorption peak at 1578 cm−1 is found, possibly from a symmetric bending band of H2O either bonded to the dye or adsorbed in the nanostructured TiO2 film. These electrodes also showed blue-shifted absorbance maxima with UV–VIS spectroscopy. For the electrode exposed to air in the dark, the absorption maxima were 380 nm and 530 nm compared to 400 and 550 nm for a newly prepared electrode being the reference (Fig. 3). After visual illumination in air both absorption maxima were suppressed and rather featureless. The UV–VIS spectra of the samples exposed to air resemble the UV–VIS spectra of samples exposed to electrolyte with 5 v% water (Fig. 8) discussed in Section 3.7, indicating a ligand exchange between SCN− and H2O and/or OH−. In an earlier study (Moser et al., 1998), the formation of amino complexes by the oxidation of the SCN− ligand was discussed. A decomposition of the SCN− ligands in the presence of strong oxidants is expected with cleavage of the CN bonds yielding amino complexes. No formation of amino groups could be distinguished in Fig. 3 at the typical vibrational wave numbers for NH stretching (3200–3500 cm−1) due to the presence of stretching bands in this region from water molecules, adsorbed in the nanostructured TiO2 film. In another study, (Kohle et al., 1997) sulphur loss was induced in dye-sensitized TiO2 films (the dye cis–bis(thiocyanato)bis(2,2′-bipyridyl-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid)Ru(II)) by visible illumination in air, forming the bis(cyano) complex. Sulphur loss with the formation of cyano ligands has not been observed in the present study as judged from the absence of the typical vibrational wave numbers for CN stretching (2215–2275 cm−1) in Fig. 3B and C.3.4Working electrodes exposed to heat treatmentThermal stability of the dye N719 in free form and adsorbed onto nanostructured TiO2 films, have been studied earlier with TG analysis, differential scanning calorimetry (DSC), IR and UV–VIS spectroscopy (Amirnasr et al., 2000; Nazeeruddin et al., 2000). The processes observed with the thermoanalytical methods were dehydration at ∼35–180 °C, deamination of the counter ion at ∼200–300 °C, decarboxylation at ∼340–380 °C and decomposition starting at ∼380 °C. In the dehydration process water mainly from the pores of TiO2 (mostly adsorbed from the environment) was released. In the deamination process of the tetrabutylammonium cation (NBu4+) decomposition is believed to occur. Higher thermal stability in the decarboxylation and decomposition processes was observed when the dye was adsorbed onto the nanostructured TiO2 as compared to the dye in free form (the temperatures mentioned above are valid for the dye adsorbed onto TiO2). The increased stability against decarboxylation and decomposition was rationalised in terms of the strong interaction between the carboxylate groups and TiO2 (Finnie et al., 1998). Higher thermal stability in the decarboxylation process was also observed for dyes with tetrabutylammonium counter ions instead of protons.The result of the thermogravimetry performed in the present study, mainly agreed with the findings presented by Amirnasr et al. (2000) and Nazeeruddin et al. (2000).According to the spectral data in the present study, the dye N719 adsorbed onto TiO2 did undergo observable chemical change between the temperatures 125–200 °C. From samples exposed to temperatures ⩾125 °C in the dark and in argon atmosphere, a decreased and blue-shifted low energy MLCT transition was observed in the UV–VIS spectra (Fig. 4). It was observed that the samples prepared with distilled spectral grade ethanol were more stable in the heat treatment compared to the samples prepared with spectral grade ethanol as seen from the UV–VIS maxima for the samples exposed to 150, 175 and 200 °C (Table 1). To get further information of molecular changes of the heat-treated working electrodes, IR spectra were registered of the electrodes treated at 135 °C for 48 h, 175 °C for 4 h and 200 °C for 2 h (Fig. 4). A decreased amount of the thiocyanate ion ligand coordinated to the dye could be seen from the decreased intensity of the CN stretching band at 2100 cm−1. A new peak with absorption maximum at 1992 cm−1 was observed for samples heat-treated at 135 °C (48 h), 175 °C (4 h) and 200 °C (2 h) (Fig. 4). This peak was attributed to the CN stretching in a sulphur coordinated thiocyanate ion ligand, Ru–SCN (Grünwald and Tributsch, 1997). A decreased intensity (∼30%) of the CN stretching band at 2107 cm−1 can also bee observed in the study of Amirnasr et al. (2000) for the free dye cis–bis(thiocyanato)bis(2,2′-bipyridine-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid)ruthenium(II) heated to 330 °C compared to the non-heated dye.3.5Working electrodes exposed to electrolyteNo changes of the working electrodes were observed, using IR and UV–VIS spectroscopy, when the electrodes had been immersed in the solvent in darkness or under visual and ultra-violet radiation for 200–300 h in argon atmosphere. An IR spectrum was however not registered for the sample exposed to visual illumination to confirm the observation with UV–VIS spectroscopy.When lithium iodide was added to the solvent on the other hand, a broadening, a blue-shift and decreased intensity of the SCN− ligand peak at 2100 cm−1 was observed in the IR spectra of the samples in the dark and those exposed to visual and ultra-violet radiation for 200–300 h.The relation between the amount of COO− and COOH groups differed for working electrodes being immersed in the solvent only and solvent with salt added. The intensity of the antisymmetric (1610 cm−1) and symmetric C–O stretching band (1380 cm−1) increased as the CO stretching band (1715 cm−1) decreased for working electrodes being immersed in the solvent with added salt compared to both a dry working electrode or a working electrode being immersed in the solvent (see Table 1). A possible explanation to the increased amounts of COO− groups observed in the IR spectra is the exchange from the cations H+ to Li+ in the COOH group. This cation exchange is an interesting topic of itself to study further.The electrodes were then immersed in the whole electrolyte, i.e. with both lithium iodide and iodine added to the solvent, in the dark or visually illuminated for 840 h. A broadening, a decrease of the peak intensity and a blue-shifted CN stretching band around 2100 cm−1, were observed (Fig. 5). The UV–VIS spectra, of these working electrodes, showed blue-shifted absorption maxima. The absorption maximum for the sample being in the dark was at 533 and 527 nm for the sample being visually illuminated, compared to 550 nm for the references (Fig. 5). Mechanisms of the thiocyanate ion ligand exchange reactions between the redox couple iodide/triiodide and the ruthenium complex bis(tetrabutylammonium) cis–bis(thiocyanato)bis(2,2′-bipyridine-4-carboxylic acid, 4′-carboxylate)ruthenium(II), in a sealed dye-sensitized solar cell has been studied earlier using resonance Raman scattering (Greijer et al., 2001). The amount of thiocyanate ion coordinated to the dye, estimated from the CN stretching observed at 2105 cm−1, decreased with increased amount of iodine added or triiodide formed in the electrolyte. It was suggested that a complex between I2 and SCN− was formed in the solar cell. Recently, however, we concluded that I2 SCN− is formed only to a small extent and that triiodide (I3−) was found to exchange the SCN− ligand of the dye (Greijer Agrell et al., in preparation).Improvement of the long-term stability due to the addition of 4TBP has been reported (Rijnberg et al., 1998; Kern et al., 2000). It has also been suggested (Greijer et al., 2001) that the additive 4TBP improves the stability of the solar cell by suppressing the loss of the SCN− ligand from the dye. It was recently proposed that an ion pair Li+…I3− formation occurred which by a reduced electrostatic repulsion between I3− and SCN− facilitated the exchange of these anions at Ru(II) of the dye (Greijer Agrell et al., in preparation). The additive 4TBP is believed to suppress the SCN−/I3− ligand exchange by forming complex with Li+.The role of the additive 4TBP was studied in the present work with IR and UV–VIS measurements. No difference between working electrodes immersed in the electrolyte with and without the additive 4TBP for 70 h in the dark was observed with UV–VIS spectroscopy. The UV–VIS spectra, of treated working electrodes, showed blue-shifted absorption maxima (Fig. 6). The absorption maxima for the treated working electrodes were at 382 and 531 nm (without 4TBP) and at 383 and 533 nm (with 4TBP) compared to 400 and 550 nm for the reference samples.The only difference observed with IR spectroscopy was the lower intensity of the CO stretching band at 1715 cm−1 for the sample exposed to electrolyte with 4TBP (6D) as compared to without (6C). Deprotonation of the carboxylic acid ligand by 4TBP could explain this observation. A broadening, a decrease of the peak intensity and a blue-shifted CN stretching band around 2100 cm−1, were observed to the same extent for both samples immersed in the electrolyte with and without the additive 4TBP as compared to dry working electrodes being references (Fig. 6).It can be noted that the working electrode in the cuvette is exposed to a large volume of electrolyte compared to the electrolyte volume of a sealed solar cell device. Curing effects, e.g. the possibility that SCN− species present in the electrolyte due to ligand losses may recouple to the dye, will therefore not be present when the cuvette is used since the SCN− species will diffuse out to the relatively large volume of electrolyte bath in the cuvette. Moreover the amount of I3− is much higher in the cuvette experiments than in the real solar cell, which could mean that we so far have observed a larger degradation rate than can be expected for the actual device.3.6Working electrodes from solar cells exposed to VIS lightTo investigate complete solar cell devices (with real electrolyte volumes), the current–voltage characteristics were measured when the solar cells were newly prepared and after the solar cells had been illuminated or been in the dark for 840 h (in argon atmosphere). Thereafter the working electrodes from the opened solar cells were studied with UV–VIS and IR spectroscopy. The solar cells were prepared with and without 4TBP.From the current–voltage curves, we concluded that stability of the solar cells was not achieved. The measurements showed, however, that solar cells prepared with 4TBP, had better current–voltage characteristics as compared to without, both when the solar cells were newly prepared and after some weeks either in the dark or under visual illumination.From the UV–VIS and IR measurements of these unstable solar cells, we observed the same trend as for the working electrodes exposed to electrolyte in the cuvettes (Section 3.5). From the IR spectra (Fig. 7) a broadening, blue-shifted and a decreased intensity of the CN stretching band around 2100 cm−1 was observed as compared to a reference sample.The addition of 4TBP seemed to slightly improve the stability of the SCN− ligands in solar cells, as seen from the IR spectra. The intensity of the CN stretching band was slightly higher for solar cells prepared with 4TBP (D, E) as compared to without (B, C). The small indifference between the samples with and without the additive 4TBP as compared to earlier findings (Greijer et al., 2001) could possibly be due to the intense illumination with visual radiation in resonance Raman scattering or its inherent selectivity due to the resonance mechanism resulting in enhancement of groups being coupled to the dye. In contrast, the IR absorption from SCN−, detached from the dye but incompletely rinsed off, can still be seen.The UV–VIS spectra, of the working electrodes, showed blue-shifted absorption maxima (Fig. 7). The absorption from the high energy MLCT, were featureless. The absorption maxima from the low energy MLCT were positioned at 520 nm. Deprotonation of the dye could be the cause of the blue-shifted absorption maximum. A fully deprotonated dye of N719 such as tetrakis(tetrabutylammonium) cis–bis(thiocyanato)bis(2,2′-bipyridine-4,4′-dicarboxylate)ruthenium(II), in ethanolic solution shows an absorption maximum at 518 nm (Nazeeruddin et al., 1999). It can however be concluded from the presence of a weak CO stretching band at ∼1730 cm−1 in the IR spectra (Fig. 7) that the dye is not fully deprotonated.In conclusion, from the current–potential characteristics, long-term stable solar cells were not achieved and this instability could be related to the decrease of SCN− ligands on the dye. It can be noted, that in the IR measurements the electrodes are exposed to air. This exposure to air does not affect the SCN− ligand however. Freshly made working electrodes immersed and not immersed in the solvent 3MPN are treated in the same way without any observed loss of the SCN− ligand.3.7Working electrodes exposed to electrolyte with added waterWhen working electrodes were immersed in the electrolyte containing 5 volume per cent Milli-Q water, in the dark or visually illuminated, a broadening and decreased intensity from the SCN− ligand absorption at around 2100 cm−1 was observed as compared to a reference sample in the IR spectra (Fig. 8). At 1575 cm−1 a small absorption peak was found, possibly from symmetric bending of H2O, bonded to the dye or adsorbed in the nanostructured TiO2 film. The UV–VIS spectra (Fig. 8) of these electrodes showed blue-shifted absorption maxima; 529 and 500 nm for the electrodes in the dark and visually illuminated, respectively, compared to 551 nm for a reference. The same trend was observed for electrodes immersed in water containing electrolyte without iodine.It is probable that the thiocyanate ion ligands of the dye are exchanged with H2O and/or OH− ligands. Nazeeruddin et al. (1993) showed earlier that the cis–bis(aquo)bis(2,2′-bipyridine-4,4′-dicarboxylate)ruthenium(II) complex in ethanolic solution has one of its absorption maxima at 500 nm. A red shift of the absorption band from 500 nm to 528 nm was observed upon going from neutral to basic solution (pH 12) and is assigned to deprotonation of the H2O ligand (Nazeeruddin et al., 1997).4ConclusionThe thiocyanate ion ligand seems to be the most sensitive part of the dye, bis(tetrabutylammonium) cis–bis(thiocyanato)bis(2,2′-bipyridine-4-carboxylic acid, 4′-carboxylate)ruthenium(II), regarding degradation when adsorbed onto TiO2 in the dye-sensitized solar cell. The gradual disappearance of the SCN− ligand, as seen from the diminished intensity of the CN stretching band in the IR spectra is strongly correlated with the decrease or shift in the UV–VIS absorption bands. The ligand is lost in air, at temperatures equal to and above 135 °C, in electrolyte and possibly upon UV irradiation. The loss of the SCN− ligand in air was accelerated under visual illumination. In a complete solar cell, triiodide (I3−) was found to exchange the SCN− ligand of the dye (Greijer Agrell et al., in preparation). Together with water in the electrolyte the thiocyanate ion ligand is exchanged with H2O/OH−. The loss of the SCN− ligand with subsequent exchange with H2O was accelerated under visual illumination.To prevent degradation in a dye-sensitized solar cell (using the dye N719), the device needs to be sealed to exclude air and water. Also temperatures equal to and above 125 °C and UV exposure should be avoided. Moreover, since the SCN− ligand in the dye is believed to play an important role in the reduction of the oxidised dye (Rensmo et al., 1997), it would be desirable to find a redox couple, which would not exchange the thiocyanate ion ligand.For the production of dye-sensitized solar cells, it would be preferable if the dye was stable in the presence of air and water.AcknowledgmentsThis work was financially supported by the Swedish Natural Science Research Council (NFR), the Swedish Research Council for Engineering Sciences (TFR), the Swedish Foundation for Strategic Environmental Research (MISTRA) and the Swedish National Energy Administration (Energimyndigheten).ReferencesAmirnasr et al., 2000M.AmirnasrMd.K.NazeeruddinM.GrätzelThermal stability of cis-dithiocyanato(2,2′-bipyridyl 4,4′dicarboxylate)ruthenium(II) photosensitizer in the free form and on nanostructured TiO2 filmsThermochim.
