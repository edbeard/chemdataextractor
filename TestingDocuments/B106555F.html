A detailed mechanism is developed for the OH-initiated atmospheric oxidation of Î±-pinene in the presence of NO x , based solely on quantitative structureâactivity relationships and on theoretical quantum chemistry methods, combined with transition state theory calculations or RRKM master equation analyses. Thus, on objective theoretical grounds, the fate of some 40 organic ( oxy ) radical intermediates is predicted. Addition of OH onto the monoterpene double bond accounts for â¼90% of the reaction and leads to chemically activated P1OH â  (â¼44%) and P2OH â  (â¼44%) radicals. The subsequent chemistry of these radicals is described, and the quantitative importance of the pathways leading to first-generation products such as pinonaldehyde, acetone , formaldehyde , formic acid and nitrates is discussed. H-atom abstraction by OH from Î±-pinene is a minor route (â¼12%) but contributes importantly to the overall yield of formaldehyde . Some specific effects relating to the hyperconjugation-stabilization of terpene -derived (bi)cyclic radicals are discussed.

Introduction

The global-scale dominance of biogenic hydrocarbon emissions to the atmosphere over anthropogenic ones has now been largely recognized. Global emissions of the volatile monoterpenes (C 10 ) have been estimated 1 to be â140 MT yr â1 , and are thus comparable to total anthropogenic emissions of non-methane hydrocarbons .

Amongst the monoterpenes , Î±-pinene is ubiquitous and is often observed to have the highest emission rates as well as to be the most abundant. 2â7 In the Mediterranean region, field measurements show that, in many of the ecosystems studied, monoterpene emissions significantly exceed those of isoprene . 8â11 The dominance of Î±-pinene emissions over the other monoterpenes and isoprene has also been observed in boreal regions of Northern Europe. 6,7 Î±-Pinene is highly reactive towards ozone , OH and NO 3 radicals, with a rate constant for the OH reaction of 5.4 Ã 10 â11 cm 3 molecule â1 s â1 at 298 K, 12 implying an average tropospheric lifetime with respect to this oxidation process of only 2.6 h. 12,13

Several laboratory studies on the oxidation of Î±-pinene initiated by the OH radical are reported in the literature. 14â23 While the reaction rate constants of these oxidation processes are reasonably well known, 12 the reaction products and their yields, and especially the reaction mechanisms, are still insufficiently known or understood. One of the major divergences regarding the reported molar yields of the Î±-pinene + OH reaction concerns the yield of pinonaldehyde, the values of which range from 0.06 21 through 0.28 16 to 0.87. 18 In general, the yields of pinonaldehyde from Î±-pinene derived in the studies by Arey et al. 14 and Hakola et al. 16 are significantly lower than those reported by Hatakeyama and co-workers 15 and more recently by NoziÃ¨re et al. 18 These divergences might be related to the different experimental methods used and might be ascribed in part to the fact that FTIR spectoscopic analyses tend to overestimate yields due to the contribution of other carbonyls. More recently, Larsen and co-workers 21 have reported a very low pinonaldehyde yield (6% Â± 2%), but they explain that this difference might be the result of the strong oxidative conditions of their experiments.

While the mechanisms involved in simpler alkene + OH reactions are fairly well understood, 12 those involved in (mono)terpene oxidation processes present a different and more complex picture and are still not well characterized. Moreover, there is still a lack of theoretical information to support the validity of the few proposed mechanisms. Recently, we performed a theoretical analysis of the OH-initiated oxidation of Î±-pinene to elucidate the mechanism of acetone formation. 24 Dibble 25 very recently reported a theoretical study of the Î² CâC bond scission reactions of the alkoxyl radicals resulting from the Î±-pinene + OH reaction. A scheme for the degradation of Î±-pinene has also been incorporated into the master chemical mechanism MCM. 26,27

However, several sub-mechanisms in the OH-initiated oxidation of Î±-pinene still need to be clarified, among others the nature and quantitative importance of the pathways leading to products such as acetone , formaldehyde and formic acid .

In this paper, we develop a detailed and quantitative chemical mechanism for first-generation product formation from Î±-pinene + OH under (simulated) atmospheric conditions, based solely on either objective chemical kinetics knowledge, or quantitative structureâactivity relationships (SARs), or from first principles, i.e. ab initio DFT (density functional theory) calculated barrier heights, in combination with proven statistical rate theories [transition state theory and RRKM theory, for some cases in conjunction with master equation analysis ( ME ) using our URESAM program package]. 28 Total yields of products such as pinonaldehyde, formaldehyde , acetone , formic acid and nitrates were finally derived by propagating the relevant product fractions of each step in the mechanism. Where competing reactions are possible, our estimates are subject to uncertainties, as detailed in the next section.

Quantitative mechanism development methodologies

(a) Quantitative structureâactivity relationships

As one of our prime mechanism construction tools, we made extensive use of quantitative structureâactivity relationships (SARs). The SARs that were brought to bear here will be discussed at length in a separate paper. 29 The SARs employed in this work comprise: (i) our site-specific SAR for addition of OH to (poly)alkenes; 30,31 (ii) a recently proposed mini SAR for H-atom abstraction from (biogenic) (poly)alkenes; 32 (iii) elements of a SAR for specific radical stabilization effects in bicyclic terpenic structures, in particular strain-enhanced hyperconjugation; 29,33 (iv) a generalized SAR for barrier heights to dissociation of (OH- and/or oxo -substituted) alkoxyl radicals; (v) a generalized SAR for isomerization of such oxy radicals by 1,5-H shifts; and (vi) the recently revised SAR for the formation of nitrates in reactions of (substituted) alkylperoxyl radicals with NO. 34

Here, a brief description of the generalized SARs (iv) and (v) for oxy radicals will be given, as the fate of these radicals is generally of pivotal importance in the oxidation mechanisms of VOCs, in particular for complex compounds such as the terpenes . In general, oxy radicals may undergo various Î² CâC scission reactions, various 1,5-H migration reactions, and reaction with O 2 , all possibly competing with one another. The reactions of oxy radicals with O 2 appear to have rate constants in a narrow range, 12,35 resulting in a rate of (4â5) Ã 10 4 s â1 in ambient conditions. On the other hand, the rates of the oxy dissociation and isomerization reactions can in principle cover the entire range of â 1 s â1 to 10 11 or even 10 13 s â1 , depending on the barrier height and, for the 1,5-H shifts, on the number of degrees of freedom of internal rotation lost in the transition structure and the concomitant reduction in the density of internal states. As a result of this very wide possible range of some twelve orders of magnitude, one can generally expect that, for large oxy radical structures, one particular reaction will be one or more orders of magnitude faster than all others, and hence be the single reaction of any significance. The major factor determining the rates of Î² CâC fissions and 1,5-H migrations will be the barrier heights. (b) Computational methods and details

Quantum chemical geometry optimizations and frequency analyses were performed at the B3LYP-DFT/6-31G(d,p) level of theory to obtain accurate energetic and rovibrational data on the intermediates and transition states in the mechanism. Each geometry is fully optimized, as verified by a frequency analysis, and the relative energies are corrected for zero-point vibrational energy (ZPE). The B3LYP-DFT/6-31G(d,p) level of theory has already been validated in earlier studies on open shell systems, 40,41,47,48 for which our DFT results differed on average by about 1 kcal mol â1 from high-level ab initio calculations, and most often reproduced experimental barrier heights within 1 kcal mol â1 . For 1,5-H shifts involving an oxy function, a reaction type not considered in the earlier publications, we assessed the accuracy of the B3LYP-DFT/6-31G(d,p) method by characterizing the 1-butoxyl radical and its 1,5-H shift transition state leading to 4-hydroxybutyl, for which the ZPE-corrected B3LYP-DFT/6-31G(d,p) barrier of 8.2 kcal mol â1 is in excellent agreement with the experimental 12 Arrhenius activation energy of 8.4 kcal mol â1 . DFT-specific problems encountered in some of these reactions have been discussed earlier. 24,32,33 Detailed reports of the quantum chemical results are given in the Electronic Supplementary Information. â  Note that in the following sections B3LYP-DFT/6-31G(d,p) will be abbreviated as B3LYP.

All quantum chemical calculations were performed with the Gaussian-98 program suite, 49 while the RRKM calculations and master equation analyses for obtaining temperature- and pressure-dependent product distributions were performed using our URESAM computer program. 28 The energy-specific unimolecular rate constants are calculated from our quantum chemical rovibrational data, using RRKM theory in the harmonic oscillator approximation. Nascent energy distributions of the chemically activated intermediates are obtained by microreversibility considerations assuming MaxwellâBoltzmann energy distributions for the separated reactants. Thermal rate constants are calculated using transition state theory (further denoted as TST) from our calculated B3LYP energy barriers and rotationalâvibrational parameters, or from SAR-predicted barrier heights and frequency factors. 29 For a more detailed description of the computational methods we refer to earlier publications. 24,32

Forming a radical by removing an H atom from the âgâ carbon atom therefore requires more energy, leading to a bond strength of about 102 kcal mol â1 , i.e. even higher than the normal primary D (CâH) of 99.5 kcal mol â1 , and 22 kcal mol â1 higher than the CâH bond strength on the âdâ position. 32 Therefore, it is highly unlikely that the H-atom abstraction from the âgâ C atom of Î±-pinene can be of any significance and competitive with the three much more facile H-abstraction pathways discussed earlier in this section.

The main channel of the reaction between Î±-pinene and OH radicals, accounting for â¼90%, is OH addition on the double bond, leading to two chemically activated radicals, P1OH â  and P2OH â  (see Figs. 6 and 11 , respectively). 24 Formally, according to the site-specific SAR for alkene + OH reactions, 29â31 the branching ratio P1OH : P2OH , i.e. a âtertiaryâ versus a secondary Î²-hydroxyalkoxyl radical, is expected to be 65 : 35. However, in the P1OH radical, there cannot be an efficient hyperconjugation of the C b radical site toward the C g substituent (see Fig. 1 for the atom numbering), which is actually a cyclobutyl -type moiety that makes up (in twofold) one half of the terpenic six-membered ring. As the C b radical center in P1OH is constrained by the six-membered ring, it cannot align its half-occupied p orbital with any of the sp 3 orbitals on C g , the respective axes making angles of about 45Â°, which does not permit an effective overlap. Therefore, as far as hyperconjugation-stabilization is concerned, P1OH should be considered as almost a secondary radical, not significantly more stable than the secondary P2OH adduct radical. This novel insight 29 also rationalizes the âlowâ rate constant of the Î±-pinene + OH reaction. 53 Indeed, our above-referenced SAR for OH-addition predicts a total k OH of 6.0 Ã 10 â11 cm 3 molecule â1 s â1 for addition to an alkene with two secondary sites, in fair agreement with the experimental value 12 of 5.4 Ã 10 â11 cm 3 molecule â1 s â1 . It has been recently proposed by Orlando and co-workers 20 that radicals of the type RâC Ë HOH, where R is a long organic chain, might also add O 2 instead of only undergoing an H abstraction, and lead to HCOOH elimination.

There is ample evidence that the reaction between Î±-hydroxyalkyl radicals and O 2 to form an aldehyde and HO 2 is not a direct abstraction reaction. The high rate coefficients of RCHOH + O 2 â RCHO + HO 2 reactions (R H or CH 3 ) and their small temperature dependence, similar to those for O 2 addition to alkyl radicals, 12 as well as the product distribution results on CH 2 OH + O 2 at lower temperatures, 54 strongly indicate that reactions of Î±-hydroxyalkyl radicals with O 2 proceed via an activated RCH(OH)O 2 â  peroxyl radical intermediate, which rapidly and promptly isomerizes and then decomposes to aldehyde and HO 2 . At atmospheric pressure and for a large R, however, a sizeable fraction of such radicals should also undergo collisional stabilization.

Such stabilized RCH(OH)O 2 peroxyl radicals also decompose thermally to aldehyde + HO 2 at room temperature, 55,56 via a hydrogen-bonded OOHâ¯OCHR intermediate, 57 with activation energies of about 13â14 kcal mol â1 , and with a rate coefficient k (1 atm) of 150 s â1 for CH 2 (OH)O 2 , 55 and of 1870 s â1 for CH 3 CH(OH)O 2 , 56 the latter likely close to its high-pressure limit.

For the reactions of larger Î±-hydroxyalkyl radicals as derived from RO23 (or RO28 , see later) with O 2 , one therefore expects a large fraction of collisionally stabilized Î±-hydroxyalkylperoxyl radicals, as well as the prompt production of pinonaldehyde and HO 2 . Conclusions

In this work, for the first time to our knowledge, a detailed mechanism for the OH-initiated oxidation of Î±-pinene was developed and (first-generation) product yields were quantified, based entirely on objective chemical (kinetics) knowledge, i.e. validated SARs, ab initio DFT energy barriers and reaction energies, and proven statistical rate methodologies. Our theory-based mechanism includes the chemistry subsequent to the H-atom abstraction pathways as well as that of the more important OH-addition steps forming the chemically activated P1OH and P2OH adducts.

The competing isomerization and dissociation reactions of the critical intermediates involved in the latter reactions were also investigated, in an effort to provide theoretical grounds for verifying the validity of the few already proposed, but often not exhaustive, Î±-pinene + OH reaction sub-mechanisms.

H-atom abstraction in Î±-pinene appears to occur mainly on carbon atoms labelled âdâ and (âi,jâ) : Î±-pinen-d-yl radicals are allyl-resonance stabilized, which results in an enhancement of the rate of H-abstraction on this site; while Î±-pinen-i(j)-yl radicals are more stable than expected due to ring-strain-enhanced hyperconjugation.

The H-abstraction routes are determined to be important particularly with respect to CH 2 O formation, which is formed in high yields from these minor pathways.

The main reaction channel in the OH-initiated oxidation of Î±-pinene in the presence of NO is that of OH-addition onto the endocyclic CâC double bond, forming the two chemically activated Î±-hydroxy radical adducts P1OH â  and P2OH â  , in near-equal yields as they should show a similar hyperconjugation-stabilization. The high internal energy of these radicals opens for P1OH â  a competing route which is not accessible to the thermally stabilized adduct P1OH . The chemically activated P1OH â  can isomerize to the 6-hydroxymenthen-8-yl radical, finally yielding acetone in a fairly large amount. On the other hand, the stabilized P1OH and P2OH adducts are the precursors of pinonaldehyde, via pathways involving, sequentially, Î²-hydroxyalkoxyl and Î±-hydroxyalkylperoxyl radicals that in real atmospheric conditions are quasi-free of competition (except for nitrate production). For laboratory conditions, however, the Î±-hydroxyalkylperoxyl reaction step leading to pinonaldehyde must compete with a second pathway not expected to occur in the real atmosphere. The underlying cause is that the reaction between Î±-hydroxyalkyl radicals and O 2 is not a direct abstraction reaction, but rather appears to proceed via an activated peroxyl radical intermediate, a sizeable fraction of which, at atmospheric pressure and for larger radicals, can be collisionally stabilized. In normal atmospheric conditions, such stabilized radicals mainly undergo thermal decomposition to yield an aldehyde and HO 2 . However, in laboratory conditions with high NO levels, the thermalized Î±-hydroxyalkylperoxyl radicals react more rapidly with NO to form Î±-hydroxyalkoxyl radicals, which will result in âlaboratoryâ products such as HC(O)OH that are not produced at the much lower NO levels of the real atmosphere.

It should be emphasized that, wherever there are competing reactions or channels, our objective estimates of the fractions are of course subject to uncertainties. Cases in point are : (i) the initial H-abstraction fractions, with probable errors of a factor of 2; (ii) the P1OH : P2OH ratio, which might deviate significantly from the unity ratio adopted here; and (iii) the fraction of P1OH â  undergoing prompt isomerization, which we evaluated at 50% Â± 15%. As pointed out in the methodologies section, probable errors are expected to be much larger for the branching ratios of competing ( oxy ) radical reactions, as the precision of SAR-predicted and B3LYP-computed barriers is only â¼1 kcal mol â1 , entailing probable errors on the rate constants of a factor of â¼5; such errors are most likely responsible for the (limited) deviations of our predicted product yields from measured yields. However, it should be noted that for the major oxidation paths, i.e. the routes from P1OH and P2OH to pinonaldehyde, competing paths are either negligible, or quantifiable with a fair precision.

In conclusion, we believe that in this work we have addressed the great majority of the possible degradation routes in the OH-initiated oxidation of Î±-pinene, and have quantified most of the possible routes, using the best theoretical methodologies that can currently be brought to bear on such systems. The good agreement with the experimental observations reflects the soundness of our present undestanding of VOC oxidation chemistry, and bears witness to the potential of state-of-the-art theoretical methodologies in this area.
