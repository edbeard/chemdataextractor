Abstract

In this study, several sulfonamide derivatives, 4-(2-methylacetylamino)benzenesulfonamides were synthesized. Chemical structures of the derivatives were characterized by 1 H NMR, 13 C NMR, LC–MS–MS, UV–Vis, FTIR, photoluminescence and elemental analysis. Sulfanilamide was reacted with 2-bromopropionyl bromide, in the presence of pyridine, to form bromo-substituted sulfonamide key intermediates, which were subsequently treated with secondary amines to obtain novel sulfonamide derivatives. All the synthesized compounds were evaluated for in vitro antimicrobial activities and cytotoxicity. Increases in ring size, and rings bearing a nitrogen heteroatom led to improvements in antimicrobial activities. As the presence of CA IX and CA XII enzymes have been implicated in some cancerous tumors, the studies presented herein focuses on targeting these enzymes. It was found that the synthesized derivatives had in vitro anti-cancer properties, where compounds ( 3 – 6 ) were found to be active against all cancerous cells, and no cytotoxic effects on normal cells were observed.

1. Introduction

It is well documented that the zinc-metalloenzyme carbonic anhydrase (CA, EC 4.2.1.1) catalyzes the interconversion of carbon dioxide and water to bicarbonate, with H + ions generated in the reaction process; thus this reaction plays a crucial role in the pH regulation of many tissues, organs and organisms. To date there are six genetically different CA families being widespread in living organisms. In humans, fifteen different CA isoforms have been described so far and are involved in many physiological processes, such as, pH regulation, secretion of electrolytes, biosynthetic processes, tumorigenesis, etc. CA inhibition and activation are well known mechanisms, where CA type inhibitors bind to metal centers, while activators plug the assess to active site cavities [1] ; [2] ; [3] ; [4] ; [5] ; [6] ; [7] .

The importance of the CA isoform hCA has been exhibited by a wide number of physiological processes, where abnormally high amounts, or activities of this enzyme have often been linked to various ill human conditions. Recently, numerous hCA isozyme types have been targeted for the strategic design and development of improved inhibitors, or activators for biomedical applications [8] ; [9] ; [10] ; [11] ; [12] .

Sulfonamides, known for inhibiting the metalloenzyme carbonic anhydrase, have been widely investigated due to their attractive use as drugs in the treatment of diseases, and represent an important class of pharmaceutical compounds with a wide spectrum of biological activities, such as, anticancer, CA inhibitory effects, antibacterial, antimalarial, antitumor, antihypertensive, anti-inflammatory and antiprotozoal activities, among others [13] ; [14] ; [15] ; [16] ; [17] ; [18] ; [19] . Further, numerous sulfonamides have been found to act as antitumor agents via CA inhibition, where one such derivative is currently under evaluation in Phase I clinical trials as an anticancer/antimetastatic agent [20] ; [21] ; [22] ; [23] ; [24] . The literature shows two CA isozymes, specifically CA IX and CA XII being prominently connected with, and profoundly expressed, in many tumor states [25] ; [26] . The immense value of sulfonamide drugs was noted upon the report of the first antibacterial drug sulfonylamide [27] ; [28] . The information in the literature supports the fact that sulfonamides were used as the first effective chemotherapeutic agents for the systematic prevention, and cure of human and animal bacterial infections [29] ; [30] . Subsequently, formulation improvements in an effort to achieve greater effectiveness and lower toxicity have led to the synthesis of a multitude of compounds, bearing the sulfanilamide scaffold.

To the best of our knowledge, there are no previous studies in the literature on the sulfonamide derivatives presented herein. In this study, new 4-(2-methylacetamide)benzenesulfonamide derivatives were synthesized and screened for in vitro cytotoxicity, and antimicrobial activities for assisting in the development of drug alternatives of potentially low toxicity.

2. Results and discussion

2.1. Reaction syntheses for 4-(2-methylacetylamino)benzenesulfonamide derivatives ( 3 – 6 )

The aim of this study was to synthesize novel sulfonamide derivatives ( 3 – 6 ). Sulfanilamide ( 1 ) was reacted with 2-bromopropionyl bromide, in the presence of pyridine, to give the bromo-substituted amide key intermediate ( 2 ), which was then treated with secondary amines, such as, pyrrolidine for the synthesis of ( 3 ), piperazine for ( 4 ), 1-methylpiperazine for ( 5 ), and 4-methylpiperidine for ( 6 ) in dry THF, in separate reactions, to obtain the desired derivatives ( 3 – 6 ). The synthesized derivatives ( 3 – 6 ) were obtained as white solid products that were stable at room temperature.

2.2. Antimicrobial activity

Antimicrobial susceptibility testing may be carried out by a number of methods. In regard to the studies presented herein, where the substrates were sulfonamides, two common methods are the dihydropteroate synthase (DHPS) inhibitory and agar disk diffusion techniques. Sulfonamides are known to be competitive inhibitors of DHPS, the enzyme present in microbial cells [32] ; [33] , and the inhibition of DHPS results in damaging effects that lead to eventual microbial cell death. Thus DHPS assays may be carried out for the sulfonamides used in this study, where possible and plausible explanations for observed activities could be due to the ability or inability of these compounds to bind to the active sites. However for our study, the agar disk diffusion technique was used, and growth inhibition measurements were carried out, and the observed antimicrobial activities were discussed as below.

The results of antimicrobial screening are given in Table 1 . The synthesized compounds revealed antimicrobial activity with 11–13 mm inhibition zones. No activity was observed against fungi C. albicans and S. cerevisiae . Synthesized compounds ( 3 ) and ( 6 ) revealed antimicrobial properties, and compounds ( 4 ) and ( 5 ) were inactive against all the microorganisms. The greatest activities observed were for compound ( 3 ) and ( 6 ) against E. coli (12 mm, compound 3 ), and P. aeruginosa and Y. lipolytica (13 mm, compound 6 ) microorganisms. The antimicrobial activities of parent compound ( 1 ) were comparable to those of synthesized compound ( 3 ), exhibiting the highest activity against B. Subtilis (12 mm); further the parent compound ( 1 ) was active against a greater number of microbial lines than compound ( 3 ).

Pyrrolidine, piperazine and piperidine are nitrogen heterocycles having structural motifs, common to many natural and synthetic bioactive compounds. Nitrogen atoms may provide favorable electrostatic interactions between its protonated form and the anionic region of microbial cell components, and thus lead to the eventual disruption of cell processes. These structural units are frequently used in medicines, and were exploited herein. Variations of substituent on the nitrogen atom heterocycles can have an important effect on selectivity and potency against biological targets. Compound ( 3 ) has a pyrrolidinyl substituent, while ( 4 ) and ( 5 ) both have piperazinyl substituents, and ( 6 ) has a piperidinyl substituent. Introduction of a second nitrogen atom on a nitrogen heterocycle was expected to enhance antimicrobial activity, but the contrary, where no activity was observed for both two-nitrogen heterocycles, piperazinyl systems ( 4 ) and ( 5 ). Nitrogen atom substitution with a methyl group as for compound ( 5 ), compared to compound ( 4 ) with no nitrogen substituent, can be expected to reduce the polarity and increase lipophilicity, allowing for better permeation through microbial cell membranes. This indicates that a single nitrogen atom for these heterocycles is the most optimal structure. Further, heterocycle size plays role in antimicrobial activities where the piperidinyl substituted derivative ( 6 ) had slightly greater activity than the pyrrolidinyl substituted derivative ( 3 ). It is possible that the nitrogen atom is better facilitated for the larger ring in antimicrobial interactions. Also, increases in ring size leads to enhanced lipophilic character, and thus better permeation through microbial cell membranes. Derivative ( 6 ) was observed as having the greatest antimicrobial activity. Derivative ( 6 ) bears the optimal six-member nitrogen heterocycle having polar features that are masked by two methyl appendages, and thus bearing improved lipophilicity.

Many studies have shown that the addition of a halogen substituent resulted in improved antimicrobial activities. Studies herein however, showed that the halogen containing compound ( 2 ) had antimicrobial activity, but lower than the heterocycle derivatives ( 3 ) and ( 6 ). Further, only the halogen containing compound ( 2 ) and heterocycle derivatives ( 3 ) and ( 6 ), together with the precursor compound ( 1 ) displayed antifungal activity.

2.3. Cytotoxic activity

Cytotoxic activity of the synthesized compounds were evaluated using six cancer cell lines together with two normal cell lines using the MTT assay technique. It can be seen from Table 2 , that all the compounds exhibited moderate to good cytotoxicity against the HeLa cell type, but showed weak cytotoxicity against other cancer cell lines. Compounds ( 3 – 6 ) were active against HeLa cells, with IC 50 values of the range 85–124 μM, and all other cancer cell lines had IC 50 values >250 μM. Further, the synthesized compounds did not show remarkable cytotoxic effects on the normal cells. 5-Fluorouracil (5-FU), a commonly used drug, served as the positive control for evaluating the chemotherapeutical effects. It was observed that the synthesized compounds ( 3 – 6 ) had activity comparable to that of 5-FU. While the CA IX enzyme is responsible for cancerous states, then it may be affirmed that this enzyme was most abundant in the HeLa cell types, such that compounds ( 3 – 6 ) revealed CA IX inhibitory characteristics. As recent literature studies have revealed the presence of CA IX enzymes in some cancerous tumors [25] ; [26] , the synthesized compounds ( 3 – 6 ) presented in this study showed in vitro anti-cancer properties. Moreover, studies by Nórgádi showed high levels of CA enzyme present in HeLa cells [34] , and that expression of this CA IX enzyme in the HeLa cells appeared to be dependent on cell densities; however, work by Cianchi et al. and Pastorekova et al. showed that the other cells did not have any, or had insignificant amounts of this enzyme [35] ; [36] . Thus, this may be the reason why the synthesized compounds ( 3 – 6 ) demonstrated high cytotoxic activities on HeLa cells. Further, the literature has shown aromatic sulfonamides having CA enzyme inhibitory activities [9] ; [10] ; [11] ; [31] .

Lipophilicity determines the overall bioavailability of compounds as they cross biological membranes, and the attribute of lipophilicity is logP, the octanol/water partition coefficient. Highly lipophilic compounds have improved cell membrane permeability.

It can be seen from Table 3 , the synthesized piperazinyl derivatives ( 4 ) and ( 5 ) had the greatest lipophilicity values, however parent compound ( 1 ) had a higher lipophilicity value than ( 5 ). Hence, interestingly, derivatives ( 4 ) and ( 5 ) displayed the lowest antimicrobial activities, both compounds revealing no antimicrobial activity against any of the microorganisms; however, parent compound ( 1 ) had antimicrobial activities which were similar to the derivatives bearing lower lipophilicities. All compounds tested revealed the absence of activity against the yeast type S. cerevisiae .

The dipole moment of parent compound ( 1 ) was higher than the dipole moment of derivative ( 4 ), and dipole moment of derivative ( 6 ) was comparable that of derivative ( 5 ) and greater than that of derivative ( 4 ). The results of the dipole moments did not allow for correlations with the antimicrobial activities observed.

A comparison of the structures of five-membered heterocycle ( 3 ) with the other six-membered heterocycles ( 4 ), ( 5 ) and ( 6 ) may account for the differences in the observed antimicrobial activities. The bond angle around the nitrogen atom in the five membered heterocycle ( 3 ) is larger and of trigonal geometry, and thus implicates sp 2 characteristics. However, the bond angle around the nitrogen atom in the six membered heterocycles ( 4 ), ( 5 ) and ( 6 ) is substantially lower and more inclined to adopt tetrahedral geometries, and thus suggest sp 3 characteristics. It is known that sp 2 hybridized atoms are more electron-withdrawing than their sp 3 counterparts. This can therefore increase polarities, reflected as dipole moments, and hence can reduce lipophilicities. The reductions in lipophilicities can thus lead to reduced antimicrobial activities, as observed for derivative ( 3 ) compared to derivatives ( 4 ), ( 5 ) and ( 6) .

The structure of derivative ( 6 ) allows it to exhibit good lipophilic properties. Its lipophilicity may be attributed the electron-donating methyl group on its six-membered ring and the sp 3 nature of the nitrogen atom, discussed above. These can all account for derivative ( 6 ) having good antimicrobial activities as can be seen in Table 3 .

The molecular volume (MV), molecular refractivity (MR) and molecular weight (MW) of the precursor compound ( 1 ) was the lowest, followed by those of the pyrrolidinyl derivative ( 2 ). The five membered heterocycle ( 3 ) had lower MV MR and MW values than the six membered heterocycles ( 4 ), ( 5 ) and ( 6 ); derivatives ( 5 ) and ( 6 ) had similar values as the these compounds had only slight structural differences.

2.5. Energetics and electronic dipole analyses

The highest occupied and lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals (HOMO and LUMO, respectively), known as frontier molecular orbitals, were examined; HOMO and LUMO orbitals can be used to determine qualitative detail about the molecular structures of the compounds synthesized. Minimized energy conformations of the synthesized compounds were obtained.

Derivative ( 2 ) revealed that 44 of 75 molecular orbitals were occupied, and HOMO and LUMO calculated as −9.419 and −1.174 eV, respectively, with the energy gap (ΔE) 8.245 eV. Derivative ( 3 ) had 55 of 99 molecular orbitals occupied, with HOMO and LUMO orbitals at −9.339 and −0.859 eV, respectively, and energy gap 8.480 eV. Derivative ( 4 ) revealed 58 of 104, with HOMO and LUMO orbitals at −9.446 and −0.907, respectively, with energy gap 8.539 eV. Derivative ( 5 ) gave 61 of 110 molecular orbitals occupied, with HOMO and LUMO orbitals at −9.320 and −0.801 eV, respectively, with energy gap of 8.519 eV. Derivative ( 6 ) had 61 of 111 molecular orbitals occupied, with HOMO and LUMO orbitals at −9.468 and −0.763 eV, respectively, and energy gap 8.705 eV. It can be seen from Table 3 that the difference in the HOMO-LUMO energy gaps (ΔE) between derivatives ( 4 ) and ( 5 ) is significantly lower than that between derivatives ( 5 ) and ( 6 ); these differences may have some role on the antimicrobial activities of ( 4 ) and ( 5 ) being similar; both derivatives revealed an absence of antimicrobial activity. Derivative ( 6 ) had a high dipole moment, an unfavourable condition for microbial cell permeation, however, this was compensated for by its highly lipophilic character, as can be seen in Table 3 , and hence good antimicrobial activities.

2.6. Fourier Transform Infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) measurements

Infrared absorption spectra of synthesized compounds ( 2 – 6 ) showed characteristic NH 2 sulfonamide, N H amide, C O amide, and S O sulfonamide stretching vibrations at approximately 3350 and 3280 cm −1 , 3200 cm −1 , 1690 cm −1 and 1330 and 1160 cm −1 , respectively, as given in the experimental section. These confirmed formation of 4-(2-methylacetamide)benzenesulfonamide derivatives ( 2 – 6 ).

2.7. Electronic spectra (UV–Vis) measurements

Electronic spectra of synthesized derivatives ( 2 – 6 ), and sulfanilamide ( 1 ) were obtained in spectrophotometric grade DMF, and are given in Fig. 1 . Sulfanilamide ( 1 ) in DMF (polarity index of 6.4), revealed an intense absorption at 268 nm, with a weak band at 302 nm, and this result agreed well with previous reports [40] ; [41] ; [42] . Absorption 268 nm was assigned as benzenoid ring π-π ∗ transitions. Absorption 302 nm denotes n-π ∗ transitions, originating from the non-bonding electrons of N or S atoms of the precursor sulfanilamide ( 1 ). Hence, these two peaks observed in the spectrum of sulfanilamide ( 1 ) justifies the existence of two types of transitions, where one is strong and occurring with high probabilities, and the other being weak and occurring with low probabilities. Fig. 1 shows that all the sulfonamide derivatives had similar absorption spectra as a result of the observed absorption band, λ max at 268 nm, except that for derivative ( 2 ). A strong absorption at 270 nm, together with a shoulder peak at 286 nm were observed for derivative ( 2 ). The main absorption of derivative ( 2 ) was broader than that of the precursor molecule ( 1 ), and shifted slightly to higher wavelength (from 268 nm to 270 nm). Absorption 268 nm was assigned as the π-π ∗ transition in the structures of the sulfonamide derivatives ( 3 – 6 ). Additionally, a weak band was observed at 302 nm for the precursor molecule ( 1 ) that disappeared with sulfonamide derivative ( 3 – 6 ) formation. Thus, this illustrated that no n-π ∗ transitions for the sulfonamide derivatives ensued, that is, the prevalent electronic energy level transitions transformed into π-π∗ transitions with sulfonamide derivative formation. The spectra of the sulfonamide derivatives revealed that the π-π ∗ transition peak intensities increased with larger cyclic structural molecule formation, with respect to the starting molecule ( 1 ). This intensity increase may be as a result of these new compounds being structurally more cyclic, and thus able to donate π electrons better. Therefore, these sulfonamide derivatives bearing rich electronic structures, can lead to appreciable increases in maximum absorption intensities. Derivative ( 5 ), however, had the highest maximum absorption intensity increase among all the sulfonamide derivatives. Further, no maximum absorption shift occurred for the sulfonamide derivatives compared to the maximum absorption wavelength of the precursor compound ( 1 ).

2.9. Photoluminescence studies

Absorption and photoluminescence spectra for solutions of sulfanilamide ( 1 ) and derivatives ( 2 – 6 ), excited at 263 nm, were studied. Interestingly, irradiation by UV light of sulfanilamide ( 1 ) and derivatives ( 2 – 6 ) revealed strong emissions. The photoluminescence spectra of sulfanilamide ( 1 ) and derivatives ( 2 – 6 ) in DMF are presented in Fig. 7 . Maximum luminescent intensity was observed at 348 nm, and the full width at half maximum (FWHM) was 48 nm for compound ( 1 ). Compound ( 1 ) exhibited a photoluminescence quantum yield of 27%, and a long excited-state lifetime of 2.57 ns. Maximum luminescent intensity was observed at 374 nm, with a FWHM of 99 nm for derivative ( 5) . Derivative ( 5) exhibited 35% photoluminescence quantum yield and 3.27 ns excited-state lifetime. A greater number of electron donating groups onto ring para-positions of derivatives ( 3 – 6 ) caused increases in the intensity of main peaks, and shifted to higher emission wavelengths. Addition of more electron donating groups onto the ring para-positions of derivatives ( 3 – 6 ) caused an increase in intensity of main peaks, and shifted to higher emission wavelengths. High quantum yield is a consequence of the large molecular structures having extensive π-electron delocalizations. Therefore, the fluorescence emission intensity of the compounds increased with more π bonds in the larger compound, or with the formation of electron rich cyclic molecules. These cyclic derivatives are more electron rich, and thus this leads to improved electron delocalization and/or energy transfer from the excited state of the sulfonamide derivatives. As a result, an increase in the non-radiated transition of the sulfonamide derivative’s excited state, and increase in fluorescence emission occurs. Further, the attachment of more electron withdrawing groups onto the ring para-position of derivative ( 2 ) decreased the intensity of the main peaks, and shifted to lower emission wavelengths. Adding more electron withdrawing groups pulls electrons from cyclic derivative molecules, and thus reduces the possibility of emission from excited state of the cyclic derivative molecules.

3. Conclusion

In this study, novel 4-(2-methylacetylamino)benzenesulfonamide derivatives were synthesized and characterized by FT-IR, 1 H and 13 C NMR, LC–MS–MS and elemental analysis. Photoluminescence intensities and efficiencies of the derivatives were observed to be higher than that of the precursor molecule sulfanilamide. Physicochemical parameters of the derivatives, and their in vitro antimicrobial activities were discussed and correlations between the partiton coefficient log P and observed antimicrobial activities were examined. The fine interplay of lipophilicity and polarity effects of the synthesized compounds control antimicrobial activities, where lipophilic states led to greater antimicrobial activites, and derivative ( 6 ) showed good antimicrobial activities. Antimicrobial activities revealed that activities were best observed for bacteria; activities against yeast were minimal. Increases in ring size, and rings holding a nitrogen heteroatom led to good antimicrobial activities. The in vitro inhibitory cytotoxicity activities were investigated, and it was found that compounds ( 3 – 6 ) were moderately active against all cancerous cells, particularly against the HeLa cell types for which moderate to good cytotoxicities were observed. Compounds ( 3 – 6 ) showed significant cytotoxic effects on HeLa cells, and little activity against the other cancer cells.

The results of this study may faciliate in the design and development of metalloenzyme inhibitory drugs, for the advancement of carbonic anhydrase research field. Additionally, the compounds synthesized may assist in the development of new antimicrobial agents, and their photophysical properties may offer potential in medicine for diagnostic imaging purposes.

4. Experimental

4.1. Materials and chemicals

Chemicals were purchased from commercial suppliers (Merck, Sigma-Aldrich) and were used as received. Solvents used were dichloromethane (DCM), diethyl ether, N , N -dimethylformamide (DMF), ethanol, ethyl acetate, methanol, hexane, petroleum ether and tetrahydrofuran (THF). Reagents used were sulfanilamide, pyridine, 2-bromopropionyl bromide, sodium bicarbonate, pyrrolidine, piperazine, 1-methylpiperazine, 4-methylpiperidine, hydrochloric acid and 9,10-diphenylanthracene.

4.2. Instrumentation and measurements

Elemental analyses were carried out using a LECO CHNS-932 Elemental Analyser. FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Perkin Elmer Spectrum Two FT-IR Spectrometer in the region 400–4000 cm −1 . A Perkin Elmer Lambda 25 UV–Vis Spectrophotometer was used for the absorption spectra measurements of range 200–1100 nm in DMF at room temperature. Photoluminescence measurements were carried out with a Perkin Elmer LS55 Spectrofluorophotometer, where quartz sample cells of 1 cm optical path were used; sample concentrations in DMF were 1.0 × 10 −5 mol L −1 , and excitation wavelength 263 nm was applied. The standard 9,10-diphenylantracene was used for the photoluminescence quantum yield measurements [43] ; [44] ; [45] . NMR spectra were recorded on an Agilent-VNMRS-400 Spectrometer ( 1 H NMR 400 MHz and 13 C NMR 100 MHz) in DMSO- d 6 , with TMS as an internal standard, at 25 °C. Melting points of compounds were measured in open capillary tubes using an Electrothermal 9100 melting point apparatus. A Shimadzu LCMS 8040 Model Spectrometer was used for recording mass spectra. The course of reactions and product purities were assessed using TLC plates (Merck Silica Gel 60 F 254 ).

4.3. QSAR molecular descriptor evaluations

Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) physiochemical parameters were collected by use of HyperChem [46] , where structural optimizations were achieved using Molecular Mechanics Force Field (MM+), followed by refinements via the semi-empirical PM3 method. The geometrically optimized structures were used to compute HOMO and LUMO values.

4.6. Antimicrobial studies

Antimicrobial activity studies were performed by the agar diffusion technique using sterile filter paper disks according to the literature method [47] , with methanol as the negative control. In vitro antimicrobial activities were studied using Gram-positive bacteria Bacillus megaterium , Bacillus subtilis and Staphylococcus aureus , Gram-negative bacteria Escherichia coli , Enterobacter aerogenes , Klebsiella pneumonia and Pseudomonas aeruginosa and fungi Candida albicans , Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Yarrowia lipolytica , all of which were supplied by the Department of Biology, Kahramanmaras Sutcu Imam University, Turkey.

Microbial culture preparation was done according to the literature [47] , and is described briefly here. Bacteria were incubated at 37 °C (Difco Nutrient Broth, 24 h), and yeast for at 25 °C (Difco Sabourand Dextrose Broth, 24 h). Cultures (0.1 mL) of bacteria and yeast (105 cfu/mL and 104 cfu/mL for bacteria and yeast, respectively) were injected over petri dishes and sterile filter paper disks (6 mm) soaked with 10 μL sample solutions were implanted on the solid agar surfaces. The petri dishes were left at 4 °C (2 h) to allow samples to diffuse onto agar medium. The compounds synthesized were screened in triplicate, with the inhibition zones given as their average, and expressed in mm.

4.7. In vitro cytotoxic activity

4.7.1. Cell cultures

The cells studied were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, Virginia), and included breast adenocarcinoma (MCF-7 and MDA-MB-231), cervix carcinoma (HeLa), colorectal adenocarcinoma (DLD-1), human prostate cancer (PC-3 and DU-145), normal prostate epithelium (PNT-1A) and embryonic kidney (HEK-293) cells. As recommended by ATCC, cells were subjected to propagation using DMEM-F12 and RPMI-1640 media, with supplements 10% foetal bovine serum, l-glutamine (2 mM), penicillin (100 U/mL) and streptomycin (100 mg/mL) in a humidified atmosphere (5% CO 2 ) at 37 °C. When the cultures reached 70–80% confluence, the cells were harvested using 0.25% trypsin (Sigma).

4.7.2. Cytotoxicity assays and determination of IC 50

Cytotoxic activities were based on the reduction of (3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide) (MTT) by mitochondrial dehydrogenase of viable cells to give blue formazan product which can be measured spectrophotometrically by UV–Vis measurements. MTT colorimetric assays were performed using 96 well plates. The cells were seeded in a 96 well plate at concentration 5.0 × 10 4 cells/well and incubated at 37 °C for 24 h. After treatment with various concentrations of the test compound (1, 5, 10, 25, 50, 100 and 200 μM), the cells were incubated for an additional 48 h at 37 °C. After incubation, the medium was removed, and the cells in each well were incubated with 100 μL MTT solution (5 mg/mL) for 4 h at 37 °C. MTT solutions were then discarded, and 100 μL DMSO were added to dissolve insoluble formazan crystals. Optical densities were measured at 570 nm. Data were obtained from triplicate wells. Cytotoxic effects were determined in reference to negative controls (vehicle treated cells). Cytotoxicity was expressed as the mean percentage increase relative to the unexposed control (mean ± standard deviation). Control values were set to 0% cytotoxicity. Cytotoxicity data, where appropriate, were fitted to sigmoidal curves and a four-parameter logistic model was used to calculate the IC 50 values, the concentration of material causing 50% inhibition, compared to the untreated controls.
