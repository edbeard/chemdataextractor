The synthesis and photophysics of four platinum( II ) bipyridine (bpy) bisacetylide complexes with different degrees of Ï€-conjugation and an electron-donating diphenylamino (NPh 2 ) or electron-withdrawing benzothiazolyl (BTZ) terminal substituent on the acetylide ligands are reported. The UV-vis absorption spectra of these complexes are composed of intense ligand-localized 1 Ï€,Ï€* transitions at 330â€“430 nm and broad, moderately strong ligand-to-ligand charge transfer/metal-to-ligand charge transfer ( 1 LLCT/ 1 MLCT) transitions at 430â€“530 nm. All complexes are phosphorescent in solutions at room temperature and exhibit very broad and moderately strong triplet excited-state absorption in the visible to the NIR spectral region (425â€“800 nm). It is found that extending the Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligands via adding one or two more ethynylfluorenyl unit(s) to the acetylide ligand does not change the energies of the 1 Ï€,Ï€* and 1 LLCT/ 1 MLCT transitions pronouncedly except for increasing the molar extinction coefficients of the 1 Ï€,Ï€* transitions. The emitting triplet excited states of the four complexes are the 3 MLCT/ 3 LLCT states and have the same energy. However, the complex that contains the tris(ethynylfluorenyl) units and the terminal NPh 2 substituent on the acetylide ligand exhibits longer triplet lifetimes than the corresponding complex that has the bis(ethynylfluorenyl) units. The transient absorption band maxima of the complexes with tris(ethynylfluorenyl) units are slightly red-shifted in comparison to those of their respective counterparts with bis(ethynylfluorenyl) units. The nature of the terminal substituent does not influence the parentage and energies of the lowest singlet and triplet excited states. However, the triplet excited-state lifetimes of the complexes with the NPh 2 terminal substituent on the bis(ethynylfluorenyl) or tris(ethynylfluorenyl) ligands are much longer than that of their counterpart with monofluorenylacetylide ligands; while the triplet lifetimes of the complexes containing the BTZ terminal substituent are similar to their counterpart with monofluorenylacetylide ligands. All complexes exhibit strong reverse saturable absorption (RSA) at 532 nm for nanosecond laser pulses.

Introduction

Platinum( II ) diimine bisacetylide complexes 1â€“6 have received considerable attention in recent years because of their unique photophysical properties (like room-temperature phosphorescence, broadband excited-state absorption, and long-lived triplet excited state), and versatile potential applications in areas such as solar energy conversion, 5,7 low-power upconversion, 8â€“12 electroluminescence, 13 oxygen sensing, 11,14 nonlinear optics, 15â€“25 vapochromism, 26 photochromism, 27â€“29 etc. The interesting photophysical properties and diverse applications originate from the heavy-atom induced rapid intersystem crossing, the square-planar configuration of the Pt( II ) complexes, the presence of a variety of charge-transfer excited states, and the facile tuning of the diimine and acetylide ligands.

It has been reported that introducing the electron-withdrawing or donating substituents to the diimine ligand stabilizes or destabilizes the diimine ligand-localized lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) and thus decreases or increases the energies of the metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) and ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (LLCT) transitions. 30â€“32 However, altering the substituents on the acetylide ligands does not exhibit a monotonic trend on the characteristics of the lowest triplet excited states because these changes influence both the 3 MLCT/ 3 LLCT transitions and the acetylide ligand-localized 3 Ï€,Ï€* transitions. 30,32 The interplay of the 3 MLCT/ 3 LLCT and 3 Ï€,Ï€* transitions could change the nature of the lowest excited state, and thus cause an irregular trend of the emission properties. In addition to the donor/acceptor effect, the Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligands also influences the nature of the triplet excited state drastically. Castellano and co-workers discovered that the contribution of the acetylide ligand-localized 3 Ï€,Ï€* state to the lowest triplet excited state became more significant when the Ï€-conjugation of acetylide ligands was extended. 33â€“35 Zhao and co-workers also found that incorporation of Ï€-conjugated naphthalenediimide, coumarin, or rhodamine substituents into the acetylide ligands changed the lowest triplet excited state to the long-lived acetylide ligand-localized 3 Ï€,Ï€* state. 8,10,11 Similar results were reported by our group as well. 22 Therefore, in order to meet the specific requirements for a particular application it is critical to select an appropriate substituent on the acetylide ligand.

For nonlinear optical applications that utilize the reverse saturable absorption (RSA, a nonlinear absorption process in which the absorptivity increases when the incident fluence increases due to stronger excited-state absorption than the ground-state absorption), we have demonstrated that benzothiazol-2-yl (BTZ) and diphenylamino (NPh 2 ) substituents can enhance the triplet excited-state absorption in the visible spectral region and thus significantly improve the RSA of nanosecond (ns) laser pulses at 532 nm. 15â€“17 We also showed that the RSA at 532 nm was remarkably improved by extending the acetylide ligand Ï€-conjugation via incorporation of an ethynylphenyl unit or pyrenyl substituent into the fluorenylacetylide ligand. 21,22 However, we found that the Pt( II ) bipyridine bisacetylide complexes with a strong electron-donating NPh 2 substituent on the fluorenylacetylide ligand possess quite short-lived triplet excited states. 17,21 Therefore, even if these complexes exhibit very strong triplet excited-state absorption in the visible spectral region (as demonstrated by the fs transient absorption measurement at a longer delay time), the utilization of these complexes as reverse saturable absorbers becomes quite limited. For the Pt( II ) bipyridine bisacetylide complexes bearing the BTZ substituent on the fluorenylacetylide ligand, although its triplet lifetime is quite long (10.8 Î¼s in CH 2 Cl 2 ) 15 and the triplet excited-state absorption in the visible spectral region is strong, its triplet excited-state absorption in the near-IR region is quite weak, which is also the case for the complex with the NPh 2 substituent. 15,17 This feature would limit the application of these complexes as broadband reverse saturable absorbers in the near-IR region.

It is well known that for an ideal broadband reverse saturable absorber, weak ground-state absorption but much stronger excited-state absorption in the visible to the near-IR spectral region ( i.e. 400â€“900 nm) is desirable; and a long triplet excited-state lifetime and a high triplet quantum yield facilitate the RSA for nanosecond and longer pulse-width laser pulses. Therefore, searching for materials with a long-lived triplet excited state and a large ratio of the excited-state absorption vs. ground-state absorption cross-sections over a broad spectral range has been the ultimate goal in developing broadband RSA materials.

Inspired by the finding that the extended Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligand could increase the triplet lifetime by switching the lowest triplet excited state to the acetylide ligand localized 3 Ï€,Ï€* state, we seek to improve the triplet excited-state lifetime of the NPh 2 substituted Pt( II ) bipyridine acetylide complexes and broaden the triplet excited-state absorption to the near-IR region by further extending the Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligands. In this work, we synthesized four new Pt( II ) diimine complexes with two or three repeating ethynylfluorenyl motifs on the acetylide ligands ( Pt-1â€“Pt-4 in Scheme 1 ). The selection of NPh 2 and BTZ substituents as the terminal substituents on the acetylide ligands is based not only on the fact that the Pt( II ) bipyridine bisacetylide complexes containing these groups have been demonstrated to exhibit strong RSA at 532 nm, but also on the electronic nature of these substituents, which allows us to further evaluate the effects of the electron-donor (NPh 2 ) and the electron-acceptor (BTZ) on the acetylide ligands with extended Ï€-conjugation. For comparison purpose, some of the photophysical data for complexes bearing monoethynylfluorenyl units on the acetylide ligands ( i.e. complexes 1d and 1e in ref. 17 ) are included in this paper.

Computational method

All calculations, i.e. ground state optimization (singlet and triplet), natural transition orbital (NTO) generation, and linear absorption and emission (singlet and triplet), were performed using the Gaussian09 software package. 46 In the calculations, the solvent (toluene) was implemented by the Conductor-like Polarized Continuum Model (CPCM). 47,48 To reduce computational cost, the 2-ethylhexyl substituent on the fluorenes were replaced by the methyl group. This change has a minor effect on the optical properties of the complexes because the electronic transitions of the alkyl chains occur outside the considered energy window. The optimized ground-state geometries were calculated using density functional theory (DFT) with the hybrid functional PBE1PBE 49 and basis sets LANL2DZ 50,51 for Pt( II ) and 6-31G* 52â€“56 for all other atoms (C, N, H and S). A similar methodology has been proven to be successful for calculating other Pt( II ) complexes. 20,57 The coordinates of the resulting optimized geometries are reported in the ESI, â€  Tables S1 and S2.

The absorption spectra were calculated using the linear-response time dependent DFT (TDDFT). To improve the agreement with the experimental absorption spectra, we increased the percentage of the Hartreeâ€“Fock (HF) exchange in the PBE1 functional from 25% to 35%. The 60 lowest singlet transitions were calculated, and the optical transitions obtained from the TDDFT calculations were broadened by the Gaussian function with a linewidth of Ïƒ = 0.1 eV. Fig. S1 in the ESI â€  shows the comparison of experimental spectra and spectra calculated using the PBE1 functional with different percentages of the HF exchange (25%, 30% and 35%) and the long-range corrected functionals LcwPBE and WB97XD (with additional dispersion correction). For all TDDFT calculations, the molecular structures used were based on the geometries optimized by the PBE1PBE functional and the basis set used for the ground state calculations. Interestingly, the long-range corrected functionals altered the exciton binding energy; thus the relative ordering of the Ï€,Ï€* versus charge-transfer (CT) excitons significantly changed by moving the CT band inside the Ï€,Ï€* band, which is in agreement with literature reports. 58,59 In contrast, the hybrid functional with 35% HF exchange demonstrated the best qualitative and quantitative agreement with the experimental spectra. Therefore, it was used for both absorption and emission calculations.

For calculation of the emission energies, optimization of the analytical first-order derivatives of electronic excited states energy with respect to nuclear perturbations was performed within the framework of TDDFT. 60 It is expected that the emission of these complexes has phosphorescent nature, which is a spin forbidden transition from the triplet excited state (T 1 ) to the singlet ground state (S 0 ), but becomes partially allowed with increased spinâ€“orbit coupling in the presence of the heavy atom Pt. Our calculations do not include the spinâ€“orbit couplings. However, Kasha's rule states that the most probable transition occurs from the lowest excited state. 61 As such, the lowest energy transition T 1 â€“S 0 is expected to agree with the experimental emission energy. Usually, calculated results obtained this way agreed with the experimental results qualitatively but not quantitatively. To improve the agreement with the experimental data, we used the PBE functional with 35% of HF exchange (similar to that used in absorption calculations) and ran the analytical-gradient TDDFT. 62 This hybrid functional results in a first T 1 â€“S 0 transition within âˆ¼25 nm of experimental results.

Characterization of the nature of the optical transitions is an important part of this study, because knowing how the structural variations affect the photophysics of the organometallic complexes will allow us to rationally design new complexes with predetermined photophysical properties. To analyse the type of transition, natural transition orbitals (NTOs) 63 were calculated using Gaussian09 software and visualized via GaussView 5.09 graphical software 64 using an isovalue of 0.02. NTOs denote an excited state as an electronâ€“hole pair, while being different from the ground state orbitals due to the inclusion of electronâ€“hole interaction effects implemented for the TDDFT procedure. As such, each optical transition is characterized by an excited highest occupied NTO (a hole) and a lowest unoccupied NTO (an electron, also called a particle). 63

Results and discussion

Geometry optimization

By using density functional theory (DFT) with the hybrid functional PBE1PBE 49 and basis sets LANL2DZ 50,51 for Pt( II ) and 6-31G* 52â€“56 for all other atoms (C, N, H and S), the ground-state and excited state geometries of the ligands L-1â€“L-4 and complexes Pt-1â€“Pt-4 were calculated in toluene. The resultant optimized ground state and the lowest triplet excited state geometries of Pt-1â€“Pt-4 are reported in ESI, â€  Tables S1 and S2. The optimized geometries are asymmetric, with different torsion angles between the corresponding units in the two acetylide ligands. The torsion angles are the largest between the first ethynylfluorenyl unit and the (bpy)Pt unit, then the angles decrease between the first and the second ethynylfluorenyl units. The second and the third ethynylfluorenyl units in Pt-2 and Pt-4 are almost coplanar, with torsion angles â‰¤1.2Â°. This trend holds for both the optimized ground state geometries and the triplet excited state geometries. However, the corresponding torsion angles become smaller in the optimized triplet excited state geometries compared to those in the ground state geometries.

Because the geometries of the two acetylide ligands are quite flexible with regard to rotation across the ethynylfluorenyl units, a single minimum-energy structure may not well describe the geometries in the solution phase, which reflect an average of all possible geometries and result in symmetric structures. To confirm whether this is the case, we calculated the ground-state energies of two representative symmetric geometries of Pt-3 in which the torsion angles of one of the acetylide ligands were reserved for the optimized geometry, and the other ligand was treated as the mirror image of the reserved ligand. The energy differences between the optimized geometry and these symmetric geometries are provided in ESI, â€  Table S3. The results indicate that small variations in torsion angles strongly increase the total energy by about 2 eV. This implies that thermal fluctuations would not have sufficient energy to allow random rotations between the ethynylfluorenyl units. Since the torsion angles within the ethynylfluorenyl units are the only variables that change in these calculations, we believe that a similar energy difference holds between the optimized asymmetric geometries and the symmetric geometries of the other complexes. Therefore, the optimized asymmetric geometries shown in ESI, â€  Tables S1 and S2 for Pt-1â€“Pt-4 were used for the TDDFT calculations of the electronic absorption and emission.

Emission

The emission characteristics of the ligands L-1â€“L-4 and the complexes Pt-1â€“Pt-4 were investigated at room temperature. Fig. 3 illustrates the normalized emission spectra of L-1â€“L-4 and Pt-1â€“Pt-4 in toluene. The emission lifetimes and quantum yields in toluene are listed in Table 1 . All ligands exhibit intense fluorescence with vibronic features in the range of 375â€“550 nm ( Fig. 3a ), suggesting the 1 Ï€,Ï€* nature of fluorescence in toluene. The fluorescence energies of L-1 and L-2 that contain the electron-donating NPh 2 substituent are lower than those of L-3 and L-4 that bear the electron-withdrawing BTZ substituent, suggesting more delocalized electrons in L-1 and L-2 . The ligands L-2 and L-4 with three repeating ethynylfluorenyl units show red-shifted fluorescence with respect to their corresponding counterparts L-1 and L-3 that contain two repeating ethynylfluorenyl units due to the larger Ï€-conjugation in L-2 and L-4 . This assignment is supported by the TDDFT calculation results. As shown in Table 4 , the NTOs contributing to the T 1 â€“S 0 transition for all four complexes are the same, with the holes being on the d orbital of Pt( II ) and the first ethynylfluorenyl unit, while the electrons are predominantly on the bipyridine ligand with minor contribution from the triple bond of the first ethynylfluorenyl unit. Reverse saturable absorption (RSA)

For compounds with weak ground-state absorption but stronger excited-state absorption at a given wavelength, reverse saturable absorption ( i.e. absorptivity of the compound increases when the incident energy increases, resulting in a decreased transmission) is expected to occur. The stronger excited-state absorption than the ground-state absorption is reflected by a positive signal in its TA spectrum. Because Pt-1â€“Pt-4 all exhibit positive absorption bands in the region of 425â€“800 nm in their TA spectra, RSA in this region is anticipated to occur for these complexes. To manifest this, a nonlinear transmission experiment in toluene solution at 532 nm was carried out using 4.1 ns laser pulses in a 2 mm cuvette. The linear transmission of the toluene solution for each sample was adjusted to 80% in the 2 mm cuvette for easy comparison. In such a case, the observed RSA difference should be attributed to the difference in the excited-state absorption. The transmission vs. incident energy curves of Pt-1â€“Pt-4 are presented in Fig. 6 . The transmission of Pt-1â€“Pt-4 decreases drastically when the incident energy increases, which clearly indicates the occurrence of RSA at 532 nm. The degree of RSA for Pt-1â€“Pt-4 is quite similar to each other, which is consistent with the estimated ratios of the excited-state absorption cross section ( Ïƒ ex ) to the ground-state absorption cross section ( Ïƒ 0 ) at 532 nm for these complexes (see Table 6 ). Conclusions

In order to increase the triplet excited-state lifetime and enhance the triplet excited-state absorption in the near-IR region, we synthesized four platinum( II ) bipyridyl complexes with bisacetylide ligands that contain two or three repeating ethynylfluorenyl units end-capped with an electron-donating NPh 2 or electron-withdrawing BTZ substituent. The photophysical properties of these complexes were systematically investigated via spectroscopic techniques and theoretical simulations and were compared to their corresponding counterpart containing monofluorenylacetylide ligands. It is found that the extended Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligands does not change the energies of the ligand-localized 1 Ï€,Ï€* transitions and the 1 LLCT/ 1 MLCT transitions pronouncedly, but it increases the molar extinction coefficients of the 1 Ï€,Ï€* transitions. The emissive 3 MLCT/ 3 LLCT states of the four complexes all have the holes being localized on the first ethynylfluorenyl unit and the d-orbital of the Pt( II ) center and the electrons on the bipyridine ligand. Therefore, the emission energies of the complexes are essentially independent of the conjugation length of the acetylide ligand and the terminal substituent. However, the emission quantum yields and lifetimes of Pt-1 and Pt-2 are much higher or longer than those of their counterparts with monoethynylfluorenyl units. Moreover, the complex Pt-2 with three repeating ethynylfluorenyl units has a longer triplet lifetime than Pt-1 with only two repeating units. These changes are attributed to the diminished quenching effect of the lowest triplet excited state by the NPh 2 substituent when it is separated further away from the localized exciton of the hole. The TA spectra of Pt-1â€“Pt-4 are much broader (425â€“800 nm) and stronger in the near-IR region in comparison to their respective counterpart bearing monoethynylfluorenyl units on the acetylide ligands, owing to the charge transfer nature of the excited states that transiently produce broadly and strongly absorbing acetylide radical cation species. Additionally, the TA absorption band maxima of the complexes with three repeating ethynylfluorenyl units ( i.e. Pt-2 and Pt-4 ) are slightly red-shifted compared to those of their respective counterpart bearing two repeating ethynylfluorenyl units ( i.e. Pt-1 and Pt-3) . Interestingly, the influence of the acetylide ligand Ï€-conjugation on the triplet lifetimes of Pt-1â€“Pt-4 appears to be different for the complexes with different terminal substituents when compared to their respective counterpart with monofluorenylacetylide ligands. The triplet lifetimes of Pt-1 and Pt-2 with the NPh 2 terminal substituent are much longer than that of their counterpart with monofluorenylacetylide ligands. In contrast, the triplet lifetimes of Pt-3 and Pt-4 with the BTZ terminal substituent are similar to their counterpart with monofluorenylacetylide ligands. This difference should be attributed to the diminished quenching by the NPh 2 substituent when the Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligand is extended; while the BTZ terminal substituent does not render such a quenching effect in the first place. Therefore, extended Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligand did not affect the triplet lifetimes of the complexes with the BTZ terminal substituent. Nevertheless, the extended Ï€-conjugation of the acetylide ligand increases the excited-state absorption in the near-IR region. Due to the stronger triplet excited-state absorption than the ground-state absorption in the visible spectral region, strong RSA was observed at 532 nm for nanosecond laser pulses from all four complexes, which is stronger than those of their counterparts with monofluorenylacetylide ligands. More importantly, the increased excited-state absorption in the near-IR region could promote the potential application of these complexes as broadband reverse saturable absorbers.
