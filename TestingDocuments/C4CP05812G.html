Three dyads with a fluorene derivative as an electron-donor and with electron-acceptors of variable redox potentials were synthesized as models for two-photon activated uncaging via electron transfer. A spectroscopic and photophysical study of the component units and the dyads in solvents of different polarities demonstrated an efficient electron transfer (efficiencies > 80%) followed by charge recombination in the arrays (30 ps < τ < 1.6 ns). Recombination takes place to the ground state in all cases except for the dyad displaying the highest driving force for charge recombination in the apolar solvent. The effects of changing the solvent polarity, as well as the driving force, for electron-transfer are discussed in the frame of the current theories of electron transfer.

Introduction

Uncaging is a drug delivery method in which a physiologically active compound can be liberated via a variety of photoinduced reactions from its inactive “caged precursor” by illumination with visible or ultra-violet light. 1 The inert analogue is obtained by modification of the physiologically relevant compound with a covalently attached photolabile protecting group and this structural alteration prevents the molecule from binding to receptors, rendering it biologically inactive. Biological activity is temporarily masked and can be restored. Irreversible removal of the protecting group is triggered by light and enables interaction of the organic molecule with the targeted receptor.

Under two-photon excitation conditions, the photolysis is spatially restricted to the focal point, resulting in a tight three-dimensional control over the administration of the biologically active substance. Furthermore, the use of infrared wavelengths allows deeper penetration into the tissue and reduces scattering compared to ultra-violet and visible light. For these reasons, two-photon uncaging has attracted great interest. 2 This technique is particularly valued in neuroscience as it enables selective stimulation of sub-cellular domains in single neurons in their multilayer circuits, in brain slices. 3 Photolysis of caged neurotransmitters allows the distribution of receptors to be mapped within specific divisions of single cells, for investigating synaptic connectivity. 4 Accurate control over the location of photolysis provides unambiguous information on the structure–function relationship and thus yields insights into receptor stimulation. Despite its advantages, the full capacity of two-photon uncaging has not been explored yet. Many studies have been carried out to elucidate the structure–property relationships, in the search for protecting groups that absorb two photons of infra-red light efficiently and undergo subsequent bond-scission in high yield. The parameter quantifying the overall sensitivity of a two-photon protecting group, the two-photon uncaging cross-section δ u [GM] (1 GM = 10 50 cm 4 s photon −1 ), is the product of efficiencies of absorption and bond cleavage, the two-photon absorption cross-section δ a [GM] and the uncaging quantum yield ϕ u , respectively, ( δ u = δ a ϕ u ). Considering the diffusion and depletion of the caged species from the focal volume, it was calculated that for biological applications, δ u should exceed 3 GM to ensure (i) efficient two-photon absorption at laser power levels harmless to living cells and (ii) release of the neurotransmitter in the concentration required to induce a biological response. 5 A number of strategies have been explored to prepare highly sensitive two-photon cages. Early efforts aimed at designing new chromophores that possess both high δ a and ϕ u . Extensive studies of the structure–property relationships (usually including a series of small aromatic platforms with various substitution patterns) resulted in the development of protecting groups with δ u of 0.06 6 to 1.60 7 GM. This category includes families of caging groups such as nitroindolinyl, 6,8 o -hydroxycinnamate, 7,9 coumarin, 10 inorganic metal complexes, 11 2-(2-nitrophenyl)propyl, 12 o -nitrobenzyl 13 and quinoline. 14 The shortcoming of this approach lies in the fact that small chromophores display relatively low δ a . Consideration of the structural factors enhancing two-photon absorption cross-section (π-conjugated, coplanar systems with electron donating and accepting groups) 15 led to an alternative approach, in which components of pre-existing photolabile protecting groups were built into a large π-system. 16 Ultimately, this strategy showed some promise and yielded several derivatives within the 2-(2-nitrophenyl)propyl series with δ u > 3 GM (BNSF – 5.0 GM at 800 nm, 17 EANBP – 11 GM at 800 nm 18 ). Nevertheless, this approach presents some difficulties, as one must balance inherently related absorption and bond-scission. Moreover, extension of the pre-existing protecting group with other chromophores can lead to a low quantum yield of uncaging ϕ u , due to competitive photochemical pathways. Further development of two-photon sensitive protecting groups utilized spatial separation of absorption and cleavage, making these two processes occur in different parts of a supramolecular structure. This approach allows the complications associated with striving for a suitable compromise between absorption and release to be circumvented, however it has not yet received much attention. The concept of sensitized uncaging was initially demonstrated for one-photon photolysis 19 and then successfully transferred onto the two-photon field. Examples of inter- 20 and intra-molecular 21 energy transfer mediated two-photon uncaging show some promise, although the values of two-photon uncaging cross-section are still modest (0.86 GM at 766 nm ( ref. 20 ) and 0.5 GM at 730 nm ( ref. 21 )). Photoinduced electron transfer (PeT) mediated uncaging was demonstrated, only for one-photon photolysis, by Falvey and coworkers. 19a–c This design strategy has not yet been transferred to the two-photon field, but offers the possibility to create protecting groups with high two-photon uncaging cross-sections by optimizing absorption independently from cleavage. In order to achieve this objective, we need to understand the underlying photochemistry and photophysics, which is the motivation for the current study.

We have established a program to develop caging groups, the deprotection of which is mediated by photoinduced electron transfer (PeT) between the electron donor with high δ a and the electron-acceptor with optimized δ u . 22 We chose a bisethynyl fluorene dye ( BEF-OH ) as an electron-donor ( δ a for the parent chromophore 1260 GM at 740 nm) 23 and identified three possible acceptor candidates, for which the electron transfer would be energetically favored but with different driving forces, N -methyl pyridinium ( Pyr ), phenacyl ( Phen ) and para -nitrobenzoate ( NB ) ( Fig. 1 ). The electron acceptors used in this study are not uncaging groups by themselves, but they can be elaborated into uncaging groups, as shown by the previous work, 19 and by other work which will be published separately. 22 To gain an insight into the dynamics of intramolecular charge separation and charge recombination within each pair, both of which affect the yield of uncaging, we prepared three corresponding dyads: BEF–Pyr , BEF–Phen and BEF–NB . Here we report an extended study of photoinduced electron transfer in model compounds representing donor–acceptor units of our novel protecting groups, with the aim of gaining an insight into the parameters affecting their electron transfer dynamics, critical to the value of δ u .

Spectroscopy and photophysics

Absorption and luminescence. The experiments on the compounds displayed in Fig. 1 were performed in non-polar toluene (TOL) and polar methanol (MeOH). The absorption spectra of the dyads and models in MeOH are collected in Fig. 2 and those in TOL are reported in Fig. S35 (ESI † ). The spectra of the models Pyr , NB and Phen are characterized by absorption bands below 320 nm and with molar absorption coefficients between 5000 and 20 000 M −1 cm −1 . The absorption spectra of the dyads retain the fluorene features, displaying broad bands between 350 and 450 nm and epsilon values of about 100 000 M −1 cm −1 in MeOH. Minor bathochromic shifts on the fluorene bands and hypsochromic shifts on the acceptor bands can be observed in the dyads, but overall their absorption spectra are well-reproduced by the addition of the absorption of the constituent models, one BEF-OH and two acceptors, either Pyr , NB or Phen ( Fig. 2 ). This indicates a weak electronic coupling of the different moieties of the dyads in the ground state.
Fig. 2 Absorption spectra of the dyads and models in MeOH solutions; the molar absorption coefficient values of Pyr , NB and Phen are multiplied by 2.

The electron-acceptor models ( Pyr , NB and Phen ) are non-emissive at room temperature after excitation on the respective bands. Selective excitation at 350 nm on the fluorene chromophore in an optically matched model and dyad solutions returned the luminescence spectra collected in Fig. 3 . The fluorescence of the BEF-OH model is structured in TOL and the shape and band positions are preserved in the dyads. In MeOH the spectrum of the fluorene model is structureless and bathochromically shifted by almost 50 nm with respect to apolar toluene. In this solvent the fluorescence band appears slightly hypsochromically shifted in the dyads with respect to the model. Taken altogether, these data indicate that the excited state of the BEF-OH model has a significant charge-transfer character, hence it is stabilized in polar MeOH but slightly destabilized in the dyads with respect to the model in the same solvent. The electron donor BEF-OH emits strongly: ϕ fl = 0.52 in TOL and 0.48 in MeOH ( Table 1 ). This fluorescence is dramatically quenched in the dyads, more in MeOH than in TOL, Fig. 3 and Table 1 . The quenching in TOL in BEF–Pyr and BEF–NB dyads is to a value of 0.5% of the original model fluorescence, and in BEF–Phen to 8%. In MeOH the quenching in the first two dyads reduces the fluorescence to below 0.5% and in BEF–Phen to 0.6% of the original model fluorescence. Since a singlet–singlet energy transfer process from the fluorene chromophore to the acceptor is thermodynamically unfeasible, the quenching in the dyads is an indication of an electron transfer process.

Time-resolved luminescence experiments were performed by the time correlated single photon counting (TCSPC) technique with excitation at 373 nm and a resolution of 0.3 ns. This allows us to measure the lifetime of the BEF-OH model, 0.75 ns in TOL and 1.30 ns in MeOH. The quenched lifetime of the fluorene-based chromophore in the dyads could be measured using a picosecond resolution streak camera after excitation at 355 nm. The lifetime in MeOH was 15, 18 and 43 ps for BEF–Pyr , BEF–NB and BEF–Phen , respectively. In TOL, slightly higher lifetimes of 24 and 21 ps were measured for BEF–Pyr and BEF–NB , respectively, whereas for BEF–Phen a much longer bi-exponential decay, with lifetimes of 66 and 352 ps with 50% contribution each, was detected ( Fig. 4 and Table 1 ). The presence of two different lifetimes with an almost equivalent contribution in TOL solutions is indicative of the presence of two different populations of the fluorene excited state with a 1 : 1 ratio, which do not equilibrate during the lifetime of the state. This might correspond well to excited conformers with similar energy in the donor–acceptor structure, whose formation is favored in the non-polar solvent. In MeOH there is a single population, as testified by the mono-exponential decay. The quenching in the dyads with respect to the model, calculated on the basis of the lifetime reduction ( τ / τ 0 ) is lower than that calculated on the basis of the decrease in the fluorescence yields ( ϕ / ϕ 0 ), though the general trend is in agreement. This apparent inconsistency is very likely to be ascribed to a decrease in the radiative rate ( k rad ) of the emitting state in the dyads with respect to the model, caused by the non-identical chemical environment of the chromophore in the two cases. The two parameters are related by the equation k rad = ϕ / τ .


Fig. 4 Streak camera images of BEF–Phen in TOL (left) and MeOH (right). The time profile data and fitting at 435 nm in TOL and at 450 nm in MeOH are overlaid with the flash profile (thin line).

The excitation spectra recorded on the emission maxima of fluorene in the model and the dyads, overlay perfectly with the corresponding absorption spectra (Fig. S36, ESI † ). Thus, they indicate a genuine emission from the excited state of fluorene and the absence of emitting contaminants.

Luminescence experiments were run at 77 K in rigid glasses and the fluorescence spectra of the samples registered upon excitation at 370 nm are reported in Fig. S37 (ESI † ). Luminescence data at 77 K have only a qualitative meaning, and the conditions of the experiment (see the Experimental section for details) do not allow a direct comparison of signals from optically matched samples. However, it is quite evident that both in TOL and in MeOH the fluorene unit fluorescence is quenched in BEF–NB , whereas in BEF–Pyr and BEF–Phen an intense fluorescence from the fluorene moiety is still detected. The fluorescence of BEF–Pyr in TOL is remarkably broader and red-shifted in comparison with the other dyads. We assign this emission to an aggregate. A more conclusive confirmation of the quenching of the fluorene chromophore luminescence at 77 K comes from lifetime determinations. Following excitation at 373 nm using TCSPC apparatus, lifetimes of 0.50 ns, 0.30 ns, and 0.70 ns were measured for BEF–Pyr , BEF–NB , and BEF–Phen , respectively, in TOL against a model lifetime of 0.76 ( Table 1 ). In MeOH, a BEF-OH lifetime lower than that observed at room temperature is measured, 0.92 ns against 1.30 ns. The reason for this anomalous behavior is unclear, but it might be due to an emitting state different from the one at room temperature. The luminescence lifetime of the dyads in MeOH is 0.79, 0.30, and 0.77 ns for BEF–Pyr , BEF–NB , and BEF–Phen respectively. This indicates that only in BEF–NB the quenching process due to electron transfer is still efficient. In the dyads, BEF–Pyr and BEF–Phen , the reduction in lifetime is modest and it is difficult to state whether the small variation represents a real, modest quenching or, rather, a change in the fluorene moiety lifetime, induced by a change in radiative/non-radiative decays of the excited state in different chemical environments of the dyads. Decreased, or even totally suppressed, quenching by electron transfer in glassy solutions is well known and is often used as an evidence to identify the quenching process, i.e. electron-transfer vs. energy-transfer. Whereas energy transfer is almost unaffected by the rigidification of the solvent, this change in the physical state prevents the solvent re-polarization around a polar state and increases the energy of the polar state formed upon charge separation. In general, except for highly exergonic reactions at room temperature, this is sufficient to decrease the driving force for electron transfer from the excited state to an extent that it is not feasible anymore.

Luminescence experiments at 77 K in a solid glass are also useful to establish the existence of emitting triplets. In order to do so, luminescence was detected using a phosphorimeter (see the Experimental section for details), which allowed us to detect delayed spectra, after the decay of the fluorescence. This technique not only permitted us to detect, after excitation at 370 nm, a weak phosphorescence from BEF-OH with bands at around 510 and 550 nm, but also to observe a rather strong phosphorescence with bands at about 570 and 615 nm in all dyads in TOL ( Fig. 5 ). Whereas for the BEF-OH model the emission spectrum was too weak to obtain a reliable excitation spectrum, the excitation spectra registered on the maxima of the delayed emission from the dyads clearly display a close resemblance with fluorene moiety absorption spectra (Fig. S38a, ESI † ).


Fig. 5 Arbitrarily scaled delayed luminescence spectra of the BEF-OH model and dyads in glassy TOL at 77 K. Excitation was at 370 nm and A 370nm = 0.1. The spectra were recorded after 1 ms delay with a gate of 4 ms.

Almost identical results, not shown here, were obtained for MeOH solutions of BEF–Pyr and BEF–Phen , but not for BEF–NB , where no triplet phosphorescence was detected. We identify the emitting species as a fluorene based triplet, bathochromically shifted in the dyads with respect to the one in the BEF-OH model for the different substituents present, and the slight charge-transfer character of the excited state in the arrays. The fluorene based triplet, 3 BEF–Pyr, 3 BEF–NB and 3 BEF–Phen, can be formed in the dyads at 77 K as decay products of the unquenched corresponding singlet excited state upon intersystem crossing. We can infer from the moderate fluorescence lifetime reduction that the latter is little reduced with respect to the model, except for the case of BEF–NB in MeOH, where no triplet phosphorescence was observed. However, formation of a triplet via the charge separated state (CS) recombination cannot be excluded (see below).

Upon excitation of the acceptors at 300 nm in TOL and at 270 nm in MeOH, an intense structured phosphorescence was detected for the acceptor Phen , with bands at around 410, 440 and 468 nm in both solvents, Fig. S38b (ESI † ). In general, phosphorescence lifetimes around 250–350 ms ( Table 1 ) were measured both for the fluorene chromophores and for the acceptor Phen . In some cases the phosphorescence decay is bi-exponential, suggesting the involvement of aggregates in the glass.

Transient absorption. The fluorene model BEF-OH and the dyads were examined after excitation at 355 nm by picosecond pulses, both in TOL and MeOH. Solutions of the BEF-OH model in TOL displays a structured band (with maxima at 680 nm and 740 nm) whereas the same sample in MeOH shows a single broad maximum at 675 nm with exponential lifetimes of 0.7 and 1.2 ns for TOL and MeOH, respectively ( Fig. 6 ). Based on the reasonably good agreement with the corresponding fluorescence lifetime, 0.75 and 1.30 ns, the species can be positively ascribed to the singlet excited state of the fluorene chromophore.
Fig. 6 Transient absorption spectra detected in TOL and MeOH solutions of BEF-OH at various delays after the pulse. In the inset, the time profiles at the selected wavelengths with the corresponding fitting are given.

Upon excitation at 355 nm of the arrays in TOL, the fluorene chromophore singlet excited state, 1 BEF-OH, was detected but rapidly decayed to produce a positive band between 500 and 600 nm with a maximum at around 540 nm, Fig. 7 . Absorption between 580 and 700 nm, depending on the substitution type, is reported in the literature for the fluorene cation. 24 In agreement, we ascribe the detected band to the cation of the fluorene chromophore. This is a confirmation of the occurrence of electron transfer, with production of the charge separated state formed upon LUMO–LUMO electron transfer from excited fluorene to the acceptor. The corresponding reduced radicals of the acceptor units, Pyr − and NB − , are not detected in the spectrum since their absorption bands (for Pyr − at 410 nm, 25 for NB − at 437 and 431 nm ( ref. 26 )) are out of the spectral range of detection of the pump and probe apparatus used. Only Phen − has an absorption band at 580 nm, 19a but overlaying the fluorene cation band, it is difficult to distinguish. In toluene solutions, the band of the cation disappears with lifetimes of 0.25, 0.18 and 1.6 ns for BEF + –Pyr − , BEF + –NB − and BEF + –Phen − , respectively. One can also notice the persistence of some free fluorene chromophore in the solution of BEF–Pyr , ( Fig. 8 ) very likely coming from a slight degradation of the dyad under high energy laser irradiation. In MeOH, the spectrum of the fluorene cation can be observed at around 520 nm, however its decay is faster than in TOL, ca. 30 ps for BEF + –Pyr − and BEF + –NB − , and ca. 60 ps for BEF + –Phen − (Fig. S39, ESI † ). The poor accumulation of a charge separated state, which decayed during the pulse (35 ps FWHM) and the lifetime close to the resolution limit, prevented a more precise determination. As already noticed for the luminescence quenching, i.e. the charge separation reaction, charge recombination is also faster in polar MeOH compared to TOL, and this can be explained by the thermodynamics of the processes as explained below.

We extended transient absorption determinations to the microsecond range, where information on triplet excited states can be derived. Optically matched ( A 355 = 0.8), air-free solutions of the arrays and the BEF-OH model in TOL and MeOH solutions were examined by laser flash-photolysis after excitation at 355 nm ( Fig. 8 ). The end of the pulse absorption spectrum of the model in TOL is characterized by two bands at 600 and 730 nm, with a lifetime of 8 μs. 27 In MeOH the main band is observed at 720 nm, and the 600 nm band is less pronounced. The lifetime is longer than in TOL, 28 μs. The presence of oxygen quenches the species at a rate of 4 × 10 9 M −1 s −1 in both solvents, in agreement with a triplet assignment. If we assume a similar molar absorption coefficient of the triplet in the two solvents, ca. 40% lower yield is noticed in MeOH than in TOL. As the fluorescence quantum yields in the two solvents are similar ( ca. 50%, see Table 1 ), this points to the presence of more efficient processes competitive to inter-system crossing in the polar MeOH solution than in TOL.

As far as the behavior of the dyads is concerned, BEF–Pyr did not display any transient absorption signal in the 10 −7 to 10 −5 s time range, both in TOL and MeOH. Under the same conditions, BEF–NB displayed a very weak transient absorption band maximizing at around 600 nm in TOL with a lifetime of 4 μs, absent in MeOH solutions. At the moment we are unable to assign the band and a more detailed study would be necessary to ascribe it. No signal is detected in a MeOH solution of BEF–Phen , whereas in TOL solutions, the end of the pulse spectrum is very intense, with a strong band maximizing at 730 nm, ( Fig. 9 ) similar to that detected in the BEF-OH model but with a much weaker shoulder at 600 nm. The species has a lifetime of 8 μs and reacts with oxygen at a rate of 4 × 10 9 M −1 s −1 , confirming its identification as a fluorene-centered triplet, 3 BEF–Phen. One might notice that, provided that the molar absorption coefficient of the fluorene centered triplet 3 BEF-OH is similar to that of 3 BEF–Phen, the yield of the triplet in the dyad is higher than expected. The quenching of the singlet excited state by electron transfer in BEF–Phen competes with intersystem crossing, and the resulting triplet should be reduced to 8%, according to the fluorescence yield reduction ( Table 1 ). This is clearly not the case here and the triplet appears to have a higher yield of ca. 130% than the model. The extra triplet can be produced by recombination of the charge separated state and a detailed discussion will be given in the next section.

Excitation of the acceptors Pyr , NB and Phen at 266 nm (18 ns pulse, 3 mJ per pulse) in MeOH solutions was also performed. A transient signal was only observed for Phen , Fig. S40 (ESI † ). We identified the triplet absorption of the phenacyl group at around 330 nm, which decays with a lifetime of 20 μs, in agreement with previous reports. 19a,28 A longer lived transient species with an intense maximum overlaying the one of the triplet at 330 nm and a weaker band at 500 nm was also detected. Based on literature reports, the species is identified as an intermediate formed upon proton transfer reactions, in protic MeOH. 28 The experiment on a TOL solution of Phen was not possible since the 266 nm wavelength is absorbed by the solvent.

Photoinduced processes. The photoinduced processes can be discussed with the aid of the energy level diagrams illustrated in Fig. 10 . The energy levels of the excited states are derived by the luminescence data obtained at 77 K ( Table 1 ) and are 2.90 eV for the singlet and 2.13 eV for the triplet localized on the fluorene unit of the dyads. Another triplet localized on the acceptor Phen at above 3 eV is not relevant to the present discussion, since it is higher than the relaxed excited state of the fluorene chromophore. The electrochemistry data ( Table 2 ) provide the parameters necessary for the determination of the energy levels of the charge separated (CS) state. The corresponding cyclic voltammetries are reported in Fig. S41–S47 in the ESI. † From the table one might observe that both the anodic and cathodic shifts of the potentials in the dyads with respect to the models are within 30–40 mV, confirming a very modest electronic coupling in the dyads.

The energy level of the CS state resulting from electron transfer from the fluorene electron donor to each of the electron acceptors in the dyads is calculated rather crudely in MeOH, simply from the addition of the energy necessary to oxidize the donor and to reduce the acceptor ( Table 2 ). In the polar solvent this leads to a CS state energy of 2.16 eV for BEF–Pyr , 1.88 eV for BEF–NB and 2.56 eV for BEF–Phen . In apolar TOL solutions the determination of the CS energy levels would imply the use of corrections, 29 however it has been shown that they largely overestimate the level of the charge separated state 30 especially when excited states with some charge transfer character, as the present ones, are involved. 31 The correction is also dependent on the geometry of the molecule, liable to large errors/uncertainties in a flexible structure as the present one. 29a Therefore, we feel that it is more appropriate to discuss the results on a qualitative basis, assuming for the CS state level in TOL a destabilization with respect to MeOH, due to the lower ability to solvate the CS state, of the order of 0.3 eV for all dyads. This would lead to CS energy levels of 2.46, 2.18 and 2.86 eV in TOL for BEF–Pyr , BEF–NB and BEF–Phen respectively. This is a rather simplified view, but well sufficient for the qualitative discussion of the results.

From the lifetime of the donor fluorescence quenching in the dyads ( Table 1 ), rate constants corresponding to electron transfer process 1 , charge separation, can be determined from the relation k 1 = (1/ τ ) − (1/ τ 0 ) where τ and τ 0 stand for the lifetime of the fluorene moiety in the dyad and in the model respectively. In MeOH the rates of charge separation k 1 are 6.6 × 10 10 s −1 for BEF–Pyr , 5.5 × 10 10 s −1 for BEF–NB , and 2.2 × 10 10 s −1 for BEF–Phen . These are all very high rates, justified by a driving force of 0.70 eV for BEF–Pyr , 1.0 eV in BEF–NB and of 0.35 eV in BEF–Phen for charge separation in MeOH. We can observe that whereas for larger driving forces there is almost no dependence of the rate of charge separation on Δ G (activationless regime) 32 some slight slowing down is noticed in the case of BEF–Phen characterized by the lower driving force, with Δ G CS = −0.35 eV and k 1 = 2.2 × 10 10 s −1 . From a formal point of view we observe charge relocation rather than charge separation upon electron transfer in the case of BEF–Pyr . However, to focus on comparison of the dyads in the current discussion, we choose to use terms “charge separation” and “charge recombination” with respect to BEF–Pyr as well.

In TOL, the experimentally determined k 1 values are 4.0 × 10 10 s −1 for BEF–Pyr , 5 × 10 10 s −1 for BEF–NB , and 1.4 × 10 10 s −1 and 1.5 × 10 9 s −1 for the two conformer populations of BEF–Phen in TOL. In an apolar solvent, where all driving forces are reduced by a value of ca. 0.3 eV, we can still notice an activationless regime for BEF–Pyr and BEF–NB , Δ G CS = −0.44 and −0.72 eV, respectively, while for BEF–Phen , the less exergonic reaction with Δ G CS = −0.05 eV, the rate remarkably decreases. The above behavior is typical for the so-called “Marcus normal region”. 32

In contrast, a “Marcus inverted region” behavior, consisting of the decrease of the reaction rate with the increase of the driving force, can be noticed in the charge recombination (CR) reaction 2. In MeOH k 2 is poorly defined – BEF–Pyr and BEF–NB have lifetimes of the order of the instrumental resolution (30 ps in the absorption mode), and a rate of ca. 3.3 × 10 10 s −1 can be calculated for both. Only BEF–Phen has a recombination lifetime longer, of the order of 60 ps, which allows us to calculate a k 2 value of 1.6 × 10 10 s −1 . The trend is however indicative of a reduction of the recombination reaction by increasing the driving force, 2.16 eV for BEF–Pyr , 1.88 eV for BEF–NB and 2.56 eV for BEF–Phen , in agreement with the “Marcus inverted” behavior. This is more evident in TOL solutions, where rates for charge recombination of 4.0 × 10 9 s −1 , 5.5 × 10 9 s −1 and 6.2 × 10 8 s −1 can be measured. Whereas in the first two dyads the recombination is to the ground state (step 2) with Δ G CR = −2.46 eV and −2.18 eV; in the case of BEF–Phen the remarkably high absolute value of Δ G CR = −2.86 decreases the back electron transfer reaction to an extent that the recombination to a triplet, step 3, becomes competitive. This is evident from the triplet yield in this dyad, which is approximately 1.3 times higher than the one in the model, Fig. 9 and 10 . This is made possible by the fact that, whereas in the model the triplet yield is, at most, 0.48 ( i.e. 1 − ϕ fl ) in the dyad, the new path of triplet formation, occurring through the CS state, is more efficient. The occurrence of recombination of the CS state to a lower triplet rather than to the ground state has been reported previously not only for heavy metal containing arrays, 33 but also for systems based on purely organic chromophores. 34 In one case it has also been observed in a very similar donor–acceptor dyad based on anthracene and the acceptor Phen . 19a

However, what is surprising in the excited state dynamics of the present dyads where donor and acceptors are well decoupled and separated by a long bridge is the fast rate of both the forward and back electron transfer. The latter is many orders of magnitude faster than that reported for closely related systems based on similar protecting groups. Lifetimes of the charge separated states of the order of 10 −7 s in acetonitrile have been reported for these systems, characterized by similar thermodynamic parameters. 19a In the absence of other explanations, we ascribe the poor performances of the present systems, in terms of the lifetime of charge separation, to the flexibility of the connector. This can allow, in spite of the large through bond distance, a close through space approach of the donor and acceptor reacting partners. A further development of the system will have to take this point in due consideration, in order to extend the CS lifetime so that the de-protection step can more efficiently compete with charge recombination.

Conclusions

We have elucidated the electron transfer dynamics in a series of model dyads designed for the photoinduced release of biologically active molecules upon two-photon absorption. The process is activated by electron transfer from the relaxed lowest singlet excited state of the donor, a fluorene derivative. Several acceptors, characterized by different redox properties, have been used to assemble the dyads. Photoinduced release of the active component is competitive with the recombination of the charge separated state formed upon electron transfer, therefore, high yield and a long lifetime of this state are desirable. Polar solvents, which mimic the physiological conditions, are found to increase not only the yield of charge separation but also the recombination rate. Between the acceptors examined here, the best performance is shown by the worst (in terms of difficulty to be reduced) electron acceptor, Phen . Though the corresponding dyad has a driving force of only 0.05 eV for charge separation in TOL, the process occurs with high efficiency (87%) whereas the highly inverted charge recombination process to the ground state (Δ G CR almost −2.9 eV) is retarded to such an extent that recombination to the triplet excited state of the donor is favored. However, very likely as a result of the structural flexibility of the bridge, charge recombination in this series is fast and consequently for the current dyads the potential to promote molecular uncaging with high yields is not expected. In the design of further systems the flexibility issue would have to be addressed in order to improve the performances.

Synthesis

The synthetic procedures towards model electron donor and dyads are reported in the ESI. †

Electrochemistry

Voltammetric measurements were conducted on an Autolab Eco-Chemie PGSTAT12 instrument using the following electrodes: a glassy carbon working electrode, a reference Ag/AgNO 3 electrode (double-frit design; inner solution, 0.1 M Bu 4 NPF 6 /0.002 M AgNO 3 in acetonitrile; and outer solution, 0.1 M Bu 4 NPF 6 in THF) and a Pt counter-electrode. The chamber containing the electrolyte solution (0.1 M Bu 4 NPF 6 in THF) was degassed by bubbling with N 2 . Potentials are reported using ferrocene/ferrocenium (Fc/Fc + ) as the internal standard. A scan rate of 0.1 V s −1 and a step potential of 0.10 V were used in cyclic voltammetry measurements.

Photophysics

The solvents used were spectroscopic grade toluene and methanol (C. Erba). Absorption spectra were recorded on a Perkin-Elmer Lambda 650 spectrophotometer and the emission spectra, corrected for the photomultiplier response if not otherwise stated, were detected using an Edinburgh FLSP920 fluorimeter equipped with a R928P Hamamatsu photomultiplier. Luminescence quantum yields of fluorene BEF-OH , ϕ s , were evaluated against a standard with a known emission quantum yield ϕ r by comparing areas under the corrected luminescence spectra by using the equation, ϕ s / ϕ r = A r n s 2 (area) s / A s n r 2 (area) r , where A is the absorbance, n is the refractive index of the solvent employed, and s and r stand for sample and reference, respectively. The standard used was air-equilibrated quinine sulfate in 1 N H 2 SO 4 with an emission quantum yield ϕ fl = 0.546. 35 Luminescence quantum yields of the dyads were determined against BEF-OH ( ϕ fl = 0.52 in TOL and ϕ fl = 0.48 in MeOH). Determinations at 77 K in glassy solutions made use of capillary quartz tubes dipped in a home-made quartz Dewar filled with liquid nitrogen and were performed using the same Edinburgh FLSP920 fluorimeter. The corrected phosphorescence spectra were registered upon excitation using a microsecond xenon flash lamp μF920H, after 1 ms delay and with a 4 ms gate. The phosphorescence lifetimes in the millisecond–second range were measured using the same instrument and elaborated using the standard fitting software procedures. Fluorescence lifetimes in the nanosecond region were detected using Time Correlated Single Photon Counting apparatus (IBH) with excitation at 373 nm. For picosecond time-resolved luminescence, a Hamamatsu C1587 streak camera equipped with a fast unit M1952 (2 ps resolution) was used after excitation with a Nd-YAG laser at 355 nm (Continuum PY62/10, 35 ps pulse, energy <1 mJ). The overall resolution after deconvolution is 10 ps; for more details, see previous reports. 36

Transient absorbance measurements in the picosecond range made use of a pump and probe system based on a Nd-YAG laser (Continuum PY62/10, 35 ps pulse, 10 Hz) and a Helios transient absorption spectrometer (Ultrafast) adapted for low repetition rate lasers. The third (355 nm) harmonic with an energy of ca. 2.5 mJ per pulse was used to excite the samples with absorbance around 0.5 in 5 mm cells. 36 Transient absorbance in the 10 −8 to 10 −3 s range was performed on flash-photolysis apparatus in air-free solutions, if not otherwise specified. The solutions contained in homemade, 10 mm optical path cuvettes were bubbled with argon for 20 minutes and sealed. Quenching rates by oxygen k q were derived from k q = (1/ τ − 1/ τ 0 )/[O 2 ], where τ 0 and τ are the lifetimes of the triplet in air-free and air-equilibrated solutions, respectively, and [O 2 ] is the concentration of oxygen in air-equilibrated solutions, 1.8 × 10 −3 M for TOL and 2.2 × 10 −3 M for MeOH respectively. 37 The apparatus was based on a Nd:YAG laser (JK Lasers) delivering pulses of 18 ns. The third (355 nm) or the fourth harmonic (266 nm) were used for excitation with an energy of 3 mJ per pulse. Triplet kinetics were measured with an energy of ca. 1 mJ per pulse in order to prevent undesired second order triplet–triplet annihilation reactions. The analyzing light was perpendicular to the exciting laser beam and probed the first 0.2 cm of the sample cell. Absorbance of the solutions at the exciting wavelength was 0.8. For further details, see elsewhere. 38

Estimated errors are 10% on molar extinction coefficients, quantum yields and emission lifetimes and 2 nm on emission and absorption maxima. The working temperature, if not otherwise specified, is 295 ± 2 K.
