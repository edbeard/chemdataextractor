An improved UHPLC-UV method for separation and quantification of carotenoids in vegetable crops

Highlights

•

Separation of the major carotenoids commonly found in fruits and vegetables in 13.5 min.

•

Separation of common carotenoid structural isomers including lutein and zeaxanthin.

•

Quantitation of carotenoids using relative response factors (RRF) to the internal standard β-apo-8′-carotenal.

•

Demonstrated utility across major commercial food crops: broccoli, carrots, tomatoes, corn, and lettuce.

Abstract

Carotenoid identification and quantitation is critical for the development of improved nutrition plant varieties. Industrial analysis of carotenoids is typically carried out on multiple crops with potentially thousands of samples per crop, placing critical needs on speed and broad utility of the analytical methods. Current chromatographic methods for carotenoid analysis have had limited industrial application due to their low throughput, requiring up to 60 min for complete separation of all compounds. We have developed an improved UHPLC-UV method that resolves all major carotenoids found in broccoli ( Brassica oleracea L. var. italica ), carrot ( Daucus carota ), corn ( Zea mays ), and tomato ( Solanum lycopersicum ). The chromatographic method is completed in 13.5 min allowing for the resolution of the 11 carotenoids of interest, including the structural isomers lutein/zeaxanthin and α-/β-carotene. Additional minor carotenoids have also been separated and identified with this method, demonstrating the utility of this method across major commercial food crops.

1. Introduction

Carotenoids are a unique and diverse class of plant pigments that are responsible for many of the bright colours observed in nature. Beyond their pleasing aesthetics, carotenoids play important and well documented physiologic roles in both plants and animals as reviewed by Cazzonelli (2011) . Carotenoids serve important physiologic roles in humans and may contribute to protection from chronic diseases. Of the approximately 700 carotenoids found in nature, there are six carotenoids commonly identified in human plasma, serum, and breast milk: α-carotene, β-carotene, β-cryptoxanthin, lycopene, lutein, and zeaxanthin ( Khachik, 2006 ). Provitamin A carotenoids, like α-carotene, β-carotene, and β-cryptoxanthin, which are enzymatically cleaved, serve as an important source of the essential micronutrient vitamin A as reviewed by von Lintig (2012) . Since carotenoids are obtained from the diet through consumption of fruits and vegetables, the U.S.D.A. nutrient database provides carotenoid concentrations of various food stuffs ( U.S. Department of Agriculture, 2012 ), serving as an important resource for agricultural and food industries, researchers, and consumers.

Carotenoid biosynthesis is a complex process, resulting in significant variation in content and identity depending upon the fruit or vegetable crop under investigation ( Fig. 1 ). Accordingly, crop breeders have taken an interest in targeted breeding efforts to increase the amounts of these carotenoids to enhance appearance and nutritional content in several key vegetable crops ( Eberhardt et al ., 2005 ; Thorup et al ., 2000 ). Knowing the identity and concentration of major carotenoids, especially in fruits and vegetables, is critical to furthering our understanding of their importance in diet and health. The most commonly used method for the identification and quantification of carotenoids utilises high-pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) combined with UV–Vis absorption detection. With the utilisation of HPLC, researchers were able to improve efficiency and accuracy of analysis by separating and quantifying individual carotenoids, offering significant improvement over previous open column methodologies ( Horwitz, 2005 ). Normal phase HPLC methods are not widely used for carotenoid separation due to poor separation of non-polar carotenoids, such as α- and β-carotene as reviewed by Khachik (2009) . On the other hand, reverse phase HPLC columns offer improved separation of xanthophylls and non-polar carotenoids due to better retention on the non-polar stationary phase. In combination with various mobile phases, the varying properties of C 18 and C 30 columns allow for different degrees of separation of geometric and positional isomers, with C 30 columns offering enhanced selectivity ( Oliver and Palou, 2000 ; Rivera and Canela-Garayoa, 2012 ). While offering resolution and selectivity, many HPLC methods can require 60 min or longer for a single run, resulting in costly and inefficient methods of analysis for large sample sets.

Ultra high pressure liquid chromatography (UHPLC) utilises sub-2 μm particles, increasing the number of theoretical plates in the column, and thereby increasing column efficiency, decreasing band broadening, and increasing resolution ( Rivera, Vilaró, & Canela, 2011 ). Application of UHPLC methodologies to carotenoids, tocopherol, and retinol analyses in forages, plasma, and milk was shown to improve peak resolution and sensitivity. However, run times were increased 10 min (36–46 min) over previously applied HPLC methods ( Chauveau-Duriot, Doreau, Nozière, & Graulet, 2010 ). Granado-Lorencio, Herrero-Barbudo, Blanco-Navarro, & Pérez-Sacristán (2010) on the other hand, developed and applied a 4.5 min method for the detection of vitamin A (retinol, retinol esters, and carotenoids), vitamin E (α- and γ-tocopherol) and 25-OH-vimatin D in human serum. Despite the improvement in run time, lutein and zeaxanthin, however, remained unresolved. Even with the potential of improved separation and decreased run times, many UHPLC methods developed for carotenoid analysis have fallen short of the potential in resolution and speed, especially across broad matrixes and carotenoid chemotypes, leaving room for improvement.

The importance of carotenoids in both plant and human health underscores the need to characterise carotenoid content and identity. Industrial study of carotenoids is typically carried out on multiple crops with potentially thousands of samples per crop. Due to the large volume of samples, a fast, efficient, and robust method for detection is needed. Herein, we describe the development and application of an extraction strategy and UHPLC methodology that is capable of separation and quantitation of the major carotenoids of interest in a variety commercially relevant fruit and vegetable crops.

2. Materials and methods

2.1. Chemicals and reagents

Acetone, acetonitrile, butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT), ethanol, glacial acetic acid, hexane, methanol, and sodium chloride were purchased from Fisher Scientific (Pittsburgh, PA, USA). 1-Butanol, chloroform, and petroleum ether were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). All reagents were HPLC grade or higher. A Barnstead Nanopure Analytical water system was utilised to obtain water of purity ⩾18.0 Ω-cm. All-t rans -β-apo-8′-carotenal (⩾96.0%), β-carotene (⩾97.0%), β-cryptoxanthin (⩾97%), lycopene (⩾90%), and zeaxanthin (⩾95.0%) were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). α-Carotene (⩾90%) was purchased from DHI Lab Products (Hoersholm, Denmark). Lutein (95.1%), neoxanthin (in solution), and violaxanthin (in solution) were purchased from Chromadex (Irvine, CA, USA). Phytoene (98%), phytofluene (95%), and antheraxanthin (95%), were purchased from Carotenature (Ostermundigen, Switzerland). Purity of standard compounds in solution is further determined by the spectrophotometer measurements (see Section 2.3 ).

2.2. Plant material and sample preparation

Broccoli ( Brassica oleracea L. var. italica ), lettuce ( Lactuca sativa ), carrot ( Daucus carota ), tomato ( Solanum lycopersicum ), and corn ( Zea mays ) samples were purchased from a local grocery store in Kannapolis, NC, USA. Approximately 100 g of sample was weighed and an equal amount of nanopure water (1:1, w/w) was added (except for tomato). Samples were blended in a Vitamix blender (Vitamix Corporation, Cleveland, OH, USA) for approximately 30 s on high. The puree was transferred to a 50 mL centrifuge tube, and sample extraction and analysis was either performed immediately or sample purees were stored at −80 °C for up to 1 month. All procedures were performed on ice, using amber glassware in reduced light where possible.

2.3. Standard preparation and sample extraction

Where possible, authentic carotenoid standards were obtained and used for generation of standard curves for the quantitation of carotenoids in sample materials. In the case of neoxanthin, phytoene, amd phytofluene, standard curves were not generated yet were identified in sample preparations and quantified using the relative response factor for β-carotene. Chlorophyll a and b were identified but not quantified to demonstrate separation capabilities of the developed methodology. Briefly, 100 ppm stock standards of the individual carotenoid compounds were made in the solvent listed in Table 1 . Absorbance of the stocks were read in triplicate using a spectrophotometer, ensuring an absorbance between 0.200 and 0.800. Extinction coefficients were used to calculate the exact concentration of the standard for use in preparation of the relative response factor (RRF). A 25 mL stock solution of 0.5 ppm β-apo-8′-carotenal (internal standard) in 90/10 hexane/acetone (v/v) was created and used for dilution of the RRF standards. Using the concentrations determined from the spectrophotometric assessment, a 20 ppm stock of a carotenoid standard mix was prepared. The standard mix was used for standard curve generation using concentrations ranging from 0.05 to 5 ppm for a 7 point calibration curve. Each standard was analysed in triplicate via the developed UHPLC method. The concentrations of the analytes were used to calculate the relative response factor (RRF) of each carotenoid standard to the β-apo-8′-carotenal internal standard. The RRF value was used in subsequent analyses eliminating the need for a calibration curve for every sample set analysed. Carotenoids without commercially available standards were tentatively identified by matching peak spectra to published literature spectral data and quantified relative to β-carotene using the RRF value determined for β-carotene. Sample extractions followed standard lipid/non-polar compound extraction strategies with slight modifications. Briefly, 0.5 g of sample puree was weighed into a 40 mL amber glass vial. To each vial 17 mL of acetone: methanol (2:1, v/v; 0.5% BHT, w/v) and 6 mL of hexane (0.5% BHT, w/v; containing β-apo-8′-carotenal internal standard) were added to facilitate the extraction of the non-polar carotenoids and more polar xanthophylls. Sample extracts were placed into a sonicator filled with ice water and sonicated for 20 min. To aid in phase separation and migration of carotenoids into the hexane layer, approximately 16 mL of cold 1 M sodium chloride solution was added to the sample vial. Samples were manually shaken and placed into a centrifuge and centrifuged at 1800 rpm for 10 min at 4 °C. An aliquot of the top hexane layer was taken, filtered, and transferred to an HPLC vial for analysis.

2.4. UHPLC separation and analysis

A gradient, reverse phase UHPLC method was utilised for separation and quantitative analysis of carotenoid compounds. The UHPLC system consists of an Agilent series 1260 quaternary pump, sample handler, and photodiode array (PDA) detector (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA). Carotenoids were resolved and analysed on a reverse phase C 18 column (Waters Acquity HSS C 18 2.1 × 100 mm, 1.8 μm; Waters, Milford, MA, USA) set at a temperature of 40 °C. The solvent system consisted of (Solvent A) 75:23:2 acetonitrile, water, and hexane (v/v/v; 0.1% acetic acid v/v) and (Solvent B) 90:8:2 acetonitrile, butanol, and hexane (v/v/v, 0.1% acetic acid v/v). New mobile phases were prepared daily. Preparation of Solvent A requires the initial combination of the organic constituents (acetonitrile and hexane) followed by the addition of water. Upon water addition, a slight cloudiness appears that resolves after gentle mixing and allowing the mixture to rest on the bench top. Glacial acetic acid is then added and the solution is mixed again and allowed to rest. Failure to add the water to the organic constituents will result in the formation of an emulsion and unacceptable use as a mobile phase.

A flow rate of 0.500 mL min −1 was used with the gradient program as follows: (1) a 0.5 min hold on 100% A, followed a 1 min linear gradient to 80% Solvent A; (2) a 0.1 min step gradient to 100% Solvent B followed by a hold to 8.5 min; (3) a 0.1 min step gradient to 100% Solvent A followed by a hold to 13.5 min. Carotenoid compounds were detected at 450, 280, and 350 nm with a 10 nm bandwidth and no reference.

2.5. Data processing and analysis

Chromatograms were processed using Agilent ChemStation® (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA, USA) software to integrate and identify peaks. Carotenoids were identified based upon relative retention time and UV absorption spectra in comparison to authentic standards. A calibration curve and linear regression equation were determined for each carotenoid standard, with the exception of neoxanthin, phytoene, and phytofluene. Carotenoids were quantified based upon generated relative response factors (RRF) using β-apo-8′-carotenal as an internal standard. Relative quantification was performed for neoxanthin, phytoene, and phytofluene carotenoid pigments using the calculated RRF values of β-carotene authentic standards. The RRF values were calculated by using the carotenoid and internal standard peak areas from a 7 point calibration curve run in triplicate. RRF = [( A c * C is )/( C c * A is )], where A c is the peak area of the carotenoid, C is is the concentration of the internal standard (0.5 ppm), C c is the concentration of the carotenoid, and A is is the peak area of the internal standard. The limits of detection (LOD) for each carotenoid was calculated by LOD = (3 * STE)/ S , where STE is the standard error of the linear regression equation and S is the slope of the linear regression equation. The limits of quantitation (LOQ) for each carotenoid was calculated by LOQ = (10 * STE)/ S , where STE is the standard error of the linear regression equation and S is the slope of the linear regression equation. The %CV (percent coefficient of variation) for each carotenoid was calculated by %CV = (STE/ S ) * 100 where STE is the standard error of the linear regression equation and S is the slope of the linear regression equation.

The percent recovery of the internal standard was determined by comparing the peak area of internal standard in a blank extract or a sample extract with that of the hexane and internal standard solution alone. Recovery was calculated by R (%) = ( A s / A b ) ∗ 100, where A s is the peak area of the internal standard in the extracted sample and A b is the peak area of the internal standard in the stock solution.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Mobile phase

A variety of mobile phases have been used to achieve carotenoid separation. The selection of mobile phase in combination with column characteristics is especially important to achieve quantifiable resolution of lutein and zeaxanthin. Previous UHPLC methods that have achieved separation of lutein and zeaxanthin have typically employed high aqueous concentrations ( Chauveau-Duriot et al., 2010 ), yet these methods have been limited in scope regarding food stuffs and carotenoids. However, to achieve broad separation and quantification of a number of carotenoids, a suitable extraction and separation scheme needs to be aligned, especially considering the breadth of chemical characteristics in carotenoids of agricultural interest. Due to the use of hexane as the final extraction solvent, a mobile phase of hexane, water, and acetonitrile was used to achieve peak resolution while maintaining miscibility and minimising peak broadening and tailing. Proper and careful preparation of the mobile phase allows for a homogeneous solution. Maintaining the concentration of hexane at 2% throughout the gradient continues to facilitate the solubility of the two mobile phases especially when gradient conditions return to water containing mobile phase A. The addition of 0.1% acetic acid to the mobile phases was found to improve peak shape by minimising peak broadening for all compounds. In the second half of the separation, chlorophylls A and B would overlap with lycopene and β-cryptoxanthin, respectively, depending on the mobile phase composition. Separations improved with alcohols compared to tetrahydrofuran as constituents. Methanol, ethanol, isopropanol, and butanol were all explored at varying concentrations. Butanol performed best at separating chlorophylls A and B from lycopene and β-cryptoxanthin, while maintaining separation, peak shape, and minimising the broadening of α and β carotene.

3.2. Chromatographic separation

The resulting chromatographic method separates a total of 11 carotenoid standards, including the structural isomers lutein/zeaxanthin and α-/β-carotene ( Fig. 2 ). The resolution of 0.5 ppm standards of lutein and zeaxanthin was found to be 0.840 and the resolution of 0.5 ppm standards of α-carotene and β-carotene was found to be 0.853, allowing for reliable quantitation of these compounds. In carrots, we achieved a resolution of 0.847 for α and β carotene ( Fig. 3 B). In corn, which contains high levels of the structural isomers lutein and zeaxanthin, we achieved a resolution of 0.908 for lutein and zeaxanthin using this method ( Fig. 3 C). Additionally, we achieved separation of interfering compounds, such as chlorophylls A and B, from carotenoids of interest ( Table 1 ). Moreover, the separation is achieved in a total run time of 13.5 min.

To demonstrate the applicability of the developed method five crops were analysed for carotenoid content: broccoli, carrots, tomatoes, corn, and lettuce ( Table 2 ). Data was not included for lettuce/leafy greens because it has previously been shown that many leafy greens have a similar carotenoid profile to broccoli ( Perry, Rasmussen, & Johnson, 2009 ). For green vegetables, such as broccoli and lettuce, adequate separation of the chlorophylls from the compounds of interest is needed for accurate and reliable results. Moreover, separation of chlorophylls from the carotenoids eliminates the need for saponification of samples for removal of chlorophylls. With our method, chlorophyll B was found to elute slightly later than the β-cryptoxanthin standard ( Fig. 3 A). While β-cryptoxanthin is rarely found in broccoli or leafy greens, β-cryptoxanthin remains distinguishable from chlorophyll B via retention time and absorption spectra ( Hegazi, Pérez-Ruzafa, Almela, & Marı́a-Emilia, 1998 ). In corn we detected a small amount of β-cryptoxanthin, which coeluted as a shoulder of an unknown peak. Previous studies have identified this unknown peak as α-cryptoxanthin ( Scott & Eldridge, 2005 ). Spectral analysis indicated our unknown peak as α-cryptoxanthin. The major carotenoids in tomatoes, including lycopene and β-carotene, were evidenced at 450 nm while both phytoene and phytofluene were detected at 280 and 350 nm, respectively ( Fig. 4 ). In mature green and breaker stage tomatoes, a chlorophyll isomer was found to coelute with the β-carotene peak. Detection at 480 nm may prove useful for β-carotene quantitation in these stages. We have also examined the carotenoid content of tangerine tomatoes and have successfully separated the major carotenoid compounds associated with tangerine tomatoes: 7,9,7′,9′-tetra-cis-lycopene, 9,9′-di-cis-zeta-carotene, and 7,9,9′-cis neurosporene (data not shown).

3.3. Method validation

The limits of detection (LOD), limits of quantitation (LOQ), and percent CV (%CV) for each compound are listed in Table 1 . The dynamic ranges of α-carotene, β-carotene, β-cryptoxanthin, and lycopene were all found to be linear from 0.05 to 5 ppm with correlation coefficients ⩾0.999. The linear ranges of antheraxanthin, lutein, violaxanthin, and zeaxanthin were found to be 0.1–5 ppm with a correlation coefficient ⩾0.999. Neoxanthin, phytoene, and phytoene were quantitated relative to β-carotene. Sample purees were found to be stable for up to 30 days when stored at −80 °C. Filtered sample extracts were found to be stable for up to 30 days when stored at −20 °C.

The percent recovery of the internal standard was found to be >97% in broccoli ( n = 5), carrots ( n = 7), corn ( n = 7), and tomato ( n = 7) compared to a blank matrix extraction. The instrumental %CV for 7 injections of a single sample was less than 1% for all carotenoids measured in the various crops. The extraction %CV for the various sample extracts ( n = 5 broccoli; n = 7 carrot, corn, and tomato) was less than 1% for the internal standard in all crops. The extraction %CV was less than 15% for all compounds detected in the analysed crops.

3.4. Carotenoid quantitative analysis

Retail purchased broccoli was found to contain β-carotene (8.47 ± 0.80 μg/g FW), lutein (13.02 ± 1.51 μg/g FW), violaxanthin (1.80 ± 0.35 μg/g FW), and lutein epoxide (0.81 ± 0.14 μg/g FW relative to lutein) as the major identified carotenoids. Neoxanthin, however, was not detected in our test samples ( de Sá and Rodriguez-Amaya, 2003 ; Guzman et al ., 2012 ). Our violaxanthin results are lower than previous reported values from Guzman et al. (2012) who reported 28.2–63.9 μg/g DW (approximately 3.6–8.3 μg/g FW), but are in agreement with those reported by Rodriguez-Amaya, Kimura, Godoy, & Amaya-Farfan (2008) . (1.5–2.0 μg/g FW in frozen samples). The β-carotene values reported herein align with previously reported values, which ranged from 44.5 to 73.5 μg/g DW (approximately 5.7–9.6 μg/g FW) ( Guzman et al., 2012 ). Our lutein values align with concentrations reported in the literature, ranging from 64.1 to 120.5 μg/g DW (approximately 8.3–15.7 μg/g FW) ( Guzman et al., 2012 ). There is significant variation in reported β-carotene and lutein values. Potential sources of variation can derive from preparation conditions with higher values reported in frozen samples ( Rodriguez-Amaya et al., 2008 ), but lower values reported in boiled or stir fried samples ( de Sá & Rodriguez-Amaya, 2003 ). Freezing broccoli samples prior to analysis may introduce more water to the sample, diluting the apparent carotenoid content. On the other hand, cooking broccoli removes water contained within the broccoli thereby concentrating the carotenoid content.

Retail purchased carrots contained α-carotene (45.00 ± 2.31 μg/g FW), β-carotene (53.58 ± 2.76 μg/g FW), and lutein (1.51 ± 0.09 μg/g FW) as the primary carotenoids identified. Lutein and β-carotene values aligned with reported literature values, ranging from 0.02 to 5.1 μg/g FW and 41.6 to 71.6 μg/g FW, respectively ( Koca Bozalan and Karadeniz, 2010 ; Niizu, 2005 ). Our α-carotene values, on the other hand, were higher than previously reported values, which ranged from 13.4 to 35.0 μg/g FW ( Koca Bozalan and Karadeniz, 2010 ; Niizu, 2005 ). While previous studies have reported a 2:1 ratio of β-carotene to α-carotene, the calculated ratio of β-carotene to α-carotene in our samples were approximately 2:1.6. While the exact varietal and growing regions for our samples is unknown, varietal and seasonal differences impact carotenoid content and could account for the differences in α-carotene values observed in our samples ( Koca Bozalan & Karadeniz, 2010 ).

Retail purchased frozen yellow sweet corn samples were found to contain 13.08 ± 0.50 μg/g FW lutein, and 6.20 ± 0.38 μg/g FW zeaxanthin, and 1.74 ± 0.15 μg/g FW of α- + β-cryptoxanthin as the major identified carotenoid compounds. While previous reports have also identified α-carotene and β-carotene in some corn varieties, we did not detect either carotenoid in the samples analysed ( De Oliveira and Rodriguez-Amaya, 2007 ; Monge-Rojas and Campos, 2011 ; Scott and Eldridge, 2005 ). Our value for α- + β-cryptoxanthin (1.402 μg/g FW frozen corn) was similar to values previously reported ( Scott & Eldridge, 2005 ). While the lutein and zeaxanthin values reported herein are elevated compared to previous reports of fresh, canned, and frozen corn, there is significant varietal variation in corn carotenoid content ( De Oliveira and Rodriguez-Amaya, 2007 ; Scott and Eldridge, 2005 ). Scott & Eldridge (2005) identified Golden Whole Kernel, as a medium pigmented variety while high pigment varieties are reported to contain between 11 and 30 μg/g FW total carotenoids.

Tomato carotenoids have been intensively investigated for their quality and health attributes. Genetics and growing season can have a dramatic impact on tomato carotenoid content. Accordingly, the red tomatoes used for our analysis were purchased from a local retailer during the winter months. Observed lycopene concentrations (16.98 ± 2.28 μg/g FW) were lower than previously reported values of 27.2 μg/g FW to approximately 145.9 μg/g FW ( Biehler et al ., 2012 ; Gama et al ., 2006 ). However, our values are within the year round ranges reported in the USDA nutrient database (15.95–46.31 μg/g FW) ( U.S. Department of Agriculture, 2012 ). In addition to lycopene, β-carotene (1.69 ± 0.17 μg/g FW), phytoene (1.57 ± 0.16 μg/g FW), and phytofluene (0.69 ± 0.06 μg/g FW) were also identified with observed values lower than previous reports ( Biehler et al ., 2012 ; U.S. Department of Agriculture, 2012 ). Other tomato carotenoids, including lutein, lycoxanthin, γ-carotene, ζ-carotene, zeaxanthin, lycopene, antheraxanthin, and β-carotene isomers, have been reported by other researchers ( Gama et al., 2006 ).

As interest in vegetable nutritional and quality attributes increases, there is an ever growing need for efficient and broadly applicable analytical methods. The extraction and chromatographic methods presented in this study enable rapid separation and quantitation of the major carotenoids commonly found in fruits and vegetables. The results obtained from the four crops analysed are concordant with previously published results. This method represents an improvement over previously developed methods and is applicable to screening programs for carotenoids in fruits and vegetables. Benefits of this UHPLC method include the cost savings as a result of lesser amounts of mobile phase solvents used and the increased sample throughput.
