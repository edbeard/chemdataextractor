Probing the interactions of structurally similar but chemically distinguishable organic solutes with 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium alkyl sulfate (alkyl = ethyl, hexyl and octyl) ionic liquids through fluorescence, NMR and fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (FCS) studies †

The rotational diffusion of two organic solutes, 4-(azitidine-1-yl)-7-nitrobenzo[1,2,5]oxadi-azole (ANBD) and 7-nitrobenzo[1,2,5]oxadiazole-4-amine (HNBD), has been examined in 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium alkyl sulfate (alkyl = ethyl, hexyl and octyl) ionic liquids with the aim of understanding intermolecular interactions among solute and solvent molecules. Solute–solvent interaction has also been investigated by employing fluorescence, NMR and fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (FCS). The ionic liquids (ILs) having a fixed cationic moiety are chosen to monitor the roles of the alkyl chain length as well as the solute–anion (sulfate) interactions in the rotational diffusion of the solutes in the given ILs. In the present study, two chemically distinguishable solutes with structural similarity in their fluorophore unit are employed so that the difference in the rotational behavior of the solutes, if any, can be correlated with the solute–solvent interactions, and not with other factors like the size of the solutes etc. The steady state absorption and emission data indicate that HNBD and ANBD exist in different microenvironments in both ILs. The rotational diffusion data for both the solutes have been analyzed in light of hydrodynamic and quasi hydrodynamic theories. Interestingly, the rotational dynamics of ANBD is observed to be relatively fast and shows slip hydrodynamics, whereas HNBD exhibits hindered rotation with superstick behavior. The hindered rotation of HNBD as compared to that of ANBD in both ILs has been explained by considering the strong hydrogen bonding interactions between the two N–H groups of HNBD and sulfate anions of ILs. The faster rotation of ANBD with the increasing anion chain length has been explained by invoking quasi hydrodynamic theory. The presence of a specific hydrogen bonding interaction between solute (HNBD) and solvent (ILs) molecules has been confirmed by 1 H-NMR experiments. FCS measurements have independently demonstrated the relatively strong association of HNBD with the present ILs as compared to that of ANBD.

1. Introduction

Intermolecular interactions between solute and solvent molecules have a profound role in determining the physicochemical properties of liquids and solutions. 1 An understanding of solute–solvent and solvent–solvent interactions in liquids and solutions has always gathered considerable attention from the scientific community. 2–12 In recent times, room temperature ionic liquids (ILs) have emerged as very special solvent systems 13 owing to their interesting physicochemical properties such as negligible vapour pressure, high viscosity, a high electrochemical window and thermal stability etc. 14–16 Intermolecular interaction among the constituents of ILs is expected to play a profound role in determining the attractive physicochemical attributes of ILs. It may also be mentioned here that ILs are microheterogeneous in nature 17,18 which itself on its own way can cause a significant variation in their physicochemical properties. Thus to understand the structure–property correlation of these specialized media both the intermolecular interactions and the microheterogenity prevailing in the ILs need to be realized in an appropriate manner.

Recently, studies on molecular rotation in light of hydrodynamic description have been performed successfully to study the solute–solvent and solvent–solvent interactions in liquids. 19,20 In these studies, generally reorientation time is correlated with the bulk viscosity of the medium. One of the approaches by which the solute–solvent interaction in liquids can be studied is to measure the reorientation time of the solute molecule in polar and nonpolar media under isoviscous conditions, and if the reorientation time significantly differs then the variation can be rationalized in terms of solute–solvent interaction. However, this approach has some limitations as the measured reorientation time will also depend on the size and free volume of solvent systems. 23,24 An alternative approach is to find/synthesize suitable structurally similar but chemically distinguishable solute molecules and study their rotational diffusion behavior in the solvents of interest and then the difference in the reorientation time can be ascribed to the solute–solvent interaction alone. Moreover, the idea of solute–solvent interaction will also help in understanding the solvent–solvent interaction in the liquids. Even though several literature reports are available on rotational dynamics in conventional solvents, a limited number of literature reports are available on the same aspect of ILs. 19–26

It may also be noted here that ILs are complex solvents due to the fact that their local microstructures are structurally heterogeneous unlike most non-hydrogen bonded conventional solvents. 27–55 Studies have shown that structural organisation within ILs arises due to their polar and nonpolar subunits. 31,32,47,49 It may be noted here that an idea about the micro-heterogeneous nature of ILs can be obtained by investigating the excitation wavelength dependent fluorescence behaviour of appropriate solute molecules in the ILs. 28,35,65 Solute–solvent interaction may significantly contribute to the inhomogeneous broadening of the absorption spectrum of the solute in a given solvent. 35 Due to the ground state heterogeneity and a slow excited state relaxation process in ILs a gradual red-shift of the emission spectrum with a change in the excitation wavelength can be observed. 35,65 Even though these studies cannot comment on the exact length scale over which the structural heterogeneity of the ILs can occur, they can certainly provide qualitative information about the heterogeneous nature of the medium.

Keeping the above facts in mind, two largely structurally similar but chemically distinguishable compounds 7-nitrobenzo[1,2,5]oxadiazole-4-amine (HNBD) and 4-(azitidine-1-yl)-7-nitrobenzo[1,2,5]oxadi-azole (ANBD) have been synthesized and their rotational relaxation behaviors have been investigated in 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium ethyl sulfate (EMIM-ES), 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium hexyl sulfate (EMIM-HS) and 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium octyl sulfate (EMIM-OS). These ILs are purposefully chosen, so as to have an appreciable variation in the alkyl chain length on the anionic moiety. It may be noted here that the alkyl chain length plays an important role in governing the physicochemical properties 25,58–60 and microheterogeneous 61–63 behavior of the ILs. Out of the two solute molecules, HNBD can form a hydrogen bond with the sulphate anion due to its acidic hydrogens (–NH), whereas ANBD cannot form such a bond as in the latter case the two hydrogen atoms are replaced by an alkyl moiety. 2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials

The ILs EMIM-ES, EMIM-HS and EMIM-OS were procured from Merck Germany (>99% purity) with <100 ppm halide and water. To avoid moisture, ILs were kept in a high vacuum before use. DMSO-d 6 (99.9%) and CDCl 3 were purchased from Across Chemical. Rhodamine 6G (Rh6G) was obtained from Sigma Aldrich.

2.2. Instrumentation

The absorption and fluorescence data were collected using a Cary 100 Bio spectrophotometer and a Perkin Elmer, LS 55 spectrofluorimeter respectively. A time-correlated single-photon counting (TCSPC) spectrometer (Edinburgh, OB920) was used to carry out time-resolved fluorescence anisotropy measurements. A 405 nm picosecond pulse diode laser (EPL) was used to excite the samples. The signals were collected at the magic angle (54.7°) using a Hamamatsu microchannel plate photomultiplier tube (R3809U-50). The quality of the fit was determined by monitoring the chi square ( χ 2 ) values and the residuals. The emission intensities at parallel ( I ∥ ) and perpendicular ( I ⊥ ) polarizations were obtained alternatively, until we get a peak count difference between parallel ( I ∥ ) and perpendicular ( I ⊥ ) decay (at t = 0) of ∼5000. A similar procedure was employed with 5 cycles and horizontal polarization of the excitation laser source for G -factor calculations. The anisotropy decay profiles were analyzed using the F900 software. The temperature was controlled by circulating water through the cell holder using a Quantum, North West (TC 125) temperature controller with ±0.2 °C accuracy bands. Viscosity measurements of the ILs were carried out using an LVDV-III Ultra Brookfield Cone and Plate viscometer (1% accuracy and 0.2% repeatability). The torque on the rotating cone is proportional to the shear stress of the fluid under investigation. Viscosity in centipoises units (mPa s) is calculated from the known geometric constants of the cone, the rate of rotation, and the stress related torque. A Bruker microTOF-QII was used to collect ESI-MS spectra. For carrying out 1 H-NMR measurements, a Bruker Avance 400 NMR spectrometer was used with tetramethylsilane (TMS) as an internal standard. The FCS measurements were performed by using a confocal fluorescence microscope, MicroTime 200 (PicoQuant). An excitation light of 405 nm was reflected by a dichroic mirror and focused onto the sample using a water immersion objective (60×/1.2 NA).

2.4. Synthesis of HNBD and ANBD

Synthesis of HNBD. Synthesis of HNBD was carried out by following the synthetic procedure proposed by Satyen et al. 64 Ammonia (1.2 mmol) dissolved in ethyl acetate was added drop wise in an NBD-chloride (1 mmol) solution in ethyl acetate with rigorous stirring in an ice bath. After stirring for 40 min in the ice bath, the reaction mixture was then stirred for an additional 2 h at room temperature ( Scheme 1 ). The compound was purified through the column chromatography technique using neutral alumina. Hexane and ethyl acetate (40/60) were used to separate the product. The compound was characterized by NMR and mass spectrometric studies.

Very dilute (∼10 −7 M) solutions of HNBD and ANBD in ILs were used for the fluorescence spectroscopy investigation. Again, ∼10 −9 M solutions of HNBD and ANBD in ILs were used for FCS measurements.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Steady state absorption and emission of HNBD and ANBD

The absorption spectral profiles of HNBD and ANBD in EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS are provided in Fig. 1(a) and 2(a) respectively. In both EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS, both the NBD compounds exhibit a broad absorption band. The broad absorption band for NBD compounds arises due to the intramolecular charge transfer (amino to nitro) nature of the transition. 64 For HNBD, the absorption maxima appear at 465 nm and 466 nm in EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS respectively ( Table 1 ). However, for ANBD, the absorption maxima appear at 495 nm and 489 nm in EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS respectively ( Table 1 ). These absorption data clearly demonstrate that the locations of the probes in these ILs are different. Nearly the similar absorption maxima for HNBD in both EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS indicate that the microenvironment for HNBD in these ILs is very similar. It is reasonable to expect that HNBD resides in a more polar region (near the sulphate group) due to the hydrogen bonding interaction between the acidic hydrogen atoms of the HNBD moiety and the sulphate group of ILs. A nearly 6 nm blue shift of absorption for ANBD upon changing the solvent from ethyl to octyl derivatives indicates that ANBD is located near the hydrophobic chain of the ILs, and the blue shift of the absorption in the case of a longer chain containing system arises due to the increase in apolar character upon increasing the alkyl chain length. 66

The emission maxima of HNBD and ANBD in EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS are provided in Fig. 1(b) and 2(b) respectively. The emission maxima of HNBD are found to be very close (533 nm) in these ILs ( Table 1 ). However, the emission maximum of ANBD is observed to be blue shifted upon going from ethyl to octyl analogue ( Fig. 2(b) ). This observation reflects that the locations of HNBD and ANBD are different in these ILs, and the blue shift of the emission band for ANBD arises due to the increase in nonpolar character upon increasing the alkyl chain length in the octyl derivative.

The excitation wavelength dependent fluorescence behaviour of a given solute in a particular medium often helps to get an idea about the heterogeneous behaviour of the medium. 25,68,69 If there exists a distribution of energetically different solvated species in the ground state and the rate of energy transfer between them is slow then instead of obeying Kasha's rule, 67 the emission spectrum of the fluorescent solute is observed to shift gradually towards the low energy region. This type of behavior is known as the red-edge-effect (REE). 68,69 Thus, this investigation can at least provide a qualitative idea about the micro-heterogeneous behavior of a given medium. 68,69 To investigate this aspect in the present case, excitation wavelength dependent emission spectra of HNBD and ANBD in EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS are monitored (Fig. S1–S4, ESI † ). The variation of emission maximum ( λ max em ) with the excitation wavelength ( λ exc ) for each system is provided in Fig. 3 . Interestingly, for HNBD, excitation wavelength dependent emission is found in both the ILs, when the sample is excited at a longer wavelength region (red region) of the absorption spectrum. However, no excitation wavelength dependent emission is found in ANBD in these ILs ( Fig. 3 ). Again, for HNBD, the maximum fluorescence shift is observed to be larger (18 nm) in EMIM-ES but relatively smaller (∼9 nm) in EMIM-OS ( Fig. 3 ). Here, we note that the solute–solvent interaction can play an important role in creating the distribution of energetically different species in a medium like IL. 35 The observation in the present scenario suggests that the hydrogen bonding interaction between the free amine moiety of HNBD and the sulfate moiety of ILs plays an important role in creating energetically different species in the ILs. Since ANBD is not capable of forming such interaction with ILs, no excitation wavelength dependent fluorescence is observed for ANBD. The changes in the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the emission spectra for both the solutes in EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS are provided in Fig. S5 (ESI † ). Interestingly, the FWHM values are estimated to be ∼3500 cm −1 and ∼2650 cm −1 for HNBD and ANBD respectively. This indicates that the probability of formation of energetically different species in the ground state is higher for HNBD than that for ANBD.

The anisotropy decay profiles of ANBD in EMIM-ES and EMIM-OS are provided in Fig. 5 . The rotational relaxation parameters for ANBD are collected in Table 2 . The anisotropy decay is fitted by a single exponential decay function. It is evident from Fig. 5 and Table 2 that faster rotation is observed upon increasing the temperature. This happens due to the lowering of the viscosity of the medium with increase in the temperature. Interestingly, the reorientation times ( τ r ) of ANBD are observed to increase with an increase in the length of the alkyl chain ( Table 2 ). The increase in τ r values with increase in alkyl chain lengths seems to be reasonable since viscosity increases with increase in the alkyl chain length. However, upon a more careful look, one can also see that even though the bulk viscosity is increased six times from ethyl to octyl, τ r increases only two times ( Table 2 ). For example at 298 K, the bulk viscosity values increase from 75 cP to 453 cP on going from ethyl to octyl, whereas τ r increases from 2.95 ns to 6.65 ns for the same systems ( Table 2 ). This observation indicates that the rotational motion of ANBD is not that much hindered in the octyl system as compared to the ethyl derivative despite the fact that the bulk viscosity is much higher in the former. It is an interesting observation where the size of the solvent molecule seems to be playing an important role.

In the above equation, V and η denote the van der Waals volume of the solute molecule and viscosity of the medium respectively. C and f are the boundary condition parameter and the shape factor respectively. The parameter f takes into consideration of the non spherical behavior of the solute molecule and the magnitude of C indicates the degree of coupling between the solute and the solvent, and it has two limit stick ( C = 1) and slip (1 < C > 0). k is the Boltzmann constant and T is the absolute temperature. 71–73

According to SED theory, solvent is considered as a structureless continuum, which is characterized by the bulk viscosity. At this juncture, it may be mentioned that SED theory is quite successful in explaining the experimental observations, when the sizes of solvent and solute molecules are comparable. 69,71 However, it fails to explain the rotational behavior of a solute molecule, when the size of the solvent molecule is larger than that of the solute molecule. Furthermore, rotational coupling constant ( C rot ) values, which measure the extent of solute–solvent interaction, have also been estimated for HNBD in these ILs ( Table 4 ). The C rot value ( Table 4 ) at a particular temperature for HNBD is found to be significantly higher than that of ANBD indicating that the rotation of HNBD is significantly hindered as compared to its alkylated analogue. As per hydrodynamic terminology, the hindered rotational behavior of HNBD in these ILs is known as super-stick behavior. This super-stick behavior arises due to the strong association between the solute and solvent molecules. The fact that the specific solute solvent interaction can contribute to super-stick behavior has been documented in recent literature reports. 20,21,78–81 The present observation clearly indicates that the hydrogen-bonding interaction between HNBD and the ILs is responsible for the observed superstick behavior for HNBD. Interestingly, hindered rotation is observed for HNBD in both series of ionic liquids, when we go from hexyl to octyl the C rot values are very similar ( Table 4 , Table S2 and Fig. S9, ESI † ). The relatively slow rotation for ANBD as compared to HNBD arises due to the absence of solute–solvent specific interaction in the case of the former solute molecule. This indicates that in a longer alkyl chain system e.g. hexyl and octyl a similar solute–solvent interaction is observed. Here we note that, Dutt while investigating the rotational behavior of 2,5-dimethyl-1,4-dioxo-3,6-diphenylpyrrolo[3,4- c ]pyrrole (DMDPP) in several ILs has demonstrated that the alkyl chain length has no bearing on DMDPP rotation. 20

As discussed earlier, since ANBD is devoid of acidic hydrogens and thus cannot participate in forming a specific hydrogen bonding interaction with the sulfate anions, relatively faster rotation is observed for it.

It may be mentioned here that Amendola and co workers 56 have demonstrated that the acidic N–H fragment of an organic dye molecule is capable of forming hydrogen bonding interaction with the anions and can be used as the receptor for the anion sensing event. 26,56 In the present system the charge density of the hydrogen atom (N–H moiety) is estimated to be fairly high (+0.345). This indicates that these hydrogen atoms are acidic in nature and can form a hydrogen bond with the sulfate moiety. The hydrogen-bonding interaction between the acidic hydrogens of HNBD and ILs is further confirmed through the 1 H NMR study. Fig. 9 shows the 1 H-NMR spectra of HNBD, EMIM-ES and HNBD in the presence of EMIM-ES. It can be seen that the N–H proton signal of HNBD appears at 8.86 ppm. Upon addition of EMIM-ES to HNBD, the N–H proton signal becomes broadened. The broadening of the N–H peak indicates the interaction between N–H and sulfate anions. Due to hydrogen bonding interaction the relaxation time (Δ t ) becomes very short, as compared to the NMR timescale, and therefore uncertainty in energy (Δ E ) and hence uncertainty in frequency (Δ ν ) become very large. This uncertainty results in the broadening of the N–H proton signal. 57 Solute–solvent hydrogen bonding interaction is further supported by FTIR experiments. FTIR experiments reveal that the characteristic N–H peak for HNBD appears at 3343 cm −1 . The peak is shifted to a lower frequency (3150 cm −1 ) region in the presence of EMIM-ES (Fig. S10, ESI † ). The observation indicates that the decrease in N–H peak frequency in the IR spectrum arises due to the hydrogen bonding interaction between the N–H of HNBD and the ionic liquid.

The solute–solvent interactions have been investigated further by employing the FCS technique. FCS is a powerful technique and capable of measuring the concentration and the diffusion coefficient of fluorescent molecules dissolved in a solvent. 82,83 As the diffusion coefficient depends on the molecular mass and shape of the diffusing fluorescent species, it would be possible to study the association/interaction between fluorescent solute molecules and the solvent molecules. 82–84 In the present FCS study, the interaction of both structurally similar NBD derivatives with EMIM-ES has been investigated independently by measuring the translational diffusion coefficient ( D ) of the solutes in the given IL. The observed correlation curves of HNBD and ANBD in EMIM-ES are provided in Fig. 10(a) and (b) respectively. The parameters obtained from FCS measurements are given in Table 5 . Interestingly, the translational diffusion coefficient ( D ) of HNBD and ANBD is estimated to be 0.64 × 10 −11 m 2 s −1 2.75 × 10 −11 m 2 s −1 , respectively, in the neat IL at 298 K. The lower diffusion coefficient of HNBD compared to ANBD in EMIM-ES indicates relatively stronger association of the former solute with the IL.

Therefore, from the analysis of both NMR and the FCS data we can now conclude that the hydrogen bonding interaction between the acidic hydrogens of HNBD and the sulphate moiety of IL is primarily responsible for stronger association between them. Since no such hydrogen bond is formed between ANBD and EMIM-ES IL a higher diffusion coefficient is estimated for ANBD.

4. Conclusion

Interaction of two structurally similar but chemically distinguishable organic solutes HNBD and ANBD with 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium alkyl sulfate (alkyl = ethyl, hexyl and octyl) ILs has been investigated by fluorescence, NMR and FCS techniques. Steady state fluorescence measurements demonstrate that the location of the two solute molecules is quite different in two ILs. Interestingly, for ANBD, faster rotation is observed upon moving from smaller to longer alkyl group containing ILs despite the fact that the longer system is more viscous than the smaller system. The faster rotation of ANBD upon increasing the alkyl chain length of the IL can be attributed to the larger size of the IL (octyl system) which offers a lower friction to the rotating solute. Time resolved fluorescence anisotropy decay studies also indicate that the rotational diffusion of HNBD is considerably slower compared to that of ANBD in both ILs. According to SED hydrodynamics description HNBD exhibits superstick behavior and ANBD lies between the stick and the slip limit. This observation indicates strong solute–solvent specific interaction between HNBD and the sulfate moiety of ILs. 1 H-NMR spectra confirm the hydrogen bonding interaction between the N–H hydrogen of HNBD and sulfate ions. The FCS studies have also demonstrated that the association of HNBD is stronger with EMIM-ES as compared to ANBD. Interestingly, decoupling of the rotational motion of the solute with medium viscosity has been observed in the case of a longer alkyl chain derivative. This observation has been rationalized by considering the micro-heterogeneous behavior of the medium.
