An artificial photosynthetic model based on a molecular triad of boron dipyrromethene and phthalocyanine †

A boron dipyrromethene (BDP) unit and its monostyryl derivative (MSBDP) were introduced at the axial positions of a silicon( IV ) phthalocyanine (SiPc) core. The absorption spectrum of this compound virtually covered the entire visible region (300–700 nm) and could be interpreted as a superposition of the spectra of individual components. The intramolecular photoinduced energy and charge transfer processes of this triad were studied using steady-state and time-resolved spectroscopic methods in polar and nonpolar solvents. Upon BDP-part excitation, a fast and highly efficient excitation energy transfer (EET) occurred resulting in strong quenching of its fluorescence and the formation of the first excited singlet state of SiPc or MSBDP. It was found that both EET and charge transfer (CT) processes competed with each other in the depopulation of the first excited singlet state of the MSBDP moiety. The former strongly superseded CT in nonpolar toluene, whereas the latter was dominant in a polar environment. Direct or indirect ( via EET) excitation of the SiPc-part of the triad was followed by CT yielding the charge-separated (CS) species BDP–SiPc˙ − –MSBDP˙ + . The energy gap between the CS state and the S 1 -state of the SiPc moiety was found to be only 0.06 eV in toluene, which facilitated the back CT process and resulted in the appearance of thermally activated delayed fluorescence. With increasing solvent polarity, the energy of the CS state reduced resulting in the disappearance of the delayed fluorescence in CHCl 3 , tetrahydrofuran or N , N -dimethylformamide. The charge recombination rate, k CR , was very fast in polar DMF (3.3 × 10 10 s −1 ), whereas this process was two-orders of magnitude slower in nonpolar toluene ( k CR = 4.0 × 10 8 s −1 ).

1. Introduction

There has been a tremendous interest in the development of molecular and nano-devices that can capture, store and utilise solar energy. 1 Inspired by the marvelous photosynthesis occurring in nature, artificial photosynthesis using synthetic mimics is one of the promising approaches. 2 A vast number of photosynthetic models with various photo- and redox-active building blocks have been designed and constructed to mimic the primary events in natural photosynthesis, including light harvesting, photoinduced multistep electron transfer and catalysis. The studies can deepen our understanding of these fundamental processes and enable the design of sophisticated systems to maximise the light conversion efficiency.

The efficiency of light harvesting followed by charge separation depends largely on the choice and molecular architecture of the chromophores and redox-active units. Whilst nature employs chlorophylls, carotenoids and quinones as the key components, porphyrins and fullerenes have been widely used in synthetic analogues due to their intriguing spectral and redox characteristics. 2a,3 As versatile functional dyes, phthalocyanines and boron dipyrromethene (BDP) derivatives are also potential candidates which exhibit strong, tunable and complementary absorption in the solar spectrum, possess high chemical stability and can undergo facile electron transfer and be chemically modified readily. Conjugates of these dyes with other building blocks, such as porphyrins, fullerenes and ferrocenes have been reported. 3a,4 However, studies of the BDP–phthalocyanine conjugates as artificial photosynthetic models have remained rare so far. 5–7

Recently, we have prepared two BDP–phthalocyanine triads in which silicon( IV ) phthalocyanine (SiPc) is axially coupled to two BDP or monostyryl BDP (MSBDP) moieties. 6 It has been found that charge transfer (CT) proceeds predominately to depopulate the first excited singlet state of the BDP or MSBDP moiety in polar N , N -dimethylformamide (DMF), whereas the probability of excitation energy transfer (EET) to the SiPc core becomes much higher for SiPc(BDP) 2 in nonpolar toluene. Both direct and indirect ( via EET) excitation of the SiPc moiety resulted in charge separation for SiPc(BDP) 2 in DMF and for SiPc(MSBDP) 2 in both DMF and toluene. This process is energetically unfavourable for SiPc(BDP) 2 in toluene. Based on these results, we have also designed a novel BDP–phthalocyanine pentad in which a SiPc core is axially substituted with two p -phenylene-linked BDP and MSBDP moieties. 7 This compound exhibits panchromatic absorption covering a wide region in the solar spectrum. Upon excitation at the peripheral BDP units, the energy is transferred to the MSBDP moieties, which is followed by CT leading to the formation of the charge-separated (CS) species (BDP–MSBDP)˙ + –SiPc˙ − –(MSBDP–BDP). Despite having an excellent absorption feature and almost unity charge separation quantum yield, this pentad exhibits a very fast charge recombination even in chloroform with a decay time of 27 ps. As an extension of these studies, we report herein a simpler, yet more efficient, model based on the triad BDP–SiPc–MSBDP, including its synthesis and photoinduced processes in solvents with different polarity as probed by various steady-state and time-resolved spectroscopic methods. 2. Experimental section

2.1. General

All the reactions were performed under an atmosphere of nitrogen. Tetrahydrofuran (THF), toluene and pyridine were distilled from sodium benzophenone ketyl, sodium and potassium hydroxide respectively. All the solvents used for photophysical measurements were of spectroscopic grade (Aldrich) and used without prior purification. Chromatographic purifications were performed on silica gel (Macherey-Nagel, 70–230 mesh) columns using the indicated eluents. Size exclusion chromatography was carried out on Bio-Rad Bio-Beads S-X1 beads (200–400 mesh). All other solvents and reagents were of reagent grade and used as received. Compounds 1 8 and 3 6a were prepared according to the previously described procedure.

1 H and 13 C{ 1 H} NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker DPX 300 ( 1 H, 300 MHz) or AVANCE II 400 ( 1 H, 400; 13 C, 100.6 MHz) spectrometer in CDCl 3 . Spectra were referenced internally using the residual solvent [ 1 H: CDCl 3 ( δ 7.26)] or solvent [ 13 C: CDCl 3 ( δ 77.0)] resonances relative to SiMe 4 . Electrospray ionisation (ESI) mass spectra were measured on a Finnigan MAT 95 XL mass spectrometer.

2.3. UV-Vis and steady-state fluorescence spectroscopy

All spectroscopic measurements were performed at ambient temperature (298 K). The ground-state absorption spectra were recorded using a commercial spectrophotometer Shimadzu UV-2501PC. Steady-state fluorescence spectra were measured in quartz cells (1 cm thickness) using a combination of a cw-xenon lamp (XBO 150) and a monochromator (Lot-Oriel, bandwidth 10 nm) for excitation and a polychromator with a cooled CCD matrix as the detector system (Lot-Oriel, Instaspec IV). 2.4. Time-resolved fluorescence spectroscopy

Time correlated single photon counting (TCSPC) technique in combination with scanning of the detection wavelength was used to acquire decay associated fluorescence (DAF) spectra. 10 The experimental setup was previously described. 11 Pulsed, frequency-doubled, linear polarised radiation of a Nd:VO 4 laser (Cougar, Time Bandwidth Products) with a wavelength of 532 nm, a pulse width of 12 ps and a repetition rate of 60 MHz was used directly for excitation of the samples or to synchronously pump a dye laser (Model 599, Coherent) tunable in the range from 610 to 670 nm. For excitation of the samples in the blue spectral region, the frequency-doubled pulses of a Ti:Sapphire laser (Coherent Mira 900, 400 nm, FWHM 200 fs, at a repetition rate of 76 MHz) were used. Optical density at the lowest-energy absorption band was less than 0.15 for all the samples. Fluorescence was detected under a “magic” polarisation angle 9 relative to excitation with a thermoelectrically cooled microchannel plate (R3809-01, Hamamatsu). The detection wavelength was chosen by a computer-controlled monochromator (77 200, Lot-Oriel). Electrical signals were processed by a PCI TCSPC controller card (SPC630, Becker & Hickl). The instrument response function was 45 ps, as measured at an excitation wavelength using Ludox. Data were analysed by a home-made programme by applying a variable projection algorithm 12 to the global fitting problem. The Nelder–Mead simplex algorithm 13 was used for optimisation of the nonlinear parameters, and the support plane approach 9 to compute error estimates of the decay times. Decay times and time shift were linked through all measurements of one scan sampled every 2 nm.

2.5. Picosecond transient absorption spectroscopy

The setup for pump–probe experiments with picosecond time resolution was described previously. 14 Briefly, to measure the transient absorption spectra, a white light continuum was generated as a test beam in a cell with a D 2 O/H 2 O mixture using intense 25 ps pulses from a Nd 3+ :YAG laser (PL 2143A, Ekspla) at 1064 nm. Before passing through the sample, the continuum radiation was split to obtain a reference spectrum. The transmitted as well as the reference beams were focused onto two optical fibres and were recorded simultaneously at different traces on a cooled CCD-matrix (Lot-Oriel, Instaspec IV). Tunable radiation from an OPG/OPA (Ekspla PG 401/SH, tuning range 200–2300 nm) pumped by the third harmonic of the same laser was used as an excitation beam. The mechanical delay line allowed the measurement of light-induced changes of the absorption spectrum at different delays up to 15 ns after excitation. Analysis of experimental data was performed using the compensation method. 15

2.6. Electrochemical studies

Electrochemical measurements were carried out using a Princeton Applied Research Potentiostat/Galvanostat Model 263A analyser. The cell comprised inlets for a platinum-sphere working electrode, a platinum-plate counter electrode and a silver-wire pseudo-reference electrode. Typically, a 0.1 M solution of [Bu 4 N][PF 6 ] in DMF containing the sample was purged with nitrogen for 20 min, and then the voltammograms were recorded at ambient temperature. Potentials were referenced to a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) using ferrocene as an internal standard ( E 1/2 = +0.38 V vs. SCE). 16

3. Results

3.1. Synthesis

Phenol-containing BDP 1 was prepared by treating 2,4-dimethylpyrrole with 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde via sequential condensation, oxidation and complexation reactions using our previously described method. 8 This compound then underwent a condensation reaction with 4-methoxybenzaldehyde in the presence of piperidine and acetic acid to give MSBDP 2 in 27% yield. Both 1 and 2 were then treated with silicon( IV ) phthalocyanine dichloride in the presence of pyridine in toluene to afford triad 4 in 20% yield. This conjugate possessed good solubility in common organic solvents and could be purified readily by silica gel column chromatography followed by size exclusion chromatography and recrystallisation. Compound 3 was another reference compound prepared by treating silicon( IV ) phthalocyanine dichloride with methyl 4-hydroxybenzoate as described earlier. 6a

The UV-Vis absorption spectra of triad 4 and the reference compounds 1–3 in toluene are shown in Fig. 2 . The spectra of 4 in CHCl 3 , THF and DMF are presented in Fig. S1 (ESI † ) and all the data are summarised in Table 1 . In toluene, compounds 1–3 showed a strong Q-band absorption at 503, 573 and 683 nm respectively. The absorption spectrum of triad 4 was essentially a superposition of the spectra of the individual chromophoric components with a shift of 50–80 cm −1 . Therefore, the three moieties within the triad are considered to be electronically decoupled in their ground states. As shown in Fig. S1 (ESI † ) and Table 1 , the solvent effect on the absorption positions is not significant. It is interesting to note that the absorption spectrum of 4 covers almost the whole UV-Vis region, which is an advantage for solar energy harvesting.

3.3. Steady-state fluorescence

As the absorption bands of the BDP, MSBDP and SiPc moieties in triad 4 were well separated (see Fig. 2 ), it was possible to record the steady-state fluorescence spectra of the triad upon selective photoexcitation of its components. Fig. 3 shows the fluorescence spectra of 4 in toluene upon excitation at the BDP, MSBDP and SiPc parts. The spectra of the reference compounds are also included for comparison. The data as well as those recorded in the other solvents are also compiled in Table 1 . It can be seen that for all the excitation positions and in all the solvents used, only minor changes were observed with respect to the position and shape of the fluorescence bands for 4 compared to those of the reference compounds.
Fig. 3 Steady-state fluorescence spectra of triad 4 in toluene upon BDP- (A), MSBDP- (B) and SiPc- (C) part excitation. The fluorescence spectra of the corresponding reference compounds are also shown for comparison (optical densities of the triad and the corresponding reference at the excitation position are the same). The normalised spectra are presented in the insets. For better comparison, the normalised fluorescence spectra of 2 and 3 (A), and 3 (B) are also included in the corresponding insets.

Upon excitation at the SiPc moiety of triad 4 , strong reduction of fluorescence intensity compared to the reference compound 3 was observed ( Fig. 3C and Table 1 ). The fluorescence quantum yield decreased from 0.60 (for 3 ) to 0.023 (for 4 ) in toluene. With increasing solvent polarity, the quenching of the SiPc-part fluorescence became stronger resulting in an even lower fluorescence quantum yield. The quenching ratio Φ 3 fl / Φ 4 fl was calculated to be 26.1 (toluene), 29.0 (CHCl 3 ), 64.4 (THF) and 78.3 (DMF) respectively. These results show that an additional and very efficient process exists to compete with fluorescence in depopulating the first excited singlet state of the SiPc moiety of triad 4 , and this process is promoted with increasing solvent polarity.

Upon excitation at the MSBDP moiety of triad 4 at 560 nm, the MSBDP-part fluorescence was largely quenched and a weak emission due to the SiPc part was observed ( Fig. 3B and Table 1 ). For example, the MSBDP-part fluorescence quantum yield decreased from 0.78 (for 2 ) to 0.004 (for 4 ) in toluene. The appearance of SiPc fluorescence suggested the presence of EET from the excited MSBDP to the SiPc core. The values of Φ fl were consistently lower than those obtained by direct excitation at the SiPc moiety in all the solvents ( Table 1 ). Based on the results of the triad SiPc(MSBDP) 2 , 6b it is believed that a CT process played an important role in depopulation of the first excited singlet state of the MSBDP moiety of 4 regardless of the solvent polarity.

Upon BDP-part excitation at 500 nm, the triad 4 in toluene showed weak fluorescence maxima at 512, 588 and 687 nm, which can be attributed to the emission of BDP, MSBDP and SiPc moieties respectively ( Fig. 3A ). This suggests the presence of EET from the singlet excited state of BDP to the MSBDP and SiPc moieties. The fluorescence quantum yields of BDP-, MSBDP- and SiPc-part emission were found to be very small compared to the corresponding values of the reference compounds ( Table 1 ). Similarly to the case of MSBDP-part excitation, the fluorescence quantum yield of the SiPc moiety was lower when it was excited indirectly ( via EET) compared to its direct excitation.

To obtain more information about the various energy transfer processes, the excitation spectrum of 4 was also recorded by monitoring the SiPc emission at 700 nm. As shown in Fig. 4 , the spectrum exhibits two pronouncing bands at ca. 500 and 680 nm corresponding to the absorption of BDP and SiPc moieties respectively. The excitation band at ca. 570 nm belonging to the MSBDP unit is much weaker compared to its absorption as shown in the absorption spectrum of the triad. These results suggest that the first excited singlet state of the SiPc moiety is populated mainly via energy transfer from the initially photoexcited BDP moiety.

The fluorescence kinetics of triad 4 were examined using three different excitation wavelengths, namely 400, 532 and 620 nm, corresponding to selective excitation of the BDP-, MSBDP- and SiPc-parts respectively. It turned out that the fluorescence lifetimes of the BDP- and MSBDP-parts of the triad lie below the 10 ps detection limit of the setup. 3.5. Picosecond transient absorption

To gather additional evidence of non-fluorescent intermediates such as the CS and triplet states, transient absorption (TA) spectra of these compounds were also recorded. As expected, the recovery of the ground state population of 1–3 occurred mono-exponentially with characteristic times that are consistent with their corresponding fluorescence lifetimes. Using the compensation method, 15 the intersystem crossing (ISC) S 1 → T 1 quantum yield, Φ ISC , was found to be virtually zero for BDP 1 and MSBDP 2 in all the solvents, and ca. 0.3 for SiPc 3 . It was also found that the spectral features and kinetics of the TA spectra of triad 4 were independent of the excitation wavelength. Fig. 6 shows the TA spectra and decay of the SiPc ground state bleaching of 4 in toluene and THF upon selective excitation at the MSBDP moiety. The TA spectra of 4 in toluene, CHCl 3 and DMF upon excitation at the BDP-, MSBDP- and SiPc-parts are presented in Fig. S2 (ESI † ).
Fig. 6 ΔOD spectra of triad 4 in toluene (A) and THF (C) at different delay times after excitation as well as the normalised SiPc ground state bleaching signal and its mono-exponential fit of triad 4 in toluene (B) and THF (D). Excitation was made at 550 nm (MSBDP-part excitation).

Regardless of the excitation position, two sets of TA minima were observed at 570 and 680 nm, which resembled the ground state absorption of MSBDP and SiPc moieties respectively. Between these negative bands, the ΔOD spectra exhibited a pronounced induced absorption which can be assigned to the CS state MSBDP˙ + –SiPc˙ − as reported previously for the triad SiPc(MSBDP) 2 . 6 The SiPc ground state population recovered mono-exponentially in 1.7 ns, 0.34 ns, 100 ps and 30 ps in toluene, CHCl 3 , THF and DMF respectively. These recovery times were much slower compared to the fluorescence lifetimes of the SiPc moiety of 4 measured in the corresponding solvents ( Table 1 ). Obviously, the energy was accumulated in the CS state. It is worth noting that the spectra lack any trace of BDP-part transient absorption. It seems that the BDP moiety is not involved in the formation of the CS state. In toluene and CHCl 3 , the negative ΔOD signal at 680 nm (which belongs to the SiPc-part ground state depletion) was observed even at delay times in the order of 15 ns after excitation (see Fig. 6A and B as examples). This can be ascribed to population of the first excited triplet state 3 SiPc, which has an energy of ca. 1.26 eV. 17 In toluene, the quantum yield of triplet state formation was found to be 0.37 and this value is somewhat greater than that of SiPc 3 (0.32). With increasing solvent polarity, the quantum yield of the triplet state population was reduced to 0.07 in CHCl 3 , 0.01 in THF and 0 in DMF.

The electrochemical data for triad 4 in DMF were obtained by cyclic voltammetry. The compound exhibited three reductions at −0.56, −1.11 and −1.40 V and two oxidations at 0.87 and 1.12 V. 4. Discussion

The UV-Vis and fluorescence spectra of triad 4 can be well described by a superposition of the individual components in both polar and nonpolar solvents. The shapes of all the absorption bands are only slightly altered. Moreover, the spectral shifts are comparably small. For this reason, it is assumed that the molecular parts BDP, MSBDP and SiPc of the triad are electronically decoupled in their ground state as well as in their first excited state. It follows that the molecular parts can be described as independent of each other. Due to the edge-to-face linkage, the polarity of the solvent has only a minor influence on the molecular geometry of the triad. Thus, it is expected that the effects of solvent on these aspects are insignificant.

In line with our previously reported results, 6,7 two different interactions between the BDP, MSBDP and SiPc components are taken into account, namely photoinduced EET and CT. The former is first discussed below.

4.1. Excitation energy transfer

In steady-state fluorescence experiments, the SiPc-part fluorescence was observed upon excitation at the BDP- or MSBDP-part of 4 . In addition, the MSBDP-part fluorescence was also seen after excitation at the BDP moiety. Hence, EET is expected to be responsible for these observations. The separation between the BDP (or MSBDP) unit and the SiPc core is approximately 6 Å 6 and the distance between the BDP and MSBDP moieties in triad 4 was estimated to be 15 Å. The covalent edge-to-face linkage prevents the close contact between the chromophores within the triad, allowing them to orient almost in an orthogonal manner. It follows that the probability of an exchange energy transfer is likely to be small. Moreover, a broad spectral overlap between the emission of the BDP (or MSBDP) and the absorption of SiPc exists, which is required for the fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) mechanism. Thus, FRET is assumed to be the dominant EET process.

4.2. Charge transfer

The electrochemical data for the three reference compounds, namely BDP 1 , MSBDP 2 and SiPc 3 in DMF were reported previously. 6b They exhibit the first quasi-reversible reduction at −1.21, −1.12 and −0.57 V and the first irreversible oxidation at 1.05, 0.92 and 1.13 V (all vs. SCE) respectively. By comparing the redox potentials of triad 4 with these values (Table S1, ESI † ), it can be concluded that the first reduction of 4 at −0.56 V occurs mainly at the SiPc core, while the first oxidation at 0.87 V occurs at the MSBDP unit. Therefore, for triad 4 , the MSBDP moiety is easier to be oxidised than the SiPc core, while the latter is easier to be reduced. In other words, the HOMO lies in the MSBDP moiety, while the LUMO centres in the SiPc core of 4 . Hence, if an intramolecular electron-transfer process occurs in 4 , it will be from the MSBDP to SiPc moieties.

5. Conclusions

An interesting triad of BDP, MSBDP and SiPc (compound 4 ) has been prepared. It acts as an excellent light harvester with broad UV-Vis absorption ranging from 300 to 700 nm. The photophysical properties of the triad are strongly affected by two different types of interactions between the three components, namely EET and CT ( Fig. 7 ). Upon BDP-part excitation, fast and very efficient EET delivers the excitation energy to the SiPc or MSBDP moieties resulting in strong quenching of the BDP-part fluorescence. It has been found that both EET and CT processes compete with each other in depopulation of the first excited singlet state of the MSBDP moiety. The former strongly supersedes the latter in nonpolar toluene, whereas the latter is dominant in the polar environment. Direct or indirect ( via EET) excitation of the SiPc-part of the triad is followed by CT to form the CS state. The energy gap between the CS state and the S 1 -state of the SiPc moiety is very small in toluene ( ca. 0.06 eV) facilitating the back CT process. The latter leads to the appearance of thermally activated delayed fluorescence. With increasing solvent polarity, the energy of the CS state reduces and as a result the delayed fluorescence disappears in CHCl 3 , THF and DMF. The CR of the generated CS state BDP–SiPc˙ − –MSBDP˙ + occurs slowly in nonpolar toluene ( k CR = 4.0 × 10 8 s −1 ), whereas the lifetime of the CS state decreases with increasing solvent polarity.
