Copper( II ) complexes with peptides based on the second cell binding site of fibronectin: metal coordination and ligand exchange kinetics †

Copper( II ) complexes with short peptides based on the second cell binding site of fibronectin, PHSFN and PHSEN, have been characterized by potentiometric, UV-vis, CD, EPR and NMR spectroscopic methods. The histidine imidazole nitrogen is the anchoring site for the metal ion binding. Thermodynamic and spectroscopic evidence is given that the side chain oxygen donor atom of glutamyl residue in Ac-PHSEN-NH 2 is also involved in the binding up to physiological pH. To determine ligand exchange kinetic parameters after the imidazole nitrogen anchoring, proton relaxation enhancement NMR data have been collected for the two hydrogen atoms of the imidazole ring in the temperature range 293–315 K at pH 5.2 and globally treated within different kinetic models for ligand exchange. The best fitting model involves two steps. In the first one, which is slow, a water molecule disengages a carbonyl or a carboxylate group coordinated to the metal ion in the complex formed by PHSFN or PHSEN, respectively. This stage is one order of magnitude slower for PHSEN, due to entropic effects. In the second step, which is fast, the complex just formed exchanges with the ligand. In this step, no appreciable differences are found for the two cases examined.

1. Introduction

Fibronectin is a modular glycoprotein ubiquitously expressed in the extracellular matrix. It plays a critical role in the adhesion to cell-surface integrin receptors during development, tissue repair and angiogenesis. 1 Besides a principal cell-binding domain involving the sequence Arg–Gly–Asp (RGD), 2 another sequence, Pro–His–Ser–Arg–Asn (PHSRN), has been identified as a second cell binding site and it is supposed to act synergically or in competition with the RGD sequence. 3 This sequence contains the prolyl-histidyl (PH) dyad, which is reminiscent of that present in other proteins such as prion proteins 4 and the Wilson's and Menkes' ATPases. 5 The peptide PHSRN is able to induce human microvascular endothelial cell invasion and initiate angiogenesis, while the single point mutated (Arg → Cys) PHSCN peptide acts as a competitive inhibitor of PHSRN. 6 PHSRN has been immobilized on micro- and nano-patterned surfaces as biomimetic platforms to support cell adhesion, spreading, migration, growth, and differentiation. 7,8 The single point mutated forms PHSEN and PHSFN adsorbed on hydrophilic silica-based surfaces showed anti-proliferative activity compared to fibronectine and to PHSRN, although for PHSFN the effect is much more pronounced. 9

Copper ions are able to modulate the activity of many peptides and influence protein interaction with specific cellular receptors. 10–13 In particular, copper is involved in angiogenic processes similar to those mentioned for fibronectin, being a potent angiogenic factor. However, the molecular and cellular mechanism by which this action takes place is still unclear. 11 It has been hypothesized that copper interacts with several angiogenic factors and this may modulate its angiogenic activity. The full understanding of copper( II ) coordination environment with fibronectin may give an indication on the biological role of the metal and allow the design of inhibitors or agonists of fibronectin and the development of new drugs. Not many studies have been carried out on the direct interaction of copper with fibronectin or related fragments. In a study focused on biomaterials, it has been shown that three-dimensional fibronectin mats and cables are stabilized by micromolar amounts of copper( II ), supporting better Schwann cells growth, involved in the peripheral nerve repair, and then suggesting a potential direct interaction between the metal and the protein. 14 In a molecular level study, the copper( II ) complexes formed by the previously mentioned PHSRN fragment were identified. 15 Evidence was provided on the binding of copper to one imidazole and to two amide nitrogen atoms at physiological pH. In this work we report on the copper coordination features and ligand exchange kinetics of the single point mutated forms PHSEN and PHSFN, blocked at the N- and C-termini ( Fig. 1 ). Glutamic acid (E) was chosen to explore the possible role of a carboxylate group attached to a side chain long enough to favour the coordination to the ion, while phenylalanine (F) was selected to highlight the role of any hydrophobic and/or aromatic interactions. We intend to assess the binding and kinetic parameters after the metal ion is bound to the peptide anchoring site and the possible role of side chain residues. These can favour the formation of copper( II ) complex species with different coordination environments and, in perspective, with potential different activities. In fact, the structure of the metal complexes is strongly dependent on the primary peptide sequence and on the presence of some side chain donor functions. 16 The charged carboxylate group carried by the glutamic acid residue and the phenylalanine hydrophobic side chain might result not only in different binding features but also in different exchange kinetics.

The coordination features of the complexes Cu–PHSFN and Cu–PHSEN were characterized by potentiometric and UV-visible, Circular Dichroism (CD), Electron Paramagnetic Resonance (EPR) and Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopic methods. These techniques allowed us to identify different copper coordination types at different pH values and to characterize the thermodynamic properties of the complexes.

The ligand exchange kinetics of the two complexes was investigated using proton NMR relaxation enhancement data. In the more recent literature, 17–21 this type of data was used to obtain distances between different protons on the ligand and the copper ion. Alternatively, the relaxation enhancement of the solvent water protons at different temperatures and magnetic fields has been used to study the structure of the hydration spheres and the exchange between different spheres in metal complexes, 22 including copper–amino-acid complexes. 23 In the present study, we use relaxation enhancement data to obtain kinetic parameters, relying on known distances. The global treatment of these data collected for the two hydrogen atoms of the imidazole ring at temperatures ranging between 293 and 315 K for two peptide/copper ratios allowed us to discriminate among different kinetic models and to obtain kinetic parameters. A mechanism for the ligand exchange has been proposed on the basis of the parameters found.

2. Materials and methods

2.1 Materials

N -Fluorenylmethoxycarbonyl (Fmoc)-protected amino-acids, 2-(1- H -benzotriazole-1-yl)-1,1,3,3-tetramethyluronium tetrafluoroborate (TBTU), and TGR resin were obtained from Novabiochem (Switzerland); N , N -dimethylformamide (DMF) peptide synthesis grade, N , N -diisopropyl-ethylamine (DIPEA), N -hydroxybenzotriazole (HOBT), piperidine, triisopropylsilane (TIS), trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), and acetic anhydride were purchased from Sigma/Aldrich. All the other chemicals (chloroform, diethyl ether, acetic acid, tert -amyl alcohol) were of the highest available grade and were used without further purification.

2.2 Peptide synthesis and purification

The peptides PHSFN and PHSEN were synthesized in the N -acetylated and C-amidated form to avoid end-group effects and to better mimic the situation when they are internal fragments of proteins. Peptides were prepared by Fmoc solid-phase peptide synthesis on a Biotage initiator + AlstraTM fully automated microwave peptide synthesizer. Syntheses were carried out on TGR resin (0.25 mmol g −1 ) on 0.11 mmol scale using a 30 mL reactor vial. Fmoc deprotection steps were performed at room temperature with piperidine/DMF solution for 15 minutes. Peptide couplings were performed using 5 equivalents of amino acid, 5 equivalents of HOBT/TBTU/DIPEA in DMF. A coupling time of 10 minutes at room temperature was employed. The N-terminal amino group was acetylated with a solution of acetic anhydride (6% v/v), DIPEA (5% v/v) in DMF. After the synthesis was completed, the resin was washed with dichloromethane and thoroughly dried. Other experimental details are as previously reported. 9 The peptides were purified by means of a preparative reversed-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (rp-HPLC) on a Varian PrepStar 200 model SD-1 chromatography system equipped with a Prostar photodiode array detector with detection at 222 nm. The peptide was eluted with solvent A (0.1% TFA in water) and B (0.1% TFA in acetonitrile) on a Vydac C 18 250 × 22 mm (300 Å pore size, 10–15 μm particle size) column, at a flow rate of 10 mL min −1 . Analytical rp-HPLC analyses were performed using a Waters 1525 instrument, equipped with a Waters 2996 photodiode array detector with detection at 222 nm.

The peptides were eluted according to the following protocol: from 0 to 5 minutes isocratic gradient in 0% B, then linear gradient from 0 to 15% B over 15 min, finally isocratic gradient in 15% B from 20 to 30 minutes.

The peptides were characterized by means of Electron Spray Ionization Mass Spectrometry (ESI-MS).

2.3 Potentiometric titrations

Potentiometric titrations were performed with a home-assembled fully automated apparatus set (Metrohm E654 pH-meter, combined micro pH glass electrode Orion 9103SC and Hamilton digital dispenser (Model 665)) controlled by the appropriate software set up in our laboratory. The titration cell (2.5 mL) was thermostated at 298.0 ± 0.2 K, and all solutions were kept under an atmosphere of argon, which was bubbled through a solution having the same ionic strength and temperature as the measuring cell. KOH solutions (0.1 M) were added through a Hamilton buret equipped with 1 cm 3 syringes. The ionic strength of all solutions was adjusted to 0.10 M (KNO 3 ). In order to determine the stability constants, solutions of the ligands (protonation constants) or the ligands with Cu 2+ (copper complex constants) were titrated by using 0.1 M sodium hydroxide. In order to minimize the error, different ligand concentrations were used ranging from 1.4 to 3.0 × 10 −3 and from 2.0 to 3.0 × 10 −3 M for the protonation and complexation experiments, respectively. Metal to ligand ratios between 2 : 1 and 1 : 1 were employed.

A minimum of three independent runs were performed to determine the protonation constants, while four independent experiments were run for the copper( II ) complexation constants.

The initial pH was always adjusted to 2.4 and the final was 11. To verify reproducibility, the electrode potential values of each experiment were taken at different time intervals. Other details were as previously reported. 24 To obtain protonation and complexation constants, the potentiometric data were refined using the Hyperquad program. 25

The species distribution as a function of the pH was obtained by using the computer program Hyss. 26

2.4 UV-vis measurements

UV-vis spectra were recorded at 25 °C, by using an Agilent 8453 or a Varian Cary 500 spectrophotometer. The concentrations of the peptides and copper( II ) used to record absorption spectra were the same as those for the potentiometric titrations. Spectroscopic metal-complex titrations were performed into a 3 mL quartz cuvette with a 1 cm path length to get the spectrum in the visible region at each pH value. These experiments were replicated at least three times for each copper–peptide system. Spectroscopic data were processed by using the Hyperquad program. 25

2.5 CD measurements

CD spectra in the 280–800 nm range were obtained at 25 °C under a constant flow of nitrogen on a Jasco model 810 spectropolarimeter at a scan rate of 50 nm min −1 and a resolution of 0.1 nm, the path length being 1 cm. The copper( II ) ion and the peptide concentrations used for the acquisition of the CD spectra were identical to those used in the potentiometric titrations. The results are reported as Δ ε (molar dichroic coefficient) in M −1 cm −1 .

2.6 EPR experiments

A Varian (Palo Alto, CA, USA) E112 spectrometer operating at the X band and interfaced to an IPC 610/P566C industrial grade Advantech computer by means of an acquisition board 27 and a software package especially designed for EPR experiments 28 was used throughout this work. The EPR spectra of frozen solutions of copper( II ) complexes were recorded at 140 K using a Varian E257 temperature control unit. A microwave power of 40 mW, a modulation amplitude of 3.2 G and a modulation frequency of 100 kHz were used. In all the samples the peptide concentration was around 1 mM and the peptide/Cu( II ) ratio was 1, with copper being the isotopically pure 63 Cu isotope. The pH was adjusted to 5.2, 7.0 and 10.7 by the addition of HCl or NaOH aqueous solutions. Moreover, methanol was added at a 10% v/v concentration to ensure homogeneity of the frozen samples. The EPR magnetic parameters, i.e. the parallel component of the g tensor, g ‖ , and the hyperfine splitting arising from the 63 Cu nuclear spin, A ‖ , were obtained directly from the spectra.

2.7 NMR experiments

Peptides were dissolved in D 2 O and the pD was adjusted to 5.2 with DCl. The pD was calculated adding 0.40 to the pH reading. 29 The PHSFN and PHSEN peptide concentrations were 29.7 and 33.6 mM, respectively. The desired concentration of copper ions was achieved by using a stock solution of copper sulfate. We prepared some solutions with peptide/Cu molar ratios ranging between 187 and 5.5 for PHSFN (five solutions) and 56 and 3.3 for PHSEN (four solutions).

All the NMR experiments were carried out on a Bruker AMX-300 WB instrument, equipped with a 5 mm probe. The π/2 pulse was 7.0 and 8.3 μs on the 1 H and 13 C channels, respectively. The sample temperature was controlled employing a BVT 1000 (Eurotherm) variable temperature unit, with a temperature stability of ±0.1 K. 1 H chemical shifts are given in parts per million after external calibration using a solution of acetone in D 2 O. For 1 H spin lattice relaxation times ( T 1 ), the standard inversion-recovery pulse sequence was used in the temperature range 293–315 K. The 13 C T 1 values of the free peptides in solution at 295 K were measured applying the inversion-recovery pulse sequence with proton decoupling. The T 1 values were determined by a three-parameter fit of peak intensities to the following equation:

I ( t ) = I 0 [1 − α exp(− t / T 1 )] (1)
where the parameter α takes into account a non-ideal inversion.

Selected 1 H signals were assigned on the basis of their multiplicity and chemical shift. The chemical shifts of β′ and β′′ protons, together with the J coupling values involved and line broadening, were obtained through a fitting procedure implemented in the program SpinWorks, written and made available by Dr Kirk Marat. 30 The chemical shifts and the linewidths of the selected resonances before and after the addition of Cu( II ) are reported in the ESI. †

The α carbons of the 13 C spectra were identified on the basis of the chemical shift according to the literature.

The imidazole ring of the histidine residue bears two hydrogen atoms, δ and ε, which are evidenced in Fig. 1 . For a histidine residue interacting with Cu( II ), the distance of the imidazole ε hydrogen from the metal is 0.3 nm, independently of copper binding to the nitrogen close to the chain (N π ) or to the far one (N τ ). On the contrary, the distance of the δ hydrogen from copper varies with the binding nitrogen: it is 0.5 nm or 0.3 nm for binding to N π or N τ , respectively. Several crystallographic studies have shown that, for oligopeptides containing histidine, copper binds to N π . 33–37 However, coordination to N τ has been hypothesized in some cases. 38 In this work, we considered only the imidazole ε and δ protons, imposing a δ proton–Cu( II ) distance of 0.5 nm for both C and B paramagnetic sites.

PHSFN and PHSEN show one and two proton accepting centres, respectively, the latter peptide having a carboxylic group besides the imidazole one. The imidazole basicity of the His side chain in the PHSEN is slightly higher than that shown in the PHSFN peptide as well as in PHSRN, 15 an effect probably due to electrostatic interaction between the deprotonated carboxylic group and the protonated imidazole ring. The p K values are in agreement with those reported for analogous peptides. 42

Starting from acidic pH values to basic ones, the stability constant analysis shows that the metal binding starts above pH = 3.5 and the first complex species formed by both peptides is CuL. In the PHSFN peptide, the anchoring site for copper( II ) ion binding is the histidine imidazole nitrogen, since the peptide has N- and C-termini blocked by acetylation and amidation, respectively. This is confirmed by NMR, as discussed in paragraph 3.2. The stability constant (log β = 3.86) is higher than that reported for the same species formed by analogous peptides containing one histidine residue (see Table 2 ); this suggests the involvement of one imidazole nitrogen atom with the assistance of a carbonyl group. 24 The presence of a CuN 1 O 3 chromophore (N Im , CO, 2O water ) is supported by UV-vis data ( λ max = 770 nm, ε = 20, Table 3 ). The EPR spectrum of Cu–PHSFN at pH 5.2, displayed in Fig. 3 , shows two spectral components characterized by different values of the parameters g ‖ and A ‖ . One component is characterized by g ‖ = 2.37 and A ‖ = 113 G, while for the other g ‖ = 2.417 and A ‖ = 110 G. The former component is due to copper bound to the peptide; the values of g ‖ and A ‖ fall in the range expected for the coordination of a single nitrogen and three oxygen atoms in the equatorial plane (CuN 1 O 3 ), according to the established Peisach–Blumberg correlations. 43 Unfortunately, the EPR data could not confirm the possible involvement of the oxygen atom bound to the carbonyl group. At pH 5.3 a prion fragment shows values very close to those observed for our system. 44 The latter component is due to aqueous Cu( II ) complex, as the parameters g ‖ and A ‖ are close to those of the literature for this complex. 43

The stability constant value of the copper( II ) complex with PHSEN is definitely higher than that found for PHSFN and for the native PHSRN peptide (see Table 2 ), a clear indication of the glutamate carboxylate oxygen atom coordination in addition to that of the histidine imidazole nitrogen atom, with the formation of a 14-membered macrochelate ring. The high stability constant makes this species the predominant one up to pH = 7. The λ max value of the CuL complex with PHSEN is lower than that of the corresponding species with the PHSFN: this is further confirmation of the direct involvement of the glutamic carboxylate group in the metal ion coordination and of the CuN 1 O 3 chromophore (N Im , COO − Glu , 2O water ) formation.

The EPR spectrum of Cu–PHSEN at pH 5.2 indicates the presence of a peptide–metal complex characterized by g ‖ = 2.3477 and A ‖ = 132 G, which are close to those reported for similar systems such as the angiogenin cellular binding site PHREN. 45 According to Peisach and Blumberg, 43 the slightly larger A ‖ and smaller g ‖ values in comparison to those of PHSFN suggest that the total charge of the metal ion and of its four equatorial ligands is lower in the case of PHSEN, which would confirm the presence of the negatively charged COO − Glu in the metal coordination sphere. No aqueous Cu( II ) was detected at this pH, in agreement with the larger prevalence of the species CuL over Cu in Cu–PHSEN compared to Cu–PHSFN ( Fig. 2 ).

Indeed, the CD spectra of Cu–PHSEN show extremely low intensity features up to pH 7 ( Fig. 4(b) ), supporting the metal ion binding to donor atoms of the side chains distant from peptide backbone chiral centres such as imidazole and carboxylate.

The next complex formed at higher pH values by both peptides is CuLH −1 . Taking into account the pH range of existence of this species, the coordination binding sites for the metal ion may involve one deprotonated amide nitrogen atom. Indeed, the p K value for PHSFN is 6.48, i.e. a value similar to that reported for the PHSRN (see Table 2 ) and the octarepeat segment of the prion protein (PHGGWGQ), in which the amide nitrogen deprotonation takes place towards the C-terminus, forming a 7-membered chelate ring. 18,46 The UV-vis parameters are in agreement with the presence of a CuN 2 O 2 (N Im , N − , 2O water ) chromophore. 12 This is confirmed by the EPR spectrum of Cu-PHSFN at pH 7; the spectral components occurring at lower pH have disappeared and two new species are observed, whose g ‖ and A ‖ values are compatible with the metal coordination of two nitrogen and two oxygen atoms, 2N2O. 24 In agreement with the distribution diagram species and the other spectroscopic results, at pH values of 6–7 the CD spectra of Cu–PHSFN show the simultaneous presence of the ligand to metal charge transfer (CT) bands related to amide (310 nm) and imidazole nitrogen atoms (350 nm), due to the involvement of deprotonated amide and imidazole nitrogen in copper( II ) ion binding, 47 and a minimum centred at 605 nm in the d–d region ( Fig. 4(a) ). This indicates that the metal ion is closer to the chiral peptide backbone than in the case of Cu–PHSEN complex. In the latter case, as observed above, the spectra collected in the same pH range show the predominance of the CuL complex, for which no amide nitrogen atom is involved in the metal coordination.

It was not possible to obtain UV-vis parameters for the mono-deprotonated species formed by PHSEN, due to the low formation percentages in comparison with the CuL ( Fig. 2 ). The p K value is higher (p K 0–1 = 7.22) and so the first amide deprotonation process occurs at higher pH values than that observed for PHSFN and PHSRN, an effect due to the involvement of the carboxylate oxygen atom in the Cu coordination environment. We suppose the presence of a CuN 2 O 2 (N Im , N − , COO − Glu , O water ) chromophore, in line with a previous study involving the mono-deprotonated ligand PHREN. 45 The supposed chromophore is compatible with the EPR parameters ( g ‖ = 2.298 and A ‖ = 166 G) extracted from the Cu–PHSEN spectrum at pH 7.0.

Increasing the pH, the bis-deprotonated species are formed by both copper–peptide systems, as revealed by the potentiometric measurements. However, thermodynamic and spectroscopic differences are to be noted: the p K of the second amide deprotonation in PHSFN is similar to that reported for PHSRN and lower than that measured for PHSEN, indicating an easier deprotonation process in the formation of CuLH −2 species which is the predominant at pH = 7, while in the Cu–PHSEN system the maximum percentage formation is at pH = 8.5. The UV-vis parameters are consistent with a 3N1O coordination mode in which one imidazole and two amide nitrogen atoms are coordinated to the metal ion (N Im , 2N − , O water ) for PHSFN. A similar coordination was reported for prion based peptides, including the single octarepeat. 44 On the other hand, even if two deprotonated amide nitrogen atoms are present in the coordination environment in the species formed by PHSEN, carboxylate must also be involved so to determine a non perfectly planar disposition of donor atoms, justifying the red-shift of λ max value. The environment is probably formed by the atoms (N Im , 2N − , O water ) in the equatorial plane and an oxygen atom from COO − Glu in an apical position. This environment was supposed also for the bis-deprotonated ligand PHREN. 45 In agreement with a slightly different geometry of the two complex species, the CD spectrum at pH 8 of Cu–PHSEN is slightly different from that obtained for Cu–PHSFN ( Fig. 4 ). In fact, the minimum in the d–d region is characterized by λ = 596 nm and Δ ε = −1.11 M −1 cm −1 for Cu–PHSFN, while λ = 618 nm and Δ ε = −0.412 M −1 cm −1 for Cu–PHSEN.

The complex species formed when the pH is further increased is CuLH −3 and the same trend of the p K value found for CuLH −2 is observed. The p K value is lower in the Cu–PHSFN system and the pH range of existence is shifted at lower values ( Fig. 2 ). The remarkable blue-shift of UV-vis λ max clearly indicates the deprotonation of a third amide and a copper( II ) coordination mode involving one imidazole and three amide nitrogen atoms (N Im , 3N − ). At the same deprotonation level, also the native peptide is characterized by this coordination environment. 15 The UV-vis parameters of the analogous species formed with PHSEN indicate a less planar disposition of donor atoms, a difference evidently due to the carboxylate group that may still be involved in the metal ion coordination.

At strong basic pH, the CuLH −4 complex species is formed and the blue-shift of UV-vis λ max clearly suggests a copper( II ) coordination environment with the four deprotonated amide nitrogen atoms, while the imidazole side-chain is no longer involved in copper( II ) binding. This coordination mode was observed also in the complex formed by PHSRN. 15 The CuLH −4 species formed by the two peptides show the same λ max indicating that also the carboxylate group is not involved in copper( II ) coordination environment anymore. The same conclusion is drawn from EPR data, which show that at pH 10.7 for both samples only one species is formed with g ‖ and A ‖ values consistent with binding to four nitrogen ligands. Accordingly, the CD spectra obtained for the two systems at this pH value are more similar to each other in comparison with those obtained at lower pH values ( Fig. 4 ).

3.2 Histidine residue as anchoring point: differential line broadening in 1 H NMR spectra

The effects of Cu( II ) on the 1 H NMR spectrum of the two peptides were studied to determine the nature of the copper binding sites, focusing on an acidic pH to highlight the effective role of the carboxylate group in metal ion coordination. In both peptides, the addition of small amounts of CuSO 4 caused no effect on the chemical shift of selected resonances (listed in the ESI † ) but gave rise to line broadening.

In PHSFN, the resonances due to the ε and the δ protons of histidine residue were significantly broadened, more than the other selected resonances. In particular, after the addition of 0.016 mole-equivalents of Cu( II ), the ε signal was broadened from about 0.8 Hz to 4 Hz linewidth at half height and the δ resonance line width doubled (from 1.8 to 4.0 Hz). The spectral regions containing the ε and δ signals before and after the metal addition are shown for comparison in Fig. 5 . Fig. 6 displays the fractional increase of the line width for the selected signals, showing that, apart from the ε and δ histidine resonances, the peaks were generally broadened, with little differential effect on particular signals.


Fig. 6 Fractional increase of line width of a selection of proton resonances of different residues in the peptides PHSFN and PHSEN due to the addition of 0.016 and 0.036 mole-equivalents of Cu( II ), respectively, at pD = 5.2 and T = 297 K.

In PHSEN, the addition of 0.036 Cu( II ) mole-equivalents caused a general broadening of all the proton signals. The largest effect was observed for the histidine ε resonance, whose linewidth doubles, increasing from about 1 to 2 Hz, whereas the increase in linewidth of the histidine δ resonance is smaller and comparable to that observed for β′, β′′ protons of histidine, serine, asparagine and for γ protons of glutamic acid ( Fig. 6 ). Fig. 6 shows that the broadening of the signals in PHSEN is much less pronounced than that in PHSFN, also considering that in the former sample the amount of copper equivalents is more than double than in the latter.

The differential broadening of the histidine ε resonance observed in the peptides studied indicates that the histidine residue is an anchoring point for copper( II ). In fact, histidine containing peptides, when complexed to Cu 2+ , generally show differential line broadening of the imidazole aromatic protons. 48 A detailed analysis has been reported on prione fragments, 38 showing that peptides carrying two histidine residues display an even larger broadening of ε and δ resonances induced by a smaller number of copper equivalents in comparison with our PHSFN sample. On the contrary, four-histidine fragments exchange copper at a slower rate than the shorter peptides and show little differential effect, this resembling our PHSEN case. The smaller broadening together with the less pronounced differential effect shown by PHSEN in comparison to PHSFN are indicative of a slower exchange rate.

3.3 Ligand exchange from 1 H relaxation enhancements R 1p data

According to the procedure outlined in paragraph 2.8, we determined the correlation time τ c via eqn (6) by measuring the 13 C longitudinal relaxation times T 1 of the α carbons of the free ligands. These carbons have similar T 1 values, which are in the range 300–400 ms, suggesting that the peptide backbone carbons experience similar motional freedom. The τ c value is equal to 0.11 ± 0.03 ns and 0.13 ± 0.03 ns for the PHSFN and PHSEN ligand at 295 K, respectively. Within the assumptions previously described, the temperature trend of τ c for the paramagnetic complexes is derived and fixed in the fitting of the relaxation rate enhancement data R 1p .

In Fig. 7(a) the temperature trend of the experimental R 1p for PHSFN is shown at the two peptide/copper ratios of 187 and 62 for the histidine ε and δ protons, R 1pε187 , R 1pδ187 , R 1pε62 and R 1pδ62 . In the following, for R 1p we will use the notation shown above, where the histidine proton observed and the peptide/copper ratio applied are indicated in the subscript. The R 1p values are rather small in comparison to those shown by similar oligopeptides containing a histidine residue, 17–19,46,49,50,51 indicating that here the exchange is particularly slow. For example, for the pentadecapeptide SPARC at pH = 5.6, the R 1pε value was about three times the value recorded by us with an even smaller peptide/copper ratio at room temperature ( R 1pε250 ≅ 1.2 to be compared to our R 1pε187 = 0.4). 49 The prion protein fragment PrP 106–126 , for which exchange is particularly slow at pH 5.7, shows a relaxation enhancement R 1pδ20 of 4 at room temperature, 19 which is comparable to the value obtained by us, R 1pδ62 = 0.9, if this is rescaled considering the difference of the peptide/copper ratios. The presence of copper does not significantly affect the longitudinal relaxation of other selected protons, such as β′, β′′ of H, S, F and N residues and δ of P. These observations suggest again that Cu( II ) is preferentially bound to the imidazole ring of the histidine residue, in agreement with the differential line broadening data shown above.

The data were globally fitted applying the three different models outlined in the Materials and Methods section adopting the strategy there described ( Fig. 7 and 8 ). The experimental trends are more satisfactorily fitted by the CBA model. The BA and the CAB models fail in predicting the R 1p 's of histidine δ protons at the lower temperatures, apart from the single case of R 1pδ187 for PHSFN. In the remaining cases both models predict values higher than the experimental ones for R 1pδ at the lower temperatures, although they acceptably reproduce the R 1pε data at any peptide/copper ratio. It should be noticed that for PHSEN the parameters of the CAB model indicate that the exchange between C and A is so slow that the second term of eqn (3) does not contribute to the R 1p value. Therefore, the CAB model is actually equivalent to the BA model, with f B < [M 0 ]/[L 0 ], which is the ratio between the total concentrations of metal and ligand.

The best fitting parameters of the CBA model, reported in Table 4 , indicate that in both peptides the exchange from C to B is a slower process, while the second stage is faster. In fact, as soon as B is generated, it exchanges with A, so that its concentration is much smaller than that of C. The first stage is one order of magnitude faster for PHSFN in comparison to PHSEN, whereas the following exchange process proceeds with a comparable rate for the two samples. This suggests that in the first stage F and/or E residues are involved in the formation of the paramagnetic species, whereas they are not in the following stage.

A discussion on the relative magnitude of these parameters is provided in the following.

On the basis of the results obtained, we can tentatively identify the paramagnetic complexes C and B. Complex C was identified with CuL of Table 3 . For this complex the ligation of the glutamyl carboxylate group and of the carbonyl moiety was supposed according to UV-vis data for PHSEN and PHSFN, respectively (see paragraph 3.1). In B the glutamyl carboxylate group or the carbonyl moiety are replaced by a water molecule. These species are schematically depicted in Fig. 9 . Thus, in the slow stage, water would disengage the carboxylate or the carbonyl group, depending on the system. Ligand exchange would take place in the next fast step and involve complex B, with no exchange occurring directly on C. This mechanism was proposed for bis(histidino)nickel( II ) complex, with the carboxylate group being the histidine terminal one. 31

When the ligand is PHSFN, the mechanism is analogous, apart from the coordination of a carbonyl group CO in complex C instead of the carboxylate COO − .

From the value of the ratio between the two paramagnetic species, f C / f B , indicated in Table 4 , one observes that the equilibrium between C and B species is more shifted to complex C in the case of PHSEN in comparison with PHSFN. For the mechanism proposed, the value of the constant K in eqn (10) is equal to 1, while β C is the stability constant of the CuL complex. Its value was determined potentiometrically and is reported in Table 2 . Since the concentration of the species B is much lower than that of C, the β B value is not available. Since the formation of the bond between the imidazole nitrogen and copper ion is probably the main contribution to the stability constant of complex B, β B , with no appreciable contribution from the carbonyl or carboxylate moieties, we assume that β B E = β B F . Using the values of β C E and β C F reported in Table 2 , we find u = 5.012 (=10 4.56 /10 3.86 ) ± 0.001, to be compared to 3.7 (=480/130) ± 1.4 obtained using the f C / f B values reported in Table 4 . Considering the approximation stated above and the error affecting the ratios derived from the NMR data, the agreement between the two estimates is satisfactory.

In both cases Δ S ≠ CB is positive. This indicates a larger disorder for the activated complex in comparison with the C species. This could be explained considering that in the activated complex a molecule of water could be coordinated to the metal ion, giving rise to a five-coordinated complex. Evidence for such a coordination number was shown for water exchange in the aqueous copper( II ) complex. 52 The coordinating water molecule would force the surrounding free water molecules to be located farther from the metal ion, thus sensing a smaller residual electric field from it. This would result in the mentioned larger disorder. This same line of arguments was used, for example, to rationalize the positive values of entropy change in the formation of copper complexes with anionic ligands. 53 However, we still need to justify the larger Δ S ≠ CB found for Cu–PHSFN in comparison to Cu–PHSEN. Since the electric field generated by a charge Q at a distance r is proportional to Q / r 2 , we notice that a larger charge results in a larger difference of the fields generated at two given distances corresponding to positions of water molecules closer to the central ion in complex C and farther from it in the activated complex. Since the net charge of CuL is +2 for Cu–PHSFN and +1 for Cu–PHSEN, due to the differently charged carbonyl and carboxylate, this argument would explain the larger Δ S ≠ CB value obtained for PHSFN.

The activation parameters found for the fast step are compatible with an associative mechanism, according to the Langford and Gray mechanistic classification for ligand substitution reactions. 54 In such a mechanism an intermediate of increased coordination number is formed, with Δ S ≠ tending to be negative and Δ H ≠ being relatively small. The intermediate species would consist of Cu( II ) complexed to two ligands. In fact, the associative substitution mechanism of nitrogen-containing ligands in solutions of copper( II ) complexes is well established. 55

4. Conclusions

The ligands examined in this work are short peptides based on the second cell binding site of fibronectin, PHSRN. In fact, the fourth residue, arginine, has been replaced by phenylalanine and glutamic acid. Potentiometric, UV-vis, CD, EPR and NMR spectroscopic methods show different coordination features to copper for the two ligands. In fact, the side chain oxygen atom of the carboxylate glutamyl residue in PHSEN binds to the metal up to pH 9, whereas this coordination site is probably occupied by an oxygen atom belonging to a carbonyl group or to a water molecule in the case of PHSFN, depending on pH. No hint of the coordination of the carbonyl group was reported for the original fragment PHSRN. In particular, at physiological pH, the main coordination environment of the copper ion involves one imidazole nitrogen atom and two water oxygen atoms besides the carboxylate group in the case of the PHSEN ligand. On the other hand, for PHSFN, two amide nitrogen atoms are bound to copper together with one imidazole nitrogen atom and a water oxygen atom. This coordination environment was reported for PHSRN.

A kinetic model for ligand exchange could fit the proton NMR relaxation enhancement data collected at pH 5.2 – i.e. after the imidazole nitrogen anchoring – in the temperature range 293–315 K for two different peptide/copper ratios and for the δ and ε protons of the histidine residue. A global fitting strategy allowed us to discriminate among different models, and to exclude the simple two-site exchange one, which is generally applied in the literature. In the model proposed, exchange takes place between two copper complexes and between one copper complex and the free ligand. A mechanism compatible with the parameters found in the fitting was proposed. In the first slow stage, a water molecule disengages a carbonyl or a carboxylate group coordinated to the metal ion in the complex formed by PHSFN or PHSEN, respectively. This stage is one order of magnitude slower for PHSEN, due to entropic effects related to the different charges of the complexes. In the second fast step, the complex just formed exchanges with the ligand and no appreciable differences are found for the two cases examined. In the absence of a group coordinated to the metal ion, such as the carboxylate of PHSEN and the carbonyl in PHSFN together with the imidazole nitrogen, N Im , such a two-step mechanism is expected not to apply.

Therefore, the kinetic results of this work are significant from a strictly chemical point of view and also for the methodological approach, based on the global fitting of NMR data and on the evidence collected with the other more standard techniques, used to build up a coherent picture. Moreover, the structural and kinetic data are remarkable in a more general perspective because they can provide insights into the behavior of analogous copper( II ) complexes formed by other biologically relevant peptides.
