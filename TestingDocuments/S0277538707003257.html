Coordination abilities of difunctional, diaminophosphonic acid and its dipeptides towards Cu(II) ions

Abstract

Phenylenedi(aminomethylphosphonic) acid ( L9 ) and its peptides with L-Ala and β-Ala residues were synthesized and their ability to bind Cu(II) ions in aqueous solution was studied. L9 forms complexes with metal to ligand ratios of 1:1 and 1:2 which are typical for aminophosphonic acids coordination mode 1x or 2 x { NH 2 , PO 3 2 - } . In the case of their dipeptide derivatives ( L10 – L12 ), only one peptidyl chain is involved in the binding of metal ion with stoichiometry 1:1 and coordination pattern similar to that found in the simple phosphonic dipeptides. The influence of metal ion chelation on the properties of uncoordinated group in the same ligand molecule was found.

Aminophosphonates and their peptidic analogues are systematically investicated compounds as the receptors for biomolecules. We have synthesized a potential receptor, 1,4-phenylene-di( N - l -alanylaminomethyl-phosphonate) able to form ionic-pairs with lysine and arginine in methanol and aqueous solutions. The binding abilities toward Cu(II) ions of this ligand as well as its analogues are presented in this paper.

1. Introduction

Aminophosphonates and their peptidic analogues are well known as potent inhibitors of many metalloenzymes and thus are of interest from a therapeutic point of view. Their chelating properties have resulted in development of the useful industrial agents such as the corrosion inhibitors, and ligands useful for the construction of contrast agents [1] ; [2] ; [3] . Therefore, many coordination studies have been performed to determine metal chelating properties of aminophosphonic acids and their derivatives [4] ; [5] ; [6] ; [7] . The interest in phosphonic compounds as the receptors for biomolecules, particularly amino acids [8] has recently systematically increased.

We have synthesized such a potential receptor, 1,4-phenylene-di( N - l -alanylaminomethyl-phosphonate) ( L10 ) which is able to form ionic-pairs with lysine and arginine in methanol and aqueous solutions [9] . The binding abilities of this ligand as well as its analogues are presented in this paper. The coordination of Cu(II) ions with series of ligands L9 , L10 , L11 and L12 ( Fig. 1 ) and their simple analogues L1 – L8 ( Fig. 1 ) were studied by means of potentiometry and spectroscopic methods such as: UV–Vis, CD and EPR.

2. Experimental

2.1. Ligand synthesis

2.1.1. Peptides of aminomethyl-, 2-aminoethyl- and 3-aminopropylphosphonic acids

Diethyl aminomethyl-, 2-aminoethyl- and 3-amino-propylphosphonates were synthesized according to previously described procedures [10] . They were obtained as viscous liquids and were purified by precipitation of their oxalates from diethyl ether solutions.

Phosphono dipeptides were obtained according to previously described procedures using mixed carboxylic–carbonic acid procedure. They were purified by recrystallization from water–ethanol mixtures [10] ; [11] .

2.1.2. Diphosphonic ligands

2.1.2.1. Synthesis of tetraethyl 1,4-phenylene-di(aminomethylphosphonate) and L9

The reaction was carried out according to the procedure reported previously [12] ; [13] . Terephthalaaldehyde (0.014 mol, 1.85 g) and benzylamine (0.028 mol, 2.98 g) were dissolved in 100 ml of methanol and stirred overnight at room temperature. The resulting crystalline Schiff base was filtered and analyzed by means of 1 H NMR (CDCl 3 ): δ : 4.86 (s, 2H, C H 2 ); 7.24–7.42 (m, 10H, benzyl protons), 7.86 (s, 4H, phenylene protons), 8.42 (s, 2H, C H N), yield 80%, t.t. 102 °C. This product was of a good purity and therefore it was not purified. It was dissolved in 50 ml of toluene, and diethylphosphite (0.012 mol, 1.61 g) was added followed by refluxing the reaction mixture for 8 h. Then the volatile components of the solution were evaporated, crude product dissolved in dry diethyl ether and precipitated as oxalate upon addition of appropriate amount of anhydrous oxalic acid in ethyl ether. The formed precipitate was filtered, dried and suspended in dichloromethane (DCM). The suspension was washed with 1 M NaOH until all soil material was dissolved. After evaporation of the solvent, crude product was dissolved in 2.4 M solution of hydrogen chloride gas in methanol and hydrogenated over Pd/C catalyst at atmospheric pressure during 8 h. The progress of hydrogenation reaction was controlled by 31 P NMR spectroscopy. When the hydrogenation process was completed, reaction mixture was filtered through 4 cm layer of Cellite and solution was evaporated under reduced pressure. The crude hydrochloride was purified by recrystallization from methanol/ethyl acetate mixture. In order to obtain L9 , the tetraethyl 1,4-phenylene-di(aminomethylphosphonate) was refluxed in 36% hydrochloric acid during 8 h, then evaporated, treated with ethanol, and collected by filtration. The crude L9 was purified by dissolving in base solution and acidification with concentrated hydrochloric acid. During this process, pure compound L9 precipitated. For coordination studies this compound was used as dihydrochloride or tetra-sodium salt.

2.1.2.2. Synthesis of L10 , L11 and L12

The synthesis of these phosphono peptides L10 , L11 and L12 was carried out accordingly to the general procedure described previously [14] . A solution of 0.005 mol of the Boc-L-Ala or Z-β-Ala and 0.0052 mol of triethylamine in 40 ml of dry chloroform was cooled to −5 °C and treated with (0.0055 mol) of ethyl chloroformate. After 40 min, 0.0025 mol of tetraethyl 1,4-phenylene-di(aminomethylphosphonate) (see Section 2.1.2.1 ) was added with stirring and the solution was left to reach room temperature, followed by slow warming up to the boiling point. The resulting solution was washed successively with water, 1 M hydrochloride acid, water, saturated solution of sodium bicarbonate and water. Finally, it was extracted with brine and dried over sodium sulphate. The solvent was distilled off and the oily residue representing crude diester of phosphono peptide was dissolved in 20 ml of 40% hydrogen bromide (for L10 ) in glacial acetic acid and left overnight. Then volatile reagents and solvents were removed under reduced pressure. The residues were dissolved in absolute ethanol and by addition of propylene oxide crude peptide precipitated. In order to hydrolyze crude diesters of L11 and L12 0.041 mol (5.4 ml) of bromotrimethylsilane with each compounds were mixed during three days. Then reaction mixtures were evaporated, dissolved in methanol and stirred for 1 day. After that, the solutions were again evaporated and products precipitated by addition of ethanol. The peptides were re-crystallized from ethanol and/or ethanol–water solutions.

2.2. Potentiometric studies

Stability constants both for protons and complexes were determined by pH-metric titration of 1.8–2.0 cm 3 samples at 25 °C over pH range of 2.5–11. The ligand concentration in the various samples was 2 × 10 −3 mol dm −3 and metal ion to ligand ratio was set as 1:2 to 1:6. Initial solutions were titrated with NaOH (Merck) delivered by a 0.25 ml micrometer syringe previously calibrated by weight titrations of standard materials. The purities and exact concentrations of the solutions of the ligands were determined by the method of Gran [15] . The pH-metric titrations were performed involving 0.1 M KNO 3 background using a MOLSPIN automatic titration system with a Russell CMAW 711 microcombined electrode calibrated daily in hydrogen-ion concentration using HNO 3 (Aldrich) [16] . The superquad computer program was used for calculations of stability constants [17] . Standard deviations quoted refer to random errors only. They are, however, a good indication of the importance of a particular species present in the equilibrium.

2.3. Spectroscopic studies (UV–Vis, EPR and CD)

The absorption spectra were recorded on Beckman DU 650 spectrofotometr termostated at 25 °C. Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectra were performed in ethylene glycol-water (1:2 v/v) solution at 77 K using Bruker ESP 300 spectrometer at the X-band frequency (9.3 GHz). Circular dichroizm (CD) spectra were recorded on a Jasco J-715 spectropolarimeter in the 800–235 nm range. The values of Δ ε , i.e. ε l − ε r , and ε were calculated at the maximum concentration of the particular species obtained from the potentiometric data. The ligand concentration used in the spectroscopic samples was 4–6 × 10 −3 mol dm −3 , and metal concentration was 1–2 × 10 −3 mol dm −3 .

3.2. Complexation studies

3.2.0.13. L1 – L8

The stability constants obtained for the Cu(II) complexes are given in Table 2 ; Table 3 . To clarify the binding modes of the complexes formed – visible, CD and EPR spectra were also measured ( Table 4 ; Table 5 ). Low pH species of the CuHL type are formed for all study ligands except for L4 and L7 . In this species Cu(II) ion coordinates in bidentate mode via N-terminal amino nitrogen and peptide carbonyl group. In almost every case, CuHL is a minor species except for L3 and L5 , which possess longer linker between terminal phosphonic group and a peptide bond ( Fig. 1 ). Deprotonation of CuHL results in formation of CuL, a minor species in pH range of 5–7, followed by formation of CuH −1 L. In the latter complex, the binding mode involves { NH 2 , N - , PO 3 2 - } donor set characteristic for dipeptides [20] ; [21] . The binding mode in CuH −1 L is clearly confirmed by the spectroscopic parameters with maximum of d–d transitions located at 620–650 nm and EPR parameters with A ∥ between 174–182 G and g ∥ close to 2.25 ( Table 4 ) [18] .

For first three ligands L1 , L2 and L3 , the major impact on the CuH −1 L formation has the size of the chelating ring with { N - , PO 3 2 - } donor set. This size depends on the linker between two donor atoms. p K values of the reaction of CuH −1 L formation which are 5.16, 5.26 and 6.95, respectively, the conclusion about five – the most favourable, six and seven-membered chelating ring can be made due to the rising p K .

The deprotonation of L1 , L6 and L7 copper complexes in high pH leads to CuH −2 L or CuH −1 LOH species formation and can be assigned to hydrolysis of water molecules from the complex coordination sphere as in the simple dipeptides. Because the measurements were performed in the presence of excess of ligand, a 1:2 species were detected potentiometrically, although CuL 2 species can not be determined by spectroscopic measurements due to a overlapping with the major equimolar CuH −1 L species. We may assume the { NH 2 , N - , PO 3 2 - ; NH 2 ,CO } coordination pattern for CuL 2 form.

3.2.0.14. L9 – L12

The interaction of Cu(II) ions with core ligand L9 yields in the formation of CuH 2 L complex ( Fig. 2 ) in which one set of { NH 2 , PO 3 2 - } donors is involved in the metal ion coordination. The formation of two other species CuHL and CuL results from the consecutive proton dissociation from amino and phosphonic functions. The coordination of Cu(II) ion causes distinct decrease of the p K values of the two functions located on the other side of the molecule ( Table 3 ). The further increase of pH results in the CuL 2 complex formation with the coordination mode { 2 × NH 2 , 2 × PO 3 2 - } . This coordination is supported by the spectroscopic parameter, the d–d transitions centered at 637 nm. The formation of the CuH −1 L 2 species at high pH indicates the beginning of the hydrolysis process.

Two peptides L10 and L11 possessing two dipeptide units in para and meta -positions ( Fig. 1 ) were found to coordinate Cu(II) ions in the same mode as X – Y – PO 3 2 - phosphonic dipeptides. Despite different positions of attached peptide units, both ligands demonstrate similar binding properties towards Cu(II) ion, in which only one peptide moiety is involved. CuH 2 L, the first formed complex, involves most likely the { NH 2 ,C O, PO 3 2 - } donor set [21] . The proton dissociation in the ligand results in the formation of CuHL species. This reaction is accompanied by the significant change in the d–d transitions shifted to 628 nm in UV–Vis and to 629 nm in CD spectra. Also the EPR parameters A ∥ = 175 and g ∥ = 2.25 correspond to formation of the 2 N species resulting from the { NH 2 , N - , PO 3 2 - } binding mode [21] . The comparison of log K values of proton release from amide of these peptides and simple phosphono dipeptides due to its copper binding, showed that they are much lower. Thus, in the case of compounds L10 and L11 , they are 4.46 and 4.81, respectively, whereas for structurally corresponding dipeptides Ala-Leu(P) and Leu-Ala(P) they are 5.58 and 5.23 [20] . The formation of CuL and CuH −1 L species is associated with deprotonation of phosphonic and amino groups from the unbound peptidyl fragment of the molecule.

The replacement of L-Ala residue in L11 with β-Ala moiety leading to L12 results in the lack of a chiral center and elongation of the peptidic chain. This change forces a little variation in the coordination properties of the ligand. The differences in stability constants derive from the different protonation constants, which for this β-Ala peptide are much higher in the case of amino group and in the change of the size of the {NH 2 ,N − } chelate ring ( Table 3 ).

4. Conclusions

Phosphono-peptides containing simple aminophosphonic acids L1 – L8 exhibited a typical coordination mode which is very similar to classic dipeptides. The main species formed is the CuH −1 L complex with { NH 2 , N - , PO 3 2 - } coordination mode. Phenylene-1,4-di(aminomethylphosphonic) acid L9 coordinates Cu(II) ions using 1x or 2 x { NH 2 , PO 3 2 - } donor sets deriving from aminophosphonic units located either on one or on two ligand molecules. Dipeptide derivatives of L9 form the 1:1 complexes, in which copper ion is coordinated by only one dipeptidyl moiety. The comparison of the co-ordination abilities of L11 and L12 has shown that also elongation of a peptide chain does not favor the second peptidic amino nitrogen coordination.
