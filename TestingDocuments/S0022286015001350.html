Abstract

A novel series of near infrared heptamethine indocyanine dyes bearing various aromatic chromophores has been synthesized. The synthetic methodology was achieved via ester condensation reactions of heptamethine indocyanine parent dye with carboxylic moiety and aromatic compounds such as anthracene, pyrene and thiophene derivatives. Structural analysis of the newly prepared dyes was accomplished using one- and two-dimensional nuclear magnetic resonance, infrared spectroscopy and electrospray ionization mass spectrometry. These dyes exhibited high molar absorptivity based on the UV–visible/near-infrared spectral data. Fluorescence emission spectral data was used to determine the relative quantum yield. The new dyes displayed formation of H-aggregates in water at low concentrations, while this behavior was not observed in methanol.

Introduction

During the last two decades there has been renewed interest in the development of near infrared (NIR) dyes as fluorescent markers and sensors in light of their potential use to facilitate the fluorescent imaging in human science [1] ; [2] ; [3] ; [4] ; [5] ; [6] ; [7] ; [8] . Near-infrared fluorescence imaging (>700 nm) reveals low absorption and autofluorescence from biological tissues in the NIR spectral range [9] . This performance enhances tissue depth penetration, reduces background interference and improves image sensitivity [10] . As a consequence, this class of dyes affords great opportunities for non-invasive in vivo applications [11] ; [12] .

Among all near infrared fluorescent dyes, indocyanine green (ICG), a biocompatible NIR dye, has been widely used for over 50 years in clinical liver function testing and in cardiology for determining cardiac blood flow [10] ; [13] ; [14] . ICG is also one of the least toxic imaging agents administered to humans. Although ICG is successfully used in medical applications such as active tumor targeted imaging, its broad applicability is mainly limited by the difficulty of making derivatives from a lack of reactive moieties [15] ; [16] . This lack of functionality confines its use in targeting only specific tumor cell types [16] . Despite these limitations, ICG has notable biocompatibility, which has paved the way for developing cyanine dye derivatives with functional groups for further reactivity [9] . Furthermore, various modifications of indocyanine green have been developed to enhance its chemical and photostabilities [17] . For instance, Patonay, Strekowski, and coworkers were able to overcome these shortcomings by introducing a chlorocyclohexenyl ring into the polymethine chain which enhances the rigidity of the polymethine bridge and increases its solubility, fluorescence emission efficiency (quantum yield) and photostability [18] ; [19] . The modified structure, which known as ‘convertible cyanine dye’, also provides a site for further functionalization at the central ring and has been used as a precursor to the targeted functionalized cyanine dye [20] . For example, water-soluble heptamethine cyanine dyes with aryl- or alkyl-ether, -thioether, or -amine, linkages have been reported [21] .

Utilizing this synthetic strategy, herein we are presenting the synthesis, characterization and investigation of the optical properties of novel heptamethine indocyanine dyes functionalized with various chromophore moieties such as anthracene, pyrene and thiophene rings.

Experimental

General considerations

All reagents were purchased from Sigma–Aldrich, Alfa Aesar, or VWR, and used without further purification. Phosphorus oxychloride (POCl 3 ) was stored under N 2 (g) upon opening. All solvents were HPLC grade from Fisher Scientific and used without further purification. Chloroheptamethine indocyanine dye as well as its carboxylic acid-functionalized NIR dye ( 1 ) were prepared according to previously established procedure [9] ; [15] ; [22] ; [23] .

Characterization

All NMR spectral data was collected on a Varian Mercury Plus Spectrometer (400 MHz), with an ATB tunable multinuclear probe with a gradient channel in dimethyl sulfoxide- d 6 (DMSO- d 6 ) at room temperature. Chemical shifts are referenced to residual solvent peaks and coupling constants are reported in Hz. Infrared spectroscopy was performed on an IRPrestige-21 FT-IR by Shimadzu with a MIRacle ATR by PIKE Technologies. All absorption measurements were performed on methanol solutions unless otherwise specified, in 1.0 cm 2 quartz cuvettes at 22 ± 1 °C, and were measured on a Shimadzu UV-2550 UV–vis spectrophotometer. Fluorescence measurements were performed on methanol solutions in 1.0 cm 2 quartz at 22 ± 1 °C. Fluorescence emission spectra were measured on a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer with the excitation set to 760 nm and with excitation and emission slit widths of 5.0 nm. Electrospray ionization mass spectra in negative mode were obtained on a Waters/Micromass LCT time-of-flight mass spectrometer equipped with an electrospray ion source, using methanol solutions of the products.

Results and discussion

Synthesis of NIR heptamethine dye with carboxylic acid functionality

Studies have shown that polymethine cyanine dyes containing a chlorocyclohexenyl ring in the polymethine chain can enhance the photostability and fluorescence efficiency [24] . Furthermore, the meso -substituted chlorine atom on the cyclohexene ring provides an excellent reactive site for the dye, allowing for the substitution with various nucleophiles [15] ; [18] ; [24] ; [25] ; [26] . Accordingly, the ease of functionalization of such compounds can be very useful for the synthesis of a variety of polymethine dyes. Therefore, this feature was taken into consideration for the functionalization of new heptamethine cyanine dyes.

Synthesis of NIR heptamethine dye 3a – e derivatives substituted with various chromophores

The synthesis of new series of NIR heptamethine indocyanine dye bearing various chromophore moieties have been accomplished via procedure utilized in Scheme 1 . Investigating the effect of varying the substituents of NIR dye 1 on the UV–vis–NIR absorption and fluorescence emission spectra was the primary objective for the synthesis of these compounds.

Furthermore, FT-IR spectral results of 3b and 3c also supported the ester linkage formation which displayed a shift in the C O frequency from a carboxylic acid at 1703 cm −1 to an ester at 1713 cm −1 . The ESI mass spectrometry of NIR dye 3b exhibited a molecular ion peak (M-Na) − at m / z 1029.4 ( m / z calculated for C 60 H 57 N 2 O 8 S 3 : 1029.3), while the ion molecular peak (M-Na) − of 3c was at 1071.2 m / z (calculated for C 63 H 63 N 2 O 8 S 3 : 1071.4 m / z ).

Functionalization of NIR dye 1 with 3-thiophenemethanol and 3-thiopheneethanol to form NIR-absorbing monomers 3d and 3e was not only to investigate UV–vis/NIR absorption and fluorescence emission for these thiophene-containing monomers, but their preparation was also intended for potential electrochemical polymerization of the thiophene-containing moiety.

The NMR spectral data of NIR dyes 3d and 3e was very similar except for the additional methylene protons in the aliphatic chain of 3e . For instance, the gHSQC spectral data of dye 3d indicates that the CH 2 O protons (H d , 5.26 ppm in Fig. 4 ) of the thiophene moiety couple to the carbon resonance at 61.6 ppm, which indicates that a downfield shift occurred for the methylene protons. The gHSQC spectrum in Fig. 4 also shows the resonances of the aromatic protons on the thiophene ring appear at 7.15 ppm (H b ), 7.51 (H c ), and 7.57 (H d ) and their corresponding carbon atoms appear at 127.6, 126.8, and 124.6 ppm, respectively. Spectral properties and molar absorptivity

UV–vis/NIR absorption and fluorescence emission spectral data were collected for the new NIR dyes 3a – e in order to understand their optical properties. The UV–vis/NIR absorption ( λ A ,max ) and fluorescence emission ( λ F ,max ) of the dyes were measured with uniform concentrations of 1.0x10 −6 M in methanol. Using the same concentration, the absorption spectra of NIR dyes 3a – c , and 3e were also obtained in pure water to investigate the aggregation formation and solvent effects. Increasing the conjugation of the thiol-substituted indolenine dyes ( 1,3a – d ) lowers the energy gap between HOMO and LUMO of the molecules and therefore causes a bathochromic shift in the absorption spectra ( Table 1 ). The carboxylic acid dye 1 possesses a NIR absorption band at 793 nm and fluorescence emission band at 812 nm, which is similar to previously published results [22] ; [27] . Esterification reactions of the carboxylic acid with various conjugated substituents resulted in slightly red-shifted UV–vis/NIR absorption and emission spectra. This also led to either smaller or unchanged Stokes shifts ranging between 15 and 19 nm ( Table 1 ). It is common for fluorophores that have relatively small Stokes shifts to exhibit lower quantum yields. This general trend was observed in the fluorescence emission spectra of the NIR dyes ( 1 and 3a – e ) which exhibited lower relative quantum yields ranging between 0.051 and 0.098 ( Fig. 5 ) in comparison to the heptamethine cyanine dye containing a chlorocyclohexenyl ring ( Fig. 1 ). The maximum wavelength absorptions of the esterified heptamethine cyanine dyes range from 795 to 798 nm and did not change significantly compared to the parent NIR dye 1 . This suggests that the replacement of various linkers on NIR dye 1 did not affect the excited state intramolecular charge transfer (ICT). Additionally, the change in maximum wavelengths observed in the emission spectra ( λ F ,max ranged from 812 to 816 nm) was negligible ( Fig. 5 ). The fact that the absorbance and emission wavelength maxima were not significantly altered among the new NIR dye 3a – e and NIR dye 1 indicated that the ground-state interaction between the ester substituents on the sulfur-bridging benzene moiety and the heptamethine chain was minimal in all of them.

On the other hand, the molar absorption coefficients and fluorescence quantum yields were altered, depending on the nature of the ester substituents. All of the dyes exhibited high molar absorption coefficients ( ε ) ranging between 1.3 and 1.9 × 10 5 M −1 cm −1 . However, it was found that the dye substituted with longer aliphatic linkers ( 3c) , exhibited lower molar absorption coefficients compared to the other dyes containing either methylene or ethylene linkages to NIR dye 1 . This behavior could be attributed to a variance in electron density of the mercaptobenzoate moiety. Thus, higher electron density is observed in the moieties containing more electron-donating, longer aliphatic bridging chains, while the derivatives with methylene or ethylene aliphatic bridges have lower electron density due to the closer proximity of the more electron-withdrawing aromatic substituents. Similar trends have been observed by Urano et al. [28] .

Aggregation effects

Cyanine dyes are known to form aggregates in aqueous media in high and even low concentrations, as well as in higher concentrations in organic solvents [29] . Different structures such as dimers form as a result of aggregation, and it depends on how the carbocyanine substituents interact with one another that result in the formation of either H-aggregates (hypsochromic shift) or J-aggregates (bathochromic shift) [30] .

This aggregation phenomenon was investigated for dyes 3a – c and 3e , by measuring the absorption of each dye dissolved in 100% water solution and comparing them to their absorption spectra in 100% methanol. The spectral data, Fig. 6 , indicates that thiophene-containing dyes 3d and 3e exhibit decreased H-aggregate formation, which may be attributed to the increased solvent–dye interaction from the sulfur on the thiophene moiety with the hydrogen on the water molecules, which in turn reduces dye–dye interactions, and thus minimizes aggregation. Furthermore, there may be interactions between the hydrogen atoms next to the sulfur of the thiophene moiety and the oxygen of the water molecules. Note that dye 3e was used as a representative example for the thiophene-containing dyes 3d and 3e since both dyes are very similar in structure with only minor differences in their photophysical properties.

On the other hand, dyes containing terminal fused aromatic rings (dyes 3a – c ) exhibit the highest H-aggregate formation, probably due to the more hydrophobic nature of the aromatic rings, thus reducing the solvent interaction and increasing its intermolecular interactions. Even though indocyanine dye with linker that has a longer alkyl chain length ( 3c ), it provided more steric bulk, which hinders aggregation formation (i.e. minimizes dye–dye intramolecular interactions and dye–solvent intermolecular interactions, preventing the dyes from self-assembling).

Furthermore, in comparison to the change in molar absorptivities (Δ ε ) of the linkers with small bridging aliphatic chains, the Δ ε of the long chain has the smallest value. In contrast, the dyes containing methylene or ethylene linkers ( 3a,b and 3d,e ) exhibit increased H-aggregation, which was characterized by a more intense (more pronounced) hypsochromic-shifted absorption band. The corresponding Δ ε ’s are greater than dye 3c , which suggests that the aggregation formation is more prominent for the methylene and ethylene linkers since the molar absorptivities are more greatly affected when dissolved in more polar solvents.

Absorption and fluorescence emission spectra were also measured in 100% methanol solutions with 1 × 10 −6 M concentration at room temperature ( Table 2 ). All of the dyes showed no aggregate formation in these conditions. Increasing the aliphatic chain length on the pyrene substituent (observed for dye 3c ) caused a decrease in the molar absorption coefficient from 1.5 × 10 5 M −1 cm −1 ( 3b ) to 1.3 × 10 5 M −1 cm −1 ( 3c ) and also a decrease in the relative quantum yield. However, there were no significant differences observed between thiophene-containing NIR dyes 3d and 3e . This indicates that the difference of one methylene linker does not affect the overall photophysical properties of these particular dyes.

Altogether, the appearance of the absorption bands at lower wavelengths at 788 and 790 nm for dyes 3a – c and 3e observed in the water medium, is indicative of the formation of aggregates and that the dyes exhibit solvent-sensitivity. Furthermore, the absorption spectra obtained in a methanol medium shows that aggregation is inhibited, which results in primarily the monomeric dye.

Conclusion

A number of novel heptamethine indocyanine dyes containing various aromatic chromophores were synthesized and found to possess photoactive characteristics. The molar absorption coefficients and fluorescence quantum yields of these fluorophores were dependent on the nature of the ester substituents. All of the dyes exhibited high molar absorption coefficients ranging from 1.3 to 1.9 × 10 5 M −1 cm −1 . Additionally, the esterified heptamethine indocyanine dyes containing various aromatic chromophores resulted in slightly red-shifted UV–vis/NIR absorption and emission spectra in comparison to their corresponding non-substituted chloro-heptamethine cyanine parent. The esterified dye derivatives’ absorption and emission wavelength maxima were relatively constant. This characteristic may be useful for applications requiring photo-active compounds that require functionalization without significantly altering the wavelength maxima.

The aggregation behavior of the new dyes in methanol was prohibited while this behavior was observed in aqueous solution. This could be attributed to the solvent-sensitivity of the new dyes. The formation of H-aggregates was also found depending on the chromophore substituents. For example, heptamethine cyanine dyes containing thiophene and bulkier chromophore substituents exhibited decreased aggregation due to minimal dye–dye interactions. In contrast, aggregation was the largest in the dyes containing terminal fused aromatic rings due to their increased hydrophobicity, which reduced solvent–dye interactions and increased intermolecular interactions.

These novel functionalized heptamethine indocyanine dyes could be potential candidates in industrial and biological applications due to their high molar absorptivities, water-solubility, and their ability to be fine-tuned.
