Bis-porphyrinic clamp for photo- and electro-active guests: a spectroscopic and photophysical study

Lucia Flamigni * a , Giancarlo Marconi a and Martin R. Johnston b
a Istituto FRAE-CNR, Via P. Gobetti 101, 40129, Bologna, Italy. E-mail: flamigni@frae.bo.cnr.it ; Fax: + 39 (0)51 639 9844; Tel: + 39 (0)51 639 9812
b Center for Molecular Architecture, Central Queensland Uni ersity, Rockhampton, Queensland 4702, Australia

Received 25th June 2001 , Accepted 14th August 2001

First published on the web 21st September 2001

The spectroscopic and photophysical characterization of a V-shaped Zn( II ) bis-porphyrinic structure, ZnH , its free-base analogue FBH and the model porphyrin components ZnB and FBB is presented. With respect to the unsubstituted zinc porphyrin Zn , asymmetric substitution with tetraazaanthracene is responsible for the increase of dipole moment in the excited state of Zn( II ) derivatives. The charge transfer character of the transition causes a remarkable solvent effect on the luminescence properties. Axial complexation of the zinc ion in ZnB and ZnH by pyridine induces a strong perturbation of the photophysical properties. In the free base derivatives FBB and FBH the substituent displaces the equilibrium population with respect to unsubstituted FB , on both the ground and excited states of the two tautomers, allowing for the presence of two independent populations ( ca . 90% and 10%) of distinct species detected by UV-Vis spectroscopy . The data are supported by semiempirical calculations.

Introduction

The demand for complex, multi-component molecular architectures able to perform useful acts upon external stimuli is rapidly increasing. 1 The use of light to trigger a transformation of the system appears appealing and the spatial and temporal definition of today's laser sources allows a high degree of control over the key function of either a “molecular machine” or a “molecular logic gate”. 2 In addition, one of the most important consequences in the use of light to drive a chemical transformation in a system is the conversion of light energy into chemical energy, which opens new possibilities in the demand for a sustainable and environmentally friendly energy source. “ Artificial photosynthesis” 3 is the discipline that addresses this point, and is based on a re-interpretation of aspects of the natural process including light collection and transport, and charge separation over long distances. Porphyrins , or closely related structures, are key components in the processes occurring in nature, 4 and they have been widely used in artificial systems due to their remarkable photo- and electrochemical properties and to the presence of spectroscopically diagnostic signals associated with their photoinduced intermediates. 5,6

In nature, the organization of chromophores in space is achieved by a cooperative network of weak non-covalent interactions, which provide optimal distances and orientations of chromophores so as to maximize the efficiency of processes. 4 Synthetic multi-component systems have mostly been based on covalent connections between components, 6 a means to provide the necessary geometrical arrangement and electronic connections, but there is now an increasing interest in systems based on weak interactions that better mimic the natural approach. 7 Serious drawbacks for such systems are the lower association constants between components and the less well defined geometry of the chromophores . 8 To overcome such problems, synthetic chemists have focussed their efforts on the preparation of systems able to offer multi-point interactions between chromophores , a successful strategy to ensure both a precise geometry and efficient self-assembling of components. 9

The Zn( II ) bis-porphyrin host ZnH here presented, Fig. 1 , is a V-shaped cavity which is able to coordinate with a precise geometry a complementary guest containing two pyridyl units at the extremities. 10 Complexation occurs with a high constant, K a ca . 10 8 M −1 , and fast photoinduced processes, like electron transfer , can take place efficiently within the complex. 11 A detailed spectroscopic and photophysical characterization of ZnH , of the free base analogue FBH and of the porphyrin building components shown in Fig. 1 , is presented. The experimental findings are supported by the results of semiempirical calculations carried out on the model compounds FBB and ZnB .

Experimental

Spectroscopic and photophysical determination

The synthesis of the porphyrinic hosts and mono-porphyrin reference compounds has been reported in preliminary accounts. 10,12 The solvents used were spectroscopic grade (C. Erba). Absorption spectra were recorded with a Perkin-Elmer Lambda 9 spectrophotometer , and emission spectra , uncorrected if not otherwise specified, were detected by a Spex Fluorolog II spectrofluorimeter equipped with a Hamamatsu R928 photomultiplier. A phosphorimeter accessory (1934D-Spex) allowed for the determination of time delayed spectra and phosphorescence lifetime; the phosphorescence was detected with a gate open for 20 ms after a delay of 40 μs with respect to the lamp pulse. Relative luminescence intensities were evaluated from the area (on an energy scale) of the luminescence spectra corrected for the photomultiplier response. Luminescence quantum yields ϕ for the samples were obtained with reference to a standard, model Zn in toluene with ϕ = 0.08. 13 Luminescence lifetimes in the range 20–2000 ps were determined by an apparatus based on a Nd:YAG laser (Continuum PY62-10) with 35 ps pulse duration, 532 nm, 1 mJ/pulse and a streak camera. 14 Lifetimes longer than 2 ns were detected by a time correlated single photon counting apparatus with 1 ns resolution and excitation at 337 nm. T-T absorption spectra and lifetimes were determined by a laser flash photolysis apparatus based on a Nd:YAG laser (Surelite II, Continuum) coupled to an optical parametric oscillator (SL OPO, Continuum) delivering 5 ns pulses of 1–7 mJ from 410 to 705 nm. More details on the detection and acquisition systems are reported elsewhere. 15 T-T absorption spectra at room temperature were conducted in air-free solutions, whereas removal of air was unnecessary for fluorescence experiments at room temperature and luminescence at 77 K. Samples were contained in 10 mm quartz cuvettes and, for luminescence experiments at 77 K, in quartz capillary tubes immersed in liquid nitrogen contained in a home-made quartz dewar. Estimated errors are 10% on lifetimes and 20% on quantum yields. 1 H NMR spectra were recorded using Bruker spectrometers (300 or 400 MHz) using standard Bruker pulse programs and were referenced to internal tetramethylsilane .

Calculations

Quantum mechanical semiempirical calculations were performed both on the ground state and on the excited states of model compounds FBB and ZnB . The heat of formation of the possible isomers was calculated by the AM1 method, whereas the excitation energies, oscillator strengths and transition dipole moments were evaluated using the ZINDO/S program on structures optimized by the MM + method (all programs included in the Hyperchem package 16 ). The solvent effect was estimated through standard methods 17 using the calculated dipole moments of the states, the molecular radius and literature dielectric constants. 18

Results and discussion

Steady state emission and absorption spectroscopy in toluene

The ground state absorption spectra of the two bis-porphyrins ZnH and FBH in toluene are reported in Fig. 2 and 3 respectively, together with the spectra of the mono-porphyrinic ref erence systems, ZnB and FBB . In the same figures are shown, for comparison purposes, the spectra of the simple unsubstituted porphyrins Zn and FB , measured in the same solvent . The mono-porphyrinic models ZnB and FBB display spectra identical in shape to those of the bis-porphyrinic ZnH and FBH , while the molar absorption coefficient value is half that of the corresponding bis-porphyrin host, as expected on the basis of the number of chromophore units. This is indicative of the absence of interactions between the chromophores , electronically isolated by the rigid alicyclic backbone, in the bis-porphyrinic compounds. In comparison with the unsubstituted Zn and FB , the presence of the tetraazaanthracene substituent, connecting the porphyrin and the alicyclic backbone, introduces an asymmetry element which is responsible for the observed differences in the spectra.

Fig. 3 Ground state absorption spectra in toluene of FB (···), FBB (– – –), FBH (——).

In addition, substitution causes a sizeable energy difference in the two isomers 19 denoted as a and b ( Fig. 4 ), which have to be considered for each porphyrinic unit. In the case of ZnB and ZnH the interconversion of the two species is expected to be fast since no net molecular motion of the metal is required and, see below, the calculated population distributions in the ground state are rather close. This allows us to consider the mixture of isomers for the zinc porphyrins as a single species. On the contrary, in the case of the free base derivatives, isomerization requires hydrogen ion motion, for which rates of the order of 10 4 –10 5 s −1 have been reported in similar systems. 20 Such a rate is slow compared to the excited state lifetimes and moreover calculations (see below) indicate rather different energies for the ground state of the free-base derivative. These facts require that the single isomeric species, FBBa and FBBb , are considered as separate molecular entities whose relative abundance will depend on the Boltzmann distribution at room temperature. FBB was examined by NMR spectroscopy in dichloromethane and toluene solutions, both at room temperature and at lower temperatures down to 203 K, which revealed only the presence of isomer a . This was achieved by decoupling experiments to remove the 4 J long range coupling between the inner hydrogen atoms and the β-pyrrole protons of the porphyrin macrocycle . 20 In all experiments, only the resonances for pyrrole hydrogen atoms positioned adjacent to the tetraazaanthracene substituent were affected by the decoupling experiments. In the case of FBH , similar results were seen in toluene solutions at room temperature and at lower temperatures down to 203 K. The indication which can be derived from such determinations is that at least 90–95% of the free-base systems is present in the form denoted as a ; nonetheless spectroscopic techniques which are more sensitive than NMR clearly indicate the existence of a sizeable percentage of the form b , as discussed below.


Fig. 4 Formulae of the possible isomers.

The spectroscopic features can be summarized as follows. The Soret band (S 0 → S 2 ) for zinc and free-base derivatives ZnH , ZnB , FBB and FBH is broader with respect to the models Zn and FB due to a splitting of the B x and B y components, caused by the decrease in symmetry. A weaker band in the 460–490 nm region, not present in the model compounds, derives presumably from π–π* transitions of the azaanthracenic fragment. In the visible, the number of Q bands in ZnB and ZnH increases with respect to Zn as expected on the basis of the decreased symmetry. In addition they display a red shift with a general increase in the molar absorption coefficient (inset of Fig. 2 ). In the case of the free-base derivatives FBB and FBH , the bands appear broad and display several shoulders accounted for by the presence of two distinct isomers; in addition the peaks are slightly red shifted with respect to the unsubstituted model FB (inset of Fig. 3 ). All the above facts can be accounted for by a general decrease in the symmetry of the molecules which is also responsible for the existence of independent species in the case of FBB and FBH . A detailed account is given in the calculations subsection. Similar features have been observed previously for related systems and in some cases they have been discussed on the basis of theoretical calculations. 20,21

The emission spectra upon excitation on the Soret band of ZnH and FBH in toluene at ambient temperature are reported in Fig. 5 (a) and (b), along with their low energy absorption bands. The emission of optically matched solutions of the reference porphyrins Zn and FB are also reported. The properties of the mono-porphyrin reference compounds are essentially identical to those of the bis-porphyrin, which will be discussed in detail. The emission of ZnH is a good mirror image of the absorption spectrum [ Fig. 5 (a)], whereas the situation is less straightforward for FBH , whose high energy emission band is exactly overlapping the lowest absorption transition [ Fig. 5 (b)]. This can only be understood if one considers that FBH is a mixture of distinct species, not interconverting on the time scale of the excited state (10 −8 s). As a consequence, the low energy absorption band reported in Fig. 5 (b) would represent the convolution of two different transitions: a high oscillator strength transition belonging to the minor component, placed at lower energy, and a low oscillator strength transition belonging to the major component at a slightly higher energy. The emission occurs for 90–95% of the molecules from the latter while the minor component emits independently at lower energies and has its band overshadowed by the main emission. The absolute emission quantum yields for the compounds have been determined following the procedures outlined in the experimental section, and are reported in Table 1 . There is a general decrease to ca . 50% with respect to the quantum yields of the reference porphyrins Zn and FB .

The luminescence spectra of the bis-porphyrins in a glassy toluene solution at 77 K are shown in Figs. 6 (a) and (b). In the case of ZnH (and ZnB ) a delayed emission, assigned to the phosphorescence from the triplet state of the tetraazaanthracene porphyrin moiety, can be detected at 830 nm and is shown in the inset of Fig. 6 (a). No delayed emission could be measured for the free-base porphyrins FBH and FBB up to 850 nm, a wavelength at which the sensitivity of the instrumentation drops. Also the triplet state of ZnH displays, in analogy to the singlet excited state, a lower energy than the triplet state of Zn , at 776 nm in the same solvent . 13 A summary of the emission properties and of the energy levels of the compounds studied along with the reference FB and Zn is reported in Table 1 , both at 298 K and at 77 K.

Time resolved spectroscopy in toluene

The luminescence lifetimes of ZnH and ZnB at 298 K are essentially identical, 1.2 ns, and lower than that for the corresponding Zn model, which is 2.2 ns. The phosphorescence lifetime at 77 K of both ZnH and ZnB is ca . 12 ms, slightly shorter than the one measured for the model Zn in butyronitrile , 18.9 ms. 22 The free-base analogues FBB and FBH display a single luminescence lifetime, 8.5 ns, in spite of the existence of two independent species; perhaps a modest difference in lifetimes and a very minor amount (5–10%) does not allow the detection of the minor component. The measured lifetime is essentially identical to the one of the model FB . From the lifetime data ( Table 1 ), the radiative rate constants, k r = ϕ / τ , can be calculated and some information on the deactivation pathways inferred. In particular, in the case of the zinc derivative ZnH the radiative rate constant is apparently the same as the one in the porphyrin model Zn , i . e . 3.6 × 10 7 s −1 , and the detected difference in emission quantum yields can be assigned to an increased intersystem crossing to the triplet. The singlet–triplet energy difference is 0.36 eV in the case of ZnH , and 0.48 eV in the case of Zn ; thus such a reduction could well be the reason for the postulated increase in intersystem crossing.

The triplet states of ZnH and FBH have been characterized at room temperature by determining their absorption spectra with a nanosecond laser flash photolysis apparatus. The spectra are dominated by the bleaching of the ground state bands superimposed on a broad absorption extending up to 800 nm [ Figs. 7 (a) and (b)]. The lifetime in air-free solutions is 330 μs for ZnH and 300 μs for FBH , as shown in the insets of Fig. 7 . The reactions of 3 ZnH and 3 FBH with oxygen have been determined to be 1 × 10 9 M −1 s −1 and 1.4 × 10 9 M −1 s −1 , respectively, typical of porphyrin triplets. 23

The excited state properties of the two isomers of FBB are quite different and fit well the model proposed above. It should be noted, however, that the calculated energies are relati e and should be considered on a qualitati e basis. From inspection of Table 2 , one notices that, while the first absorption band is calculated at almost the same energy (∽650 nm), the oscillator strength of the two Q components appears inverted with Q x intense for FBBb (the less stable isomer) and weak for FBBa and ice ersa for Q y . This feature explains the presence of a fluorescence band superposed on the first absorption band, and we interpret the fluorescence band as specular to the hidden absorption band of the most abundant isomer, FBBa . The Q bands are calculated at lower energies with respect to the symmetrical porphyrin , as expected for a system with a more extended π system [Δ ν (Q x )≈10 nm] and both ground and excited states appear more polar due to the decreased symmetry of the molecule (from D 2h to C 2v ). A similar splitting is calculated for the Soret bands below 460 nm.

In the metal substituted compounds, one notices the relative inactivity of the zinc atom in affecting the spectral properties of the molecule, when compared to the free base, in accord with Gouterman's type of classification of the zinc porphyrins as regular-normal. 24 In the present case, the energies of the Q bands are calculated at higher energies for the zinc derivative, as predicted for Zn when compared to FB , but with much enhanced polarity (Δ μ , Table 2 ).

An examination of the electron density changes brought about by the electron promotions involved in the emitting state reveals a large amount of charge concentration on the tetraazaanthracene moiety. This is apparently caused by the presence of the zinc atom since in the free-base porphyrins this phenomenon is much less evident. The increased dipole moment of the ground state, and especially its difference Δ μ (S 1 –S 0 ), is responsible for the large solvent effects observed on both the spectral and dynamic properties of ZnB . Indeed, on changing from an apolar solvent such as toluene to a polar solvent such as benzonitrile , a red shift of ∽30 nm is estimated on the basis of the calculated dipole moments of ZnB (and ∽0 for the corresponding free base), in qualitative agreement with the experiment (see next subsection).

Effect of solvents and coordination on the photophysical properties of ZnB and ZnH

The absorption and emission properties of both ZnH and ZnB were found to be solvent dependent. This is shown for ZnH in Figs. 8 (a) and (b), where normalized absorption spectra (a) and emission upon excitation of optically matched solutions at 431 nm (b) are shown for a group of solvents possessing different dielectric constants. In contrast to this behaviour, the analogues FBH and FBB , in passing from toluene to dichloromethane , exhibit no change in either the absorption properties or the emission maxima, yet reveal a reduction to ca . 40% in quantum yield (data not shown). For the simple Zn model there is no solvent effect, with respect to emission yield, lifetime or energy of the transitions.
Fig. 8 (a) Normalized ground state absorbance and (b) luminescence upon excitation at 431 nm of optically matched solutions, of ZnH in: toluene (– – –), dichloromethane (——) and benzonitrile (···). In the inset the normalized luminescence decay of ZnH following excitation at 532 nm (35 ps pulse, 1 mJ) in the different solvents is reported.

The luminescence lifetimes of ZnH in different solvents are collected in Table 3 with various luminescence and solvent parameters. ZnB displays essentially identical data, except for a lifetime in dichloromethane which is 0.75 ns in the mono-porphyrinic compound compared to 0.52 ns in the bis-porphyrin ZnH . From inspection of Table 3 , one can see that the energy level of the emitting state appears stabilized by increasing the polarity of the solvent , the luminescence lifetime decreases and the calculated radiative rate constant, k r , increases remarkably. These data are in agreement with the fact that the emitting state has a higher dipole moment than the ground state, as discussed in the previous section. In contrast to this, there is no solvent effect on the free-base analogues, in accord with the results of the calculations undertaken indicating a weak increase (and decrease in the case of the most abundant species, FBBa ) in the dipole moment of the emitting state.

The solvents causing stabilization in the excited state of ZnH and ZnB can also display a good complexation ability with respect to axial coordination to the zinc( II ) ion within the porphyrin . This type of interaction is known to displace the zinc ion from the porphyrin plane 25 and could contribute to altering the parameters of the emitting states. To gain a better insight into this point we have determined the photophysical properties of the complex of ZnH with pyridine , a ligand which is known to coordinate axially the zinc( II ) porphyrins . The association constant can be determined by measuring the effect of increasing amounts of pyridine on the Soret band maximum which displays a shift, from 424 nm to 434 nm in this case, typical of axial complexation. 8e,9d,f 8 e ,9 d , f The association constant calculated is of the order of 5 × 10 3 M −1 , in agreement with similar complexes. 8a,8e,9a The data for ZnH– pyridine complex are reported in Fig. 9 (a) and Table 3 , and show that upon complex formation there is a spectral red shift of the emission maximum, a decrease in the emission quantum yield and a minor decrease in the lifetime. The results of a similar experiment performed on the model Zn are reported in Fig. 9 (b) for comparison, and indicate a red shift of the emission but no quenching. The radiative rate constant in the ZnH– pyridine complex appears only slightly decreased with respect to ZnH . Therefore, the detected solvent effect on the luminescence properties of ZnH and ZnB seems to be mostly ascribable to a polarity effect on the stabilization of the transition rather than the influence of axial coordination of the zinc ion by the solvent .

Conclusions

The UV-Vis spectroscopy and photophysical properties of the novel tetraazaanthracene porphyrinic compounds ZnB , ZnH , FBB and FBH have been determined in toluene at ambient temperature and at 77 K. A comparison with the unsubstituted model porphyrins has been performed and rationalized with the aid of semiempirical calculations. The increase of dipole moment of the emitting state of the zinc derivatives, related to a partial charge transfer character of the transition, is responsible for the dramatic solvent effect observed on the luminescence properties. Axial complexation of the zinc porphyrin derivatives ZnB and ZnH by pyridine is found to alter the luminescence properties of the porphyrin . This fact is of relevance for the use of ZnH as host for bis-pyridyl derivatives in the construction of self-assembling multi-component systems for light energy harvesting and conversion.
