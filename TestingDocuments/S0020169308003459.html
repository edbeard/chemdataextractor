Abstract

Aminophosphonates were found to be effective metal ion chelators. Ethylenediamine (EN) itself binds strongly to a series of metal ions forming efficient five-membered chelate ring using two nitrogen donors. Combination of EN with phosphonate function results in the family of low molecular chelating agents. Introduction of a pyridine moiety into EN–phosphonate structure results in the very effective ligands involving a four-nitrogen donor set to coordinate the metal ion. In this work, the potentiometric and spectroscopic data for two analogues 2,2′-(ethylenedi-imino)bis(3-pyridylphosphonic-acid) – L 2 and 2,2′-(ethylenedi-imino)bis(2-pyridyl-phosphonic-acid) – L 3 , comprising EN, two phosphonates and two pyridines with Cu(II), Ni(II) and Zn(II) ions are presented showing very high efficacy of one of the ligands studied.

Ethylenediamine (EN) itself binds strongly to a series of metal ions forming efficient five-membered chelate ring using two nitrogen donors. 1. Introduction

Aminophosphonic as well as more structurally complex polyaminopolyphosphonic acids have been widely acknowledged as ligands that are able to form stable complexes with a wide variety of metal ions [1] . Their chelating properties were intensively studied with regard to application as chelating agents used for the removal of toxic metal ions from environment as well as for the construction of systems ensuring proper uptake of medically desired metal cations by organisms [2] ; [3] ; [4] . The direct interaction of specifically tailored ligands with metal ions is also usually a basic feature for their activity as inhibitors of metalloenzymes [5] ; [6] , especially as effective inhibitors of metalloproteases [7] ; [8] ; [9] . Efficacy of polyaminopolyphosphonic ligands was also seen in the specific binding of lanthanide ions, with complexes of Gd 3+ used as MRI imaging [10] ; [11] and radionuclide complexes of 153 Sm, 166 Ho and 177 Lu being considered as possible therapeutics for bone pain palliation [12] . The 153 Sm–EDTMP complex known as Quadramet ® was approved by the US FDA in 1997 for clinical practice and became efficient for the treatment of painful skeletal metastases [13] .

Polyaminoalkanes functionalized with methylphosphonic acid moieties acting as strong metal chelators have found application as anticorrosive agents, additives to washing powders and agents regulating cement hardening [14] ; [15] . Among them, the most extensive applications have been found for aminophosphonate compounds based on ethylenediamine skeleton. This kind of complexes might also be useful as metalloreceptors in medicine and environmental science. Design and synthesis of ligands that are capable of binding metal ions via amine units and thus leaving non-coordinated deprotonated phosphonate groups may result in metalloreceptors that are able to interact with positively charged groups of amino acids, peptides or carbohydrates. Moreover, paramagnetic ions introduced into such molecule could enforce intermolecular interactions simultaneously sensitizing certain fragments of biological molecule, which might result in signal broadening in NMR spectroscopy or in a change in fluorescence.

In this paper, we describe the synthesis of two new ligands ( L 2 and L 3 ) composed of ethylenediamine (EN), pyridyl (Py) moieties and phosphonic groups, designed to bind metal ions with the donor set based on four nitrogen atoms (2N EN , 2N–Py) and two phosphonic units and the coordination abilities of L 2 and L 3 towards Cu(II), Ni(II) and Zn(II) were studied (see Fig. 1 ). As the presence of three donor systems namely –NH–, N–Py and –PO 3 H 2 could make the coordination studies quite complicated, analogue lacking pyridyl nitrogen atoms (compound L 1 ) was synthesized.

2. Experimental

2.1. Ligand synthesis

All the compounds used for the ligand synthesis were high grade commercial products purchased from Sigma–Aldrich . Purities of the obtained compounds were checked by potentiometric titration and were found to range from 92% to 95%. The synthesis of the provided two diastereoisomeric forms, e.g. meso and racemic , and their population may be judged from the signal ratio ( vide supra ) in 1 H NMR, 13 C NMR and 31 P NMR spectra. The introduced reaction showed lower stereoselectivity in comparison to the synthesis published for compound L 3 by Failla et al. [16] .

2.1.1. Synthesis of compounds L 1 and L 2

3-Pyridinecarboxaldehyde or benzaldehyde (7.2 g and 7.1 g, respectively, 67 mmol) and diethylamine (2 g, 33 mmol) were dissolved in CH 2 Cl 2 (50 ml) and left over anhydrous MgSO 4 for 24 h at room temperature. Removal of magnesium sulphate by filtration and evaporation of the solvent yielded Schiff base of satisfactory purity. The portion of appropriate Schiff base (33 mmol) and diethyl phosphite (6.01 g, 67 mmol) was dissolved in toluene (80 ml) and refluxed for 6 h. Then, the solvent was evaporated and the resulting esters were hydrolysed by refluxing with 36% hydrochloric acid for 8 h and evaporated to dryness under reduced pressure. The obtained crude compounds were resolved in water, and decolourized with charcoal and evaporated again. Hydrochloride of compound L 1 was dissolved in ethanol and pure L 1 was precipitated by the addition of propylene oxide. In order to obtain the ligand L 1 in the soluble form in water, it was converted into hydrochloride or sodium salt. Compound L 2 was recrystallized from methanol/water system.

2.1.2. Synthesis of compound L 3

This compound was synthesized according to a modified procedure described by Boduszek [17] . 2-Pyridinecarboxaldehyde (1.28 g, 12 mmol) and diethylamine (0.35 g, 6 mmol) were dissolved in CH 2 Cl 2 (30 ml) containing dry MgSO 4 and stirred for 24 h at room temperature. Then, the solution was filtered off and evaporated in vacuo . Trimethyl phosphite (1.48 g, 5.6 mmol) was dissolved in freshly distilled CH 2 Cl 2 (25 ml) and to this solution bromotrimethylsilane (2.6 g, 17 mmol) was added dropwise for 15 min. The reaction mixture was stirred for 1 h and then cooled to 0 °C. Aldiimine (1.33 g, 5.6 mmol), which was synthesized separately earlier in methylene chloride, was added. The whole mixture was stirred for 24 h at room temperature and evaporated to dryness. The resulting oily product was dissolved in the mixture of methanol/water/ethyl ether and left to stir for 24 h at room temperature. After this time crystalline white product precipitated. The product was recrystallized from water/methanol system.

2.2. Potentiometric studies

Stability constants both for protons and for Cu(II), Ni(II) and Zn(II) complexes of all ligands were determined by pH-metric titration of 1.8–2.0 cm 3 samples at 25 °C over the pH range 2–10.5. Solution of metal ions was prepared from salts: Cu(NO 3 ) 2 × 3H 2 O (Aldrich), NiSO 4 × 7H 2 O (POCh) and ZnSO 4 × 7H 2 O (Chempur). Concentrations of the ligands were set at 1.5–2 × 10 −3 mol dm −3 and metal ion to ligand ratios at 1:1 to 1:6. Initial solutions were titrated with 0.1 M NaOH (Merck) delivered by a 0.25 ml micrometer syringe previously calibrated by weight titrations of potassium biphthalate (Aldrich) at pH 4.008. The purities and exact concentrations of the ligands were determined by the method of Gran [18] . The pH-metric titrations were performed using 0.1 M KNO 3 (Merck) background by means of MOLSPIN automatic titration system with a Russell CMAW 711 microcombined electrode calibrated daily for hydrogen-ion concentration using HNO 3 (Aldrich) [19] . The superquad computer program was used for calculations of stability constants [20] . Standard deviations quoted refer to random errors only. They are, however, a good indication of the importance of particular species being in equilibrium. The pM value expressed as pM = −log [M], calculated at pH 7.4, under typical biological concentrations of metal ions and ligands ( C L / C M = 10 and C L = 10 −5 M) allows to compare directly the binding abilities of different ligands taking into account their different proton concentration dependencies [21] .

2.3. Spectroscopic studies

The absorption spectra were recorded on a Beckman DU 650 spectrophotometer thermostated at 25 °C. Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectra were measured in ethylene glycol–water (1:2 v/v) solution at 77 K using Bruker ESP 300 spectrometer at the X-band frequency (9.3 GHz). The ligand concentration used in the spectroscopic samples was 9 × 10 −3 mol dm −3 , and metal concentration was 3 × 10 −3 mol dm −3 . The 31 P NMR spectra were recorded at 25 °C on a NMR Bruker DRX300 Avance spectrometer. The ligand concentration used in the NMR measurements was 1 × 10 −2 mol dm −3 , while metal concentration was 5 × 10 −3 mol dm −3 . The chemical shifts were referenced to the signal of 5% solution orthophosphoric acid used as external standard. All NMR measurements were made in D 2 O at adjusted pH ∗ (pH not corrected for the isotopic effect) values after pH equilibrium had been reached.

The formation constant of the Cu(II)– L 3 complex in solution was evaluated by an acid titration of a 1:1 ligand/metal solution followed spectrophotometrically till the disappearance of the band due to the complex. A Cary 50 spectrophotometer, equipped with fiber optic deep probe (optical path 1 cm) that allowed to make spectral measurements directly into the titration vessel, was used.

The stability constant of the Cu(II)H 2 L 3 complex was calculated from spectral measurements using the procedures described earlier [22] ; [23] ; [24] .

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Protonation constants

L 1 ligand exhibits five protonation constants ( Table 1 ; Table 2 ). Insertion of two phosphonic groups into the ligand structure results in the increase of the protonation constants of ethylenediamine nitrogens by about one unit [25] and the large difference in the protonation constants between these two nitrogens (around 2.5 log unit). This behavior is characteristic for ethylenediamine and its simple analogues [26] ; [27] . Two other protonation constants can be assigned to phosphonic functions.

The protonation constants data for L 1 have been previously reported [25] , p K values determined in this paper are similar ones ( Table 1 ). Set of protonation constants for both L 2 and L 3 , not described earlier, is similar for each other with slightly lower p K values for EN nitrogens ( Table 2 ), while the p K values found for phosphonic functions remain almost the same. The pyridyl nitrogen atoms are acidic and their p K values do not exceed 3.22.

3.2. Cu(II) complexes

The calculations based on the potentiometric data for Cu(II)– L 1 solutions indicated the formation of the following species: CuH 2 L, CuHL, CuL and CuL 2 ( Fig. 2 ). Giron-Forest et al. reported a slightly different coordination model which included MH 2 L, MHL and ML species for Cu(II), Ni(II) and Zn(II) ions [25] . According to the spectroscopic data with the d–d transitions centered at 692 nm and EPR parameters being A II = 165, g II = 2.27, the formation of CuH 2 L species at low pH most probably results from the coordination of two nitrogen donors of ethylenediamine moiety. Surprisingly, both phosphonic moieties are monoprotonated in this complex. The increase in pH results in the formation of the CuHL and CuL complexes. There is a minor variation in the d–d transition energy suggesting that oxygen donors share the coordination sphere, where phosphonates most likely are completing the equatorial coordination. The participation of phosphonates in the complex formation is supported by the fact that log K of the deprotonation reaction of CuH 2 L to form CuHL equals to 3.53 and it is distinctly lower than pK values for phosphonic groups deprotonations, which are 5.77 and 4.31 ( Table 2 ). The formation of the minor CuL 2 species in basic pH is by potentiometry, but this could not be supported by the spectroscopic data.

The substitutions of benzyl moiety by pyridyl ring in L 2 result in the formation of four complexes: CuH 3 L, CuH 2 L, CuHL, and CuL. The spectroscopic parameters with the d–d transitions at 697 nm and A II = 162, g II = 2.27 characterize two species CuH 3 L and CuH 2 L and they are very close to those found for CuH 2 L of L 1 ( Table 3 ). This fact clearly indicates that in L 2 pyridyl, nitrogens are not coordinated to Cu(II) ion due to steric reasons. CuHL and CuL complexes with d–d band at 685 nm, A II = 165 G and g II = 2.26 are also most likely complexes with the { 2 NH, 2PO 3 2 - } coordination mode.

L 3 is potentially the most powerful among the ligands studied here due to sterically favored positions of pyridine nitrogens to coordinate the metal ion. The UV–Vis spectra show d–d transition band at 624 nm, and EPR parameters are A II = 170, g II = 2.24, which indicate the involvement of pyridine nitrogens {2N EN , 2N pyr } in metal ion binding at whole pH range studied.

The intensity of the spectra assigned to this equimolar species progressively disappeared when the pH decreased from about 1 to −1, without any change of the spectral shape ( Fig. 3 ). These data were used to evaluate stability constant of the CuH 2 L complex using spectroscopic data resulting in log β = 33.43. This value indicates an exceptionally strong metal ion binding. In the acid titration below pH 1 the ionic strength of 0.1 M changes dramatically, and thus the obtained stability constant cannot be treated as that obtained at the fixed ionic strength. The first complex formed in strongly acidic solution is doubly protonated species, most likely with protons being at phosphonic groups. When pH rises the two-step deprotonation occurs, with formation of the final strong CuL complex (log β = 23.22), in which four nitrogen atoms are involved in coordination leaving the most probably free, non-coordinated phosphonic groups. Spectroscopic parameters are not changing during the titration ( Table 4 ).

The comparison of pM values given in Table 3 shows very high selectivity of L 3 in Cu(II) binding. The pM value 15.27 for L 3 –Cu(II) system is over six units lower than pM calculated for 4 N coordinated very potent cyclic chelator: cyclam, pM 21.4 [28] ; however, these values are few units higher than those calculated for Cu(II) complexes with linear N , N -bis-(2-amino-ethyl)-ethane-1,2-diamine [29] .

3.3. Ni(II) and Zn(II) complexes

Potentiometric titrations show that both metal ions, Ni(II) and Zn(II), form with L 1 three main species: MH 2 L, MHL and ML, with similar stability constants ( Table 2 , Fig. 2 ). Also the potentiometric data for L 2 clearly show, despite pyridyl units’ presence, that coordination of Ni(II) and Zn(II) ions according to the formation of MH 3 L, MH 2 L, MHL and ML species is of the same type as that for L 1 ( Table 2 , Fig. 2 ). The major ML complex predominates in all cases in the pH range of 5–10 exhibiting most likely the { 2 N EN , 2PO 3 2 - } coordination mode. The inspection of 1 H NMR and 31 P NMR ( Fig. 4 ; Fig. 5 ) spectra proves the involvement of phosphonate in the coordination of Zn(II) ions with L 1 and L 2 ligands and excludes the binding of pyridine moieties in the case of L 2 .

Introduction of pyridyl nitrogen in position 2 in ligand L 3 changes the coordination ability towards both Ni(II) and Zn(II). 1 H NMR spectra ( Fig. 4 ) indicate the involvement of pyridine nitrogen atoms in the metal ion coordination. The fact that pyridine nitrogen donors are involved in the metal ion binding together with ethylenediamine donor system is clearly seen in the stability constants of the ML species, which are higher by about four orders of magnitude for L 3 when compared to L 1 or L 2 .

The UV–Vis spectra of Ni(II) complexes at pH 7.0 ( Fig. 6 ) show the characteristic parameters for octahedral geometry in all studied ligands and a very similar coordination pattern for L 1 and L 2 . Slightly different spectra of Ni(II)– L 3 system when compared with L 1 and L 2 suggest different types of coordination environments for L 3 . The comparison of the stability constants of NiL for L 3 with that of N 1 , N 2 -bis((pyridin-2-yl)methyl)ethane-1,2-diamine [30] having 4 nitrogens and phosphonate (log β = 14.4 and 15.89) may suggest that in L 3 besides 4 nitrogens, two phosphonates could be bound to metal ion. Due to the same stoichiometry of the formed complexes and almost the same values of the stability constants found for Ni(II) and Zn(II) ions, the coordination pattern should be very similar for both metal ions in the case of L 3 . The comparison of 31 P NMR spectra in pH 7.0 made for free ligands L 1 , L 2 and L 3 and their Zn(II) complexes confirmed the coordination of phosphonic groups in Zn(II)– L 1 and Zn(II)– L 2 and can suggest the involvement of at least one phosphonic group in metal coordination of L 3 ( Fig. 6 ).

Ligands L 1 and L 2 possessing ethylenediamine moiety placed closely to phosphonic acid units bind the Cu(II), Ni(II) and Zn(II) ions using the same donor set { 2 N EN , 2PO 3 2 - } . The presence of 2-pyridyl moieties in L 3 leads to very effective ligand (pM 15.27) for Cu(II) ions, with tetradendate coordination mode and axial involvement of 2PO 3 2 - in Zn(II) and Ni(II) octahedral complexes. L 3 is able to involve a four-nitrogen donor system and additionally two phosphates and is unusually an effective ligand for both planar and octahedral complexes.
