Wavelength dependence of the photo-induced conversion of the Ni–C to the Ni–L redox state in the [NiFe] hydrogenase of Desulfovibrio vulgaris Miyazaki F

Caroline Fichtner , Maurice van Gastel and Wolfgang Lubitz *
Max Planck Institute for Bioinorganic Chemistry, Stiftstrasse 34-36, D-45470, Mülheim/Ruhr, Germany. E-mail: lubitz@mpi-muelheim.mpg.de ; Fax: +49-208-306-3955; Tel: +49-208-306-3614

Received 27th August 2003 , Accepted 24th October 2003

First published on the web 11th November 2003

The conversion process of the Ni–C to the Ni–L redox state of the [NiFe] center in Desulfovibrio vulgaris Miyazaki F hydrogenase is investigated by EPR spectroscopy following laser excitation at distinct wavelengths. The Ni–L state is characterized by a rhombic g tensor with principal values of 2.30, 2.12 and 2.05. The action spectrum associated with the Ni–C to Ni–L photoconversion spans the complete visible range and local maxima are present at 590 and 700 nm and a shoulder around 850 nm. In the UV/VIS spectrum of the Ni–C state, bands of low intensity can also be observed at 590 and 700 nm. It is proposed that these bands are associated with the [NiFe] center and correspond to electronic transitions that trigger the conversion process. On the basis of the small molar absorption coefficients (1100 M −1 cm −1 and 1400 M −1 cm −1 ), these transitions contain at most a small amount of ligand -to-metal charge transfer character. A light-induced back conversion, starting from the Ni–L redox state, has not been observed. Based on comparison with data available from X-ray crystallography and from ENDOR and pulse EPR spectroscopy of the Ni–C and Ni–L states, we propose that the Ni–C to Ni–L conversion process is associated with a dissociation of the Ni–H bond and translocation of the released proton to the sulfur of one of the terminal cysteines.

Introduction

Hydrogenases (Enzyme Class 1.12.2.1) catalyse the reversible two-electron oxidation of molecular hydrogen (H 2 ) and play a key role in the energy generation process of a large variety of microorganisms, in particular in sulfate reducing bacteria. They can be divided into three classes according to their metal content (for a review see ref. 1 ). The enzymes without metal atoms directly participating in the catalytic cycle are known as “metal free” hydrogenases, those containing only iron are the [Fe] hydrogenases. Most commonly found are the [NiFe] hydrogenases 2 which contain both nickel and iron. This class includes the subclass of the nickel–iron–selenium [NiFeSe] hydrogenases.

The [NiFe] hydrogenase of Desulfovibrio (D.) vulgaris Miyazaki F, a sulfate reducing δ-proteobacterium, is a heterodimer consisting of two subunits. Its crystal structure has been resolved with 1.4 Å resolution. 3 The active site of the enzyme, a [NiFe] heterobimetallic center ( Fig. 1 ) is located in the large subunit with a molecular mass of 61 kDa. The nickel is coordinated by four sulfur atoms from four cysteine residues ( cysteine 81, 84, 546, 549), two of them (84 and 549) form a bridge between Ni and Fe. As a special feature the Fe ion carries three inorganic diatomic ligands . 4–7 Initially, the X-ray data of D. vulgaris Miyazaki F indicated that the inorganic ligands at the iron are one SO and two COs or CNs 3 , but recent X-ray studies 8 and FTIR data (Fichtner, unpublished results) showed that the ligands are two CNs and one CO, as also found for other [NiFe] hydrogenases by FTIR spectroscopy . 9,10 In the bridge between Ni and Fe an additional small inorganic ligand has been detected in the oxidized states. 3,4 It has been identified as a hydride in the reduced Ni–C state (see Fig. 1 ). 11,12


Fig. 1 Structure of the active site of D. vulgaris Miyazaki F [NiFe] hydrogenase. The active site is located in the large subunit of the heterodimeric protein and is deeply buried in the protein . The small bridging ligand between Ni and Fe has recently been assigned to a hydride ion in the Ni–C state. 11–13 The exact positions of the CO and CN ligands are not assigned by X-ray crystallography or spectroscopy . 13

The small subunit with a molecular mass of 29 kDa contains two [4Fe4S] clusters and one [3Fe4S] cluster. One [4Fe4S] cluster is situated close to the interface of the two subunits near the active site and is deeply buried in the protein (proximal cluster), whereas the other resides at the periplasmic site of the protein (distal cluster). The [3Fe4S] cluster is located in-between the proximal and the distal cluster. 3–5

The hydrogenase passes through different redox states during its catalytic cycle. 2 In this process the nature of the bridging ligand between the nickel and the iron atom, which occupies the fifth coordination site of the nickel atom changes. After aerobic protein isolation the hydrogenase exhibits two characteristic EPR signals that belong to the two oxidized states Ni–A ( g = 2.32, 2.24, 2.01) and Ni–B ( g = 2.33, 2.16. 2.01). Another EPR signal appears upon two-electron reduction called the Ni–C signal ( g = 2.20, 2.14, 2.01). The Ni–C state is believed to directly participate in the catalytic cycle, 14 i.e. to bind and cleave the substrate hydrogen.

Experimental and theoretical evidence exists that the bridging ligand in Ni–C is a hydrogen species. 11 Theoretical calculations on models of the Ni–C state, 11,14–16 and 1 H- ENDOR experiments 12,13,17 suggested the presence of a formal hydride (H − ) in the bridge. Indirect evidence for this proposal is obtained by the analysis of the non-physiological, light-induced Ni–L states. Upon illumination with white light under anaerobic conditions at a temperature of <100 K the formation of a mixture of two or even three different Ni–L species is observed in hydrogenases of different species. 18–20 The generation of the Ni–L states is accompanied by a change of the EPR signal. The most prominent state, here called Ni–L, is characterized by g values of 2.30, 2.12 and 2.05. The additional, less intense signal is thought to belong to a transient state produced during the Ni–C to Ni–L transition † . 21 At temperatures below 100 K the Ni–L state is stable but warming to higher temperatures re-establishes the Ni–C state. The photoreaction is nearly 6 times slower in D 2 O than in H 2 O as has been shown for Allochromatium (A.) vinosum hydrogenase. 18 Additionally, the strong proton coupling observed for Ni–C in 1 H ENDOR experiments on the hydrogenase of Thiocapsa roseopersicina , 17 Ralstonia eutropha , 12 D. gigas 22 and D. vulgaris Miyazaki F, 13 assigned to the hydride bridge, is no longer present in Ni–L. This indicates a movement of the hydrogen involved in the photoreaction leaving the bridging position unoccupied in Ni–L. 11,12,17 This assumption is supported by relativistic DFT calculations showing that the EPR parameters can be best fitted by assuming a formal Ni( III ) oxidation state and a hydride (H − ) bound between nickel and iron in the Ni–C state and a formal Ni( I ) oxidation state and a vacant bridging position in the Ni–L state. 11

Although the Ni–L state has been investigated in detail by EPR spectroscopy , 17–21,23 knowledge regarding how this state is created is limited to the observation that white light is necessary for the photoconversion to occur. In this contribution we examine this process as a function of the excitation wavelength. We found that the Ni–L state photoproduction is associated with a broad absorption that spans the range from 350 to 900 nm. In the spectrum maxima can be observed at 590 and 700 nm and a shoulder at around 850 nm. This prompted us to re-examine the UV/VIS spectrum of the reduced enzyme, which also shows maxima at 590 and 700 nm. Since the conversion process is not associated with the strong absorptions of the [FeS] clusters, which lie below 400 nm, an assignment to electronic transitions of the [NiFe] center is proposed. The molar absorption coefficients of the bands have been estimated to lie around 1250 M −1 cm −1 , indicating that the electronic transitions involve ligand based orbitals and that only small charge-transfer character is present.

Experimental

Sample preparation

D. vulgaris Miyazaki F cells were grown in 10 L cultures. The cell mass of 40 L was used for one enzyme purification , which was performed as previously described. 24 A total of 80 μl of 1.5 mM hydrogenase were filled in an EPR tube and activated in a 100% H 2 atmosphere for 6 h at 37 °C by gentle stirring. After this treatment the enzyme was in the Ni–C state and was then rapidly frozen. This procedure was also followed when performing UV/VIS spectra .

EPR measurements and data analysis

The measurements were carried out with a Bruker ESP380E X-band spectrometer using a dielectric ring (ERUI18SPT) resonator and an Oxford CF-935 flow cryostat. All spectra were recorded at 40 K. This temperature was chosen because at lower temperatures the EPR signals of Ni–C and Ni–L split due to spin-coupling with the proximal [4Fe4S] cluster. 25 The choice of T = 40 K simplifies the analysis and has the advantage that the transient Ni–L state only gives a minor contribution to the EPR spectra upon illumination. At higher temperature the intensity of the signal decreases. Prior to each measurement, the sample was dark-adapted by incubation in cold ethanol (200 K) in the dark for 10 min in order to minimize the presence of the Ni–L state. Still in the dark it was mounted into the cavity. The sample was illuminated at a fixed wavelength from an OPO laser (GWU model VISIR 120) which was pumped by a Nd ∶ YAG laser ( Spectra Physics model GCR-130). For some experiments the fundamental wavelength (1064 nm) or the energy-tripled wavelength (355 nm) of the pump laser were used. The laser repetition rate was 10 Hz. The total energy of the excitation light was measured directly at the window of the flow cryostat with a homebuilt powermeter calibrated against a Spectrolas powermeter, which has an output linear with wavelength.

To study the Ni–C to Ni–L conversion we monitored the decay of the g x component of the Ni–C spectrum. We chose this signal because it is well separated from the other signals in the first derivative EPR spectrum ( Fig. 2 ). Two different detection schemes were used. In the first method we illuminated the dark-adapted hydrogenase sample with laser light of various wavelengths at excitation energies which were chosen such that the number of photons corresponded to that present with excitation light at 630 nm and 2.6 mJ pulse −1 (pulse length: 6 ns). UV/VIS measurements

UV/VIS measurements were carried out on an ATI Unicam UV2/300 UV/VIS spectrophotometer. The enzyme solution (30 μM) was filled in a 1 ml quartz cuvette (optical pathlength 1 cm) and measured against 25 mM Tris /HCl buffer , pH 7.4.

Results

Reduction of the as-isolated [NiFe] hydrogenase with 100% H 2 gas leads to the formation of the reduced Ni–C state (characterized by g values of g x = 2.20, g y = 2.14, g z = 2.01) 2 whose EPR spectrum is shown in Fig. 2a . After illumination for about 30 min at 77 K outside the cavity of the EPR spectrometer with white light from a projection lamp (250 W with heat filter), the Ni–C signal is completely converted to the Ni–L signal according to the EPR spectrum ( g x = 2.30, g y = 2.12, g z = 2.05), 20 as shown in Fig. 2b . After 10 min of illumination of the enzyme in the Ni–C state in the cavity ( Fig. 2c ) a minor component resulting from a transient light-induced state is observed ( g x = 2.26, g y = 2.11, g z = 2.05). All signals in the EPR spectra have g values similar to those observed earlier for other hydrogenases ( A. vinosum , 18 D. gigas , 19,20 D. fructosovorans and Desulfomicrobium baculatum 19 ). At the measuring temperature of 40 K the signal of the reduced [3Fe4S] cluster ( g = 2.01) is also present in the spectra and overlaps with the g z component of the Ni–C signal.

The time course for the formation of the Ni–L states at different excitation wavelengths was monitored by recording EPR spectra every 5 min during the illumination process (described as method two in Experimental). A selection of spectra recorded at 40 K with an excitation wavelength of 630 nm and excitation energy of 5.6 mJ pulse −1 is shown in Fig. 3 . Similar to the excitation with white light the decrease of the Ni–C signal is proportional to the increase of the Ni–L signal. At 40 K, the transient light-induced state appears as a minority species in all spectra . The intensity of its g x signal increases during the first 15 min of illumination, but decreases again after longer illumination. This observation is compatible with earlier investigations, in which this state has been shown to be a transient state favored at low temperatures and short illumination time. 19,21 The signal of the Ni–L state increases monotonically with time.

Because of the presence of multiple Ni–L states, a quantitative analysis of the total conversion at different excitation wavelengths can be best performed using the decay of the Ni–C EPR signal. In order to accurately determine the conversion, a series of EPR measurements was performed in which the g x signal ( g = 2.20, B 0 = 317.8 mT) was monitored (see Experimental). In the first derivative EPR spectrum this signal does not overlap with signals belonging to other redox states of the [NiFe] center or of the [FeS] clusters. Two representative decay curves with excitation wavelengths of 530 nm and 870 nm are shown in Fig. 4 after normalization to the EPR intensity at zero illumination time, i.e. , pure Ni–C. From the decay curves it is obvious that the conversion process is slower at 870 nm than at 530 nm. Surprisingly, the decay curve at 870 nm can be fitted with a mono-exponential decay function, whereas at 530 nm a tri-exponential function is needed. The Ni–C to Ni–L conversion takes place with three time constants for all observed wavelengths below 780 nm (see Table 1 ). Above 780 nm the process is best described by one time constant that increases from ca . 110 min at 780 nm to ca . 162 min at 870 nm, i.e. , the lower the excitation energy the more difficult it becomes to generate Ni–L.

From extrapolation of the decay curves to infinite illumination time, the total conversion of Ni–C to Ni–L was determined. The converted amount of Ni–C as measured in the EPR spectrum at g x = 2.20 vs. the excitation wavelength λ exc , i.e. , the resulting action spectrum, is presented in Fig. 5 . The spectrum is broad and covers almost the complete visible range. Using light with 550 nm < λ < 700 nm a maximum conversion rate of ca . 75% was achieved. In the near infrared region ( λ > 900 nm) the conversion decreases and for λ > 1050 nm no conversion process was observed at all. Using quanta of higher energy (550 nm < λ < 350 nm) the conversion decreases from 65 to 35%. Local maxima at about 590, 700 and 850 nm can be identified ( Fig. 5 ). Although care was taken to ensure complete illumination of the sample, at none of the wavelengths and light intensities a conversion of 100% was obtained. This can result from spectrophotoselection and the fact that polarized laserlight is used, which means that molecules which are oriented such that their transition dipole moment is parallel to the k vector of the incident photons (and perpendicular to the plane of the polarization vectors) are not converted. UV/VIS spectra were recorded for the as-isolated (oxidized) and Ni–C (reduced) states of the enzyme ( Fig. 6 ). Due to the fact that the enzyme contains three [FeS] clusters in addition to the [NiFe] center, the UV/VIS spectrum is dominated by the absorption of the [FeS] clusters. 8 The principal absorption bands for [3Fe4S] and [4Fe4S] clusters of several iron–sulfur proteins in their oxidized and reduced states have been reported ( Table 2 ). 26 In the spectrum of the oxidized hydrogenase ( Fig. 6 , black curve) the broad absorption band around 420 nm results from contributions of the oxidized [4Fe4S] (390 nm) and the [3Fe4S] clusters (415 nm and 455 nm). Reduction of the enzyme by an H 2 atmosphere leads to a reduction of all [FeS] clusters and to a decrease of the absorption at 420 nm.

Strikingly, two features are present in the absorption spectrum of the reduced enzyme ( Fig. 6 , grey curve), which are absent in the spectrum of the oxidized form. These are the bands at 590 nm and 700 nm, which have been observed before 8 but not further analyzed. In order to understand the origin of these bands, an attempt was made to estimate their molar absorption coefficients by subtracting an exponential background function from the UV/VIS spectrum of the reduced enzyme. The resulting difference spectrum is included in Fig. 5 and the molar absorption coefficients of these bands, calculated according to Lambert–Beer's law, were estimated to be ca . 1100 M −1 cm −1 and 1400 M −1 cm −1 , respectively (error ca . 300 M −1 cm −1 ).

An attempt was made to observe the conversion back from Ni–L to Ni–C by starting from a 100% Ni–L sample (created by illumination outside the cavity with white light from a projection lamp) and illuminating with the laser at different wavelengths at 40 K. At none of the wavelengths between 355 nm and 900 nm, a recovery of the Ni–C signal was observed.

Discussion

The photoconversion of the Ni–C to the Ni–L state is associated with a broad action spectrum in the visible range. This indicates that electronically excited states have to be involved. In the discussion we first address the conversion as a function of wavelength ( Fig. 5 ) and the relation to the structure observed in the UV/VIS spectrum of the Ni–C state. Subsequently, an assignment of the bands observed in both spectra is proposed based on their molar absorption coefficients and a comparison with absorption spectra of nickel model complexes and [FeS] clusters. Finally, implications for the reaction mechanism of the enzyme are discussed.

EPR and UV/VIS spectra

The decay of the Ni–C signal after illumination with light of distinct wavelengths can be described by an empirical fit involving a three-exponential decay for wavelengths between 350 nm and 780 nm. Only one time constant is necessary when the excitation wavelength is longer than 780 nm. At all wavelengths, the decay is dominated by the process assosiated with the largest time constant ( t 3 , see Table 1 ). The other two time constants are required to correctly simulate the first part of the decay curve. The observation that t 3 hardly varies from 350 to 830 nm implies that the Ni–C to Ni–L conversion process proceeds via the same mechanism in this spectral region. Beyond 830 nm, t 3 increases and the increase correlates with the decrease in conversion to Ni–L. At excitation wavelengths longer than 1050 nm, the energy is no longer sufficient to reach an electronically excited state associated with the conversion of Ni–C to Ni–L.

We then make the simplification that the population of the Ni–C* state decays uni-directionally towards Ni–L ( k 2 = 0, k 3 = 1). At shorter wavelengths either more than one excited state is involved or an equilibrium may exist between the species ( k 2 ≠ 0, k 3 ≠ 1), both of which would lead to a multi-exponential decay of the population in the Ni–C state. This may be the reason for the multi-exponential function necessary to fit the decay curve at wavelength shorter than 830 nm. A shortcoming of the model is that it does not include spectrophotoselection and all population eventually ends up in the Ni–L state. As opposed to excitation with a laser spectrophotoselection does not occur when the sample is excited with a projection lamp and a complete conversion of Ni–C to Ni–L is obtained.

A comparison of the action spectrum of the conversion and the bands in the UV/VIS spectrum reveals striking similarities. Both spectra exhibit absorption maxima at 590 nm and 700 nm ( cf. Fig. 5 ). Interestingly, the conversion decreases when going from yellow to blue excitation light, where the absorption of [FeS] clusters reaches its maximum. Although it is clear from this observation that the conversion process is not directly coupled to these large absorptions ( ε total ∼ 47 000 M −1 cm −1 ), the conversion in the blue region ( Fig. 5 ) might be underestimated, since the [FeS] clusters absorb many of the photons . Nevertheless, with 5.6 mJ pulse −1 of excitation energy at 355 nm, it was possible to saturate the conversion process to a similar extent as it was the case at 630 nm and 5.6 mJ pulse −1 . Under these conditions the respective time constants of the decay curves did not change. This shows that the conversion extrapolated to infinite illumination time should be influenced only to a minor extent by the absorption of the [FeS] clusters. We therefore conclude that the bands at 590 and 700 nm observed in both the UV/VIS spectrum and the action spectrum and also the low-intensity band at 850 nm observed in the latter spectrum involve electronic transitions that trigger the Ni–C to Ni–L conversion process.

Nature of the transitions

The Ni–C to Ni–L conversion process affects predominantly the electronic and geometric structure of the [NiFe] center. Recently, it was shown experimentally that Ni–C carries a hydride as bridge between Ni and Fe and that this hydride is lost in the Ni–L state. 11–13 For Ni–C it is assumed that the Ni has a 3d z 2 electronic ground state whereas Ni–L has a larger 3d x 2 − y 2 contribution. 27 It is tempting to assign the bands at 590 and 700 nm and possibly the broad absorption around 850 nm to electronic transitions of the [NiFe] center. It is known, however, that the [NiFe] center has a magnetic spin–spin interaction with the proximal reduced [4Fe4S] cluster with a significant contribution from exchange interaction. 21,25 An energy transfer mechanism from an excited proximal [4Fe4S] cluster to the [NiFe] center can therefore not be excluded. In order to see whether [FeS] clusters exhibit structure in the visible absorption spectrum between 500 and 900 nm, a literature search of UV/VIS spectra for [FeS] clusters was performed. The overall spectroscopic characteristics of [FeS] clusters differ depending on their redox state 26 ( Table 2 ). It is important to realize that in the Ni–C and the Ni–L state the [4Fe4S] clusters are in their reduced S = ½ state. Although UV/VIS spectra for oxidized [4Fe4S] clusters can be rich in bands over the visible range, those for reduced [4Fe4S] clusters are structureless and show a declined absorbance in the region between 500 and 900 nm. 28–30 Therefore, we conclude that the bands present above 500 nm in both the UV/VIS spectrum of the reduced enzyme and the action spectrum of the conversion originate from the [NiFe] center.

In 1964, Gray and coworkers 31 characterized the square-planar nickel–maleonitriledithiolate complexes Ni(mnt) 2 − and Ni(mnt) 2 2− in which the nickel is coordinated by four sulfur atoms and has an electronic ground state with predominant 3d yz character. Here, ligand -to-metal charge transfer transitions ( ε ∼ 2500–8000 M −1 cm −1 ) and parity forbidden transitions ( ε ∼ 500 M −1 cm −1 ) have been observed in the 250 − 900 nm spectral region. The exact nature of the latter is still disputed, but may correspond to a transition involving ligand orbitals. 32 Although Ni(mnt) 2 − has a different electronic ground state as Ni–C (3d yz vs. 3d z 2 27 ) and does not contain iron, we use the ε values of the bands for Ni(mnt) 2 − as a confirmation that Ni-related transitions in the UV/VIS spectrum between 250 and 900 nm may exist. From the low values of the molar absorption coefficients observed for the bands at 590 and 700 nm (1100 M −1 cm −1 and 1400 M −1 cm −1 ) in the UV/VIS spectrum of the reduced enzyme it is clear that the corresponding transitions contain only a small amount of ligand -to-metal charge transfer character.

Implications for the reaction mechanism

The Ni–C state can be recovered from Ni–L at temperatures above 120 K. 19 This indicates that the former state is lower in energy than the Ni–L state. Due to the fact that the back-reaction from Ni–L to Ni–C cannot be achieved by illumination, either the relaxation from the electronically excited states is uni-directional towards Ni–L or the Ni–L state does not absorb the excitation light.

An X-ray crystallographic investigation of the binding of CO to the [NiFe] center in D. vulgaris Miyazaki F hydrogenase has been recently reported. 8 Similar to the case of the conversion of Ni–C to Ni–L, the exogenous CO in the CO-bound form can be liberated by illumination with strong white light and a typical Ni–L EPR signal is observed. In this investigation, in which CO-flushed reduced crystals were analyzed before and after illumination, changes in electron density at the sulfur of cysteine 546 were observed when comparing the CO-bound and CO-liberated forms of the crystal. In addition, a high temperature factor was found for the sulfur of this terminal cysteine 546 and it was suggested that this sulfur is protonated in the CO-liberated form. 8 The presence of such a thiol group has also been suggested earlier. 14,33,34 Analogously, it is conceivable that the bridging hydride of the Ni–C state binds as an H + to the sulfur of cysteine 546 in the Ni–L state, leaving the Ni in a formal Ni( I ) state. This would also explain the decrease observed for the respective 1 H hyperfine coupling upon Ni–C to Ni–L conversion. 12,13,17

Because of the low energy barrier between Ni–C and Ni–L the hydrogen species in the bridge appears to be relatively mobile at room temperature. It is conceivable that the binding of CO to Ni–C traps it in a position where it binds to the sulfur of cysteine 546. This would explain why after liberation of CO with strong white light at low temperature, the typical Ni–L EPR signal is observed. To validate this hypothesis it would be interesting to see if an S–H (or S–D) vibrational frequency can be observed by FTIR spectroscopy in Ni–C/Ni–L difference spectra measured at low temperature in H 2 O and D 2 O.
